Georgia packs the Greater Caucasus, vine-covered valleys and a Black Sea coast into a country the size of Ireland, and its climbing spans the same improbable range. At the top stand the glaciated giants — Kazbek, Tetnuldi, the twin-towered menace of Ushba — that have drawn alpinists since the nineteenth century. Below them, a young sport climbing scene has bolted superb limestone around Chiatura, Katskhi and Borjomi, and Tbilisi's gyms are filling with a new generation. Add legendary hospitality, supras that derail rest days and prices that stretch any budget, and Georgia is one of the most rewarding climbing countries between Europe and Asia. The places below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=ge).

## Chiatura and Katskhi: The Limestone Heart

The mining town of Chiatura, strung along the Kvirila gorge in Imereti, is the unlikely capital of Georgian sport climbing. Soviet-era cable cars once hauled miners up the same cliffs where bolted sectors now climb tier after tier of solid limestone. A short drive away, the climbing area of Katskhi offers the country's densest concentration of routes, with sectors from canyon walls to caves and an honest spread of grades. Above it all rises the Katskhi Pillar, a 40-metre natural tower topped by a monk's hermitage — not open for climbing, but an unforgettable landmark for anyone clipping bolts below.

## Sveri, the Via Ferrata and Imereti's New Wave

Near Chiatura, the village crags of Sveri have grown into a multi-sector area with everything from beginner-friendly slabs to steep caves, plus a bolted via ferrata that traverses the gorge — a rarity in the Caucasus. Sector names here are playfully modern, and development is continuous, driven by local equippers and the Georgian climbing federation. Together, Chiatura, Katskhi and Sveri form a compact limestone triangle where a week disappears quickly, with guesthouses in the villages and wine from the family qvevri at dinner.

## Borjomi and the Lesser Caucasus

Southwest of Tbilisi, the spa town of Borjomi — famous across the former Soviet world for its mineral water — has its own bolted crags in the wooded gorge of the Lesser Caucasus. Sectors with names like Drakula and Mzetamze climb volcanic and sedimentary rock above the Mtkvari valley, and the Borjomi-Kharagauli National Park behind offers some of the country's best forest hiking. The crags are smaller than Imereti's, but the combination of climbing, mineral baths and park trails makes Borjomi a fine shoulder-season stop.

## Kazbegi: Arsha and the Five-Thousander

North along the Georgian Military Highway, the town of Stepantsminda (Kazbegi) sits beneath Mount Kazbek, a 5,054-metre glaciated volcano that is the most accessible big summit in the country. The standard route via the Gergeti Glacier and the Betlemi Hut is a serious but non-technical glacier climb that draws aspirant mountaineers from around the world. For rock climbers, the bolted sector at Arsha village offers sport routes with absurd backdrops — the Gergeti Trinity Church on its hill, Kazbek's ice dome behind. The valley has become Georgia's adventure hub, with guides, gear shops and acclimatization hikes on every side.

## Svaneti and the Great Caucasus Walls

In the far northwest, the towered villages of Svaneti sit beneath the most dramatic mountains in Georgia. Ushba, the double-summited "Matterhorn of the Caucasus" at 4,710 metres, remains one of world alpinism's great prizes, serious by any route, while Tetnuldi and Shkhara — the country's highest point at over 5,100 metres on the Bezengi wall — anchor a skyline of expedition-grade objectives. Mestia, with its medieval defensive towers and small airport, is the base. This is committing, self-sufficient terrain where experience and local knowledge matter as much as fitness.

## Tbilisi: Crags and Gyms in the Capital

Tbilisi supports a lively everyday scene. The crag in the city's Botanical Garden gives bolted routes minutes from the sulphur baths of the old town, and the gym scene — led by venues like the Vake climbing hall — is expanding fast. The city makes a natural base for weekend loops: Chiatura is three hours west, Borjomi two hours southwest, Kazbegi three hours north. Few capitals put glaciers, limestone and a UNESCO-listed old town within the same day's drive.

## Seasons and Practicalities

Sport climbing in Imereti and around Tbilisi runs from spring through late autumn, with April-June and September-October ideal; summer is hot in the lowlands but perfect in the mountains. The Kazbek and Svaneti alpine season is July to mid-September. Logistics are easy by regional standards: marshrutka minibuses reach most towns, guesthouses are cheap and generous, and no permits are needed for the main peaks, though border-zone rules apply near some Svaneti objectives. Carry cash in the villages and never refuse a toast lightly.
