Egypt is not the first country most climbers picture, yet behind the temples and the Red Sea resorts lies a serious adventure-climbing landscape. The high desert of the southern Sinai is built of coarse, grippy granite, carved by wind and millennia into towers, slabs and canyons around the monastery of Saint Catherine and the holy summit of Mount Sinai. Here climbing is woven into the world of the Jebeliya Bedouin, whose guides know every wadi and water source. It is rough, remote, desert rock, climbed under enormous skies. The areas below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=eg).

## The Granite of South Sinai

The southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula is a sea of granite mountains rising above three thousand metres, with Gebel Katherina (2629 m) the highest point in Egypt and Mount Sinai (Gebel Musa) close beside it. The rock is rough, weathered granite — superb friction, with cracks, slabs and steep faces. Climbing here ranges from scrambling on ancient pilgrim paths to bolted sport routes and longer adventure lines. The coarse stone is kind to the hands and feet but unforgiving of complacency in such a remote place, where the desert demands respect and good planning.

## Saint Catherine and the High Desert

The town of Saint Catherine, set in a high mountain basin at around sixteen hundred metres, is the natural base. It sits beside the ancient monastery and below the granite peaks, with cooler air than the coast and a network of wadis leading into the range. From here climbers and trekkers reach areas with established routes — slabs and walls bearing names given by the small community of developers, such as Camel Canyon, Choppy Slab, Choppy Chop, Horny Slab, the Waterfall, and lines like Khaled's Move and the classic Alif (6a+). The mix of sport climbing, scrambling and desert trekking defines the experience.

## Bedouin Guides and the Jebeliya

Climbing in the Sinai is inseparable from the Jebeliya Bedouin, the tribe who have lived among these mountains for centuries and who guide nearly all travel into the high desert. Their knowledge of routes, weather, water and shelter is the backbone of any safe trip, and joining a Bedouin-guided expedition is both the practical and the respectful way to climb here. Guides arrange camels for gear, camps among the rocks, and the meals and tea that turn a hard day into a memorable one. Supporting them keeps the local economy and the climbing tradition alive.

## Trekking, Canyons and Summits

Beyond pure rock climbing, the Sinai is a trekking and canyon country. The Sinai Trail, a long-distance route across the peninsula, links Bedouin communities and dramatic terrain. Hidden gardens, narrow gorges and granite amphitheatres reward those who walk deeper into the range, and the ascent of Mount Sinai for sunrise remains an iconic objective. The combination of scrambling, walking and short technical pitches makes this an ideal landscape for adventurous climbers who enjoy moving through wild country as much as pulling on holds.

## The Red Sea and Beyond

Most visitors arrive through the Red Sea gateways of Sharm El Sheikh or Dahab, dive towns that double as relaxed bases before heading inland. The contrast is striking: warm coral seas one day, cold granite nights in the high desert the next. While the coast is for snorkelling and rest, the mountains a few hours inland are the real climbing draw, and many travellers combine both into a single trip that pairs the Red Sea with the granite peaks of the interior.

## Season, Heat and Safety

Timing is everything in the desert. Spring and autumn — roughly March to May and September to November — give the best conditions, with warm days and cold, clear nights at altitude. Summer brings fierce heat, while winter nights in the high desert can fall below freezing with occasional snow on the peaks. Water is scarce and distances are large, so a local Bedouin guide, ample supplies and conservative planning are essential. Check current travel advisories before visiting the Sinai interior, and treat the desert and its people with care and respect.
