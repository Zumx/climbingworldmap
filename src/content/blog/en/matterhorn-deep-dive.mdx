Rising in a near-perfect pyramid above the village of Zermatt, the Matterhorn reaches 4,478 metres on the border between Switzerland and Italy. Its four steep faces and sharp ridges make it perhaps the most recognisable mountain on earth, a shape echoed in everything from chocolate wrappers to corporate logos. The peak's first ascent in 1865, and the tragedy that followed on the descent, sealed its place in mountaineering legend. Today thousands attempt it each summer, most by the Hörnli ridge, the line of that first climb. Find it on the [map](/map?country=ch).

## The Mountain and Zermatt

The Matterhorn — Monte Cervino to Italians, le Cervin to the French-speaking Swiss — stands above the car-free resort of Zermatt in the canton of Valais. The village, reached only by train, is a thoroughly alpine place, its streets lined with hotels, guides' offices, and gear shops, all in the shadow of the great peak. The mountain has four faces, the north, south, east, and west, divided by four ridges: the Hörnli, the Furggen, the Lion, and the Zmutt. Each ridge has its own history and character, but the Hörnli is the trade route to the top.

## The Hörnli Ridge

The Hörnli ridge, the northeast ridge, is the normal route and the line of the 1865 first ascent led by Edward Whymper. It begins at the Hörnli hut at around 3,260 metres and climbs roughly 1,200 metres of mixed rock and snow to the summit. The route is not extreme in technical grade, but it is long, exposed, and relentless, with fixed ropes on the harder sections and serious rockfall potential. Speed is everything: parties typically leave the hut before dawn to summit and descend before the afternoon weather and crowds make the ridge dangerous.

## The Other Ridges and Faces

For accomplished alpinists, the Matterhorn offers far more than the Hörnli. The Italian, or Lion, ridge rises from the Breuil-Cervinia side and is a fine climb in its own right, while the Zmutt ridge is a longer, wilder, more serious line considered one of the great ridge climbs of the Alps. The Furggen ridge is harder and less travelled. The four great faces, including the formidable north face first climbed in 1931, are the preserve of elite climbers and rank among the most storied walls in alpine history.

## The 1865 First Ascent and Its Legacy

On 14 July 1865, Edward Whymper and a party of seven reached the summit by the Hörnli ridge, narrowly beating an Italian team on the Lion ridge. On the descent, four of the party fell to their deaths when a rope broke, a disaster that shocked Victorian Europe and made the Matterhorn a symbol of both triumph and tragedy. The story shaped the public image of mountaineering for generations and remains central to how the peak is understood. Memorials in Zermatt and its mountaineering museum keep this history vivid.

## Hazards, Guides, and Fitness

The Matterhorn is a serious mountain that claims lives most years. The dangers are rockfall, sudden storms, exposure, and the simple difficulty of moving fast over long, exposed terrain at altitude. Most climbers hire a Zermatt guide, both for safety and because guided parties move faster and are prioritised on the route. A successful ascent demands real fitness, solid scrambling skill, comfort with exposure, and good acclimatisation. Many aspirants train on lower 4,000-metre peaks in the Zermatt area before attempting the Matterhorn itself.

## Season, Huts, and Conditions

The climbing season runs broadly from mid-July to mid-September, when the Hörnli ridge is most likely to be free of fresh snow and the hut is fully staffed. The Hörnli hut, perched at the foot of the ridge, books out far in advance and is the launching point for almost all attempts. Conditions are everything: fresh snow, ice, or high winds can shut the route down entirely, and guides will cancel rather than risk a verglas-covered ridge. Patience and flexibility in dates greatly improve the chance of a summit.
