Taiwan surprises almost everyone who climbs there. This compact, mountainous island off the coast of mainland Asia holds more than two hundred peaks above three thousand metres, and along its northeast coast the sea has carved a sandstone amphitheatre that ranks among Asia's best sport-climbing venues. From the salt spray of Long Dong to the cold ridgelines of the Holy Ridge, climbing in Taiwan ranges from accessible single-pitch cragging an hour from Taipei to demanding alpine rock high in the Central Mountain Range. Add a dense indoor scene in the capital and a warm, welcoming local community, and the island rewards far more than its size suggests.

## Long Dong: the sea-cliff heart

Long Dong, meaning Dragon's Cave, is Taiwan's flagship climbing area and the reason many travellers come. Set on the rugged northeast coast roughly an hour and a half from Taipei, it offers hundreds of routes on superb quartzite sandstone rising straight from the Pacific. The rock is featured, solid and steep, with everything from gentle slabs to overhanging test-pieces, and the sea forms a constant backdrop, sometimes calm and turquoise, sometimes hammering the lower walls in winter swell.

## The sectors of Long Dong

Long Dong is divided into named sectors strung along the coastline, including the popular First Cave and the Backdoor and Music Hall areas, each with its own character and aspect. Some sectors take morning sun, others stay shaded into the afternoon, and a few demand attention to the tide and the swell before committing to the lower pitches. Both sport and traditional climbing thrive here; the cracks and corners accept gear well, making Long Dong one of the few Asian venues where trad climbers find a genuine home alongside the bolt-clippers.

## Taipei and the indoor scene

Taipei and its surrounding cities hold a thriving gym culture that feeds the outdoor scene. Bouldering and lead walls have multiplied across the metropolitan area, and chains such as the well-known T-UP gyms in districts like Zhonghe give locals and visitors year-round training when the coast is too wet or too hot. Many climbers warm up indoors midweek and head for Long Dong or the smaller crags at weekends, and the gyms are the easiest place to meet partners and gather current conditions before a trip to the rock.

## High mountains: Xueshan and Nanhu

Beyond the cragging lies Taiwan's serious mountain country. Xueshan, the island's second-highest peak at 3,886 metres, and the dramatic Nanhu massif offer alpine rock and big-mountain walls amid genuinely wild terrain. The northeast face of Nanhu and the buttresses around the high peaks see committing climbs in a remote, high-altitude environment where weather changes fast. These mountains demand permits, fitness and respect, but they reward with a side of Taiwan few visitors ever glimpse: cold dawns above the clouds on the spine of the Central Range.

## The Holy Ridge and alpine traverses

The Holy Ridge, the knife-edge link between Xueshan and Dabajianshan, is the island's classic high-mountain traverse and a rite of passage for Taiwanese mountaineers. Dabajianshan itself, the striking Big Sharp Pointed Mountain, is one of the most photographed summits in Taiwan. These multi-day outings blend scrambling, exposed ridge travel and the logistics of high huts and national-park permits, and they sit at the mountaineering end of the spectrum rather than pure rock, but they are central to understanding what Taiwan offers the vertically minded.

## Season, access and etiquette

Long Dong climbs best from autumn through spring, when the heat and humidity ease; summer can be punishing and brings typhoon swell that closes the lower walls. The high peaks invert this, coming into condition in the warmer months, with winter bringing snow and ice to Xueshan. National-park permits are required for the big mountains and should be arranged well ahead. Throughout, salt and sea air corrode fixed gear at the coast, so checking anchors, climbing courteously and supporting the local community keeps this small but generous scene healthy.
