Peru holds some of the greatest mountains in the Americas. The Cordillera Blanca, the highest tropical mountain range on earth, rises above the town of Huaraz with a wall of glaciated peaks that draws alpinists from around the world. A few hours south, the volcanic rock forest of Hatun Machay offers a contrasting world of bolted sport routes and boulders at altitude. Between the high glaciers and the high crags, Peru is a destination defined by thin air and big ambitions. The areas below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=pe).

## The Cordillera Blanca and Huaraz

Huaraz, at around 3,050 metres, is the undisputed base for climbing in Peru. From here the Cordillera Blanca unfolds with summits like Huascarán, the country's highest at 6,768 metres, Alpamayo, often called one of the most beautiful mountains in the world, and Artesonraju, whose pyramid profile is said to have inspired the Paramount logo. Acclimatisation hikes, glacier travel and serious altitude define the experience, and the range rewards those who arrive prepared with proper time to adjust.

## Alpamayo, Huascarán and the Trekking Peaks

The Cordillera Blanca offers a ladder of objectives. Trekking and acclimatisation peaks such as Pisco (5,752 m) and Ishinca (5,530 m) introduce climbers to glaciated terrain and serve as warm-ups for the bigger lines. Alpamayo's southwest face, climbed via classic flutings, is a coveted prize for ice and alpine climbers, while Huascarán is the great altitude objective. These mountains demand glacier skills, weather judgement and respect for the thin tropical air above 5,000 metres.

## Hatun Machay: The Rock Forest

About three hours south of Huaraz, on a high plateau near Pampas Chico, Hatun Machay is Peru's premier sport-climbing and bouldering venue. This volcanic stone forest sits above 4,200 metres, with hundreds of routes and boulders threading between towers of rhyolite. Features like the Gran Desplome and the stalactite-laden overhangs offer steep, technical climbing, and the eerie landscape of spires and ancient rock art makes it unlike anywhere else. The altitude is brutal for sustained effort, so it is best visited after acclimatising in Huaraz.

## Sport Crags and Lower-Altitude Rock

Beyond the headline venues, Peru's climbing scene continues to develop. Crags near towns and in the valleys around Huaraz, including sectors such as Shupluy and other roadside walls, offer single-pitch sport climbing at more forgiving altitudes. Lima, the coastal capital, has its own gyms and small crags that serve the city's climbers and act as a starting point for visitors arriving by air. These lower venues are useful for training and for days when the high country is out of condition.

## Logistics, Permits and Altitude

Most climbers fly into Lima and travel overland to Huaraz by bus, a journey of roughly eight hours. The high peaks of Huascarán National Park require an entrance fee, and many objectives are best approached with local guides and arrieros (muleteers) who handle gear transport to base camps. Altitude is the defining hazard: rapid ascent invites acute mountain sickness, and a measured acclimatisation schedule is non-negotiable. Reliable rescue is limited, so self-sufficiency and conservative decisions matter.

## Season and Conditions

The Peruvian Andes have a clear climbing season. The dry austral winter, roughly May to September, brings stable weather and the best conditions for both alpinism and rock, with June through August the heart of the season. The wet months from December to March bring snow, storms and poor visibility to the high peaks. Even in the dry season, afternoons can deteriorate quickly, so early starts and conservative turnaround times are part of the rhythm of climbing in the Cordillera Blanca.
