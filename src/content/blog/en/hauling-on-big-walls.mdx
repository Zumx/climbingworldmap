On a multi-day big wall, a team cannot carry everything on their backs. Instead they pack their gear, food, water, and shelter into a haul bag — a heavy, durable duffel — and haul it up the wall pitch by pitch using a mechanical-advantage system. Hauling is one of the defining labours of big-wall climbing, the grinding work that gets a wall's worth of supplies up thousands of feet of rock. This guide explains how hauling systems work, why they matter, and what makes them efficient. Explore big-wall destinations on the [map](/map).

## Why Haul

On a free climb or short route, climbers carry what they need. But on a big wall taking days, a team needs gallons of water, days of food, a portaledge, sleeping gear, and a large rack — far too much to climb with. So they pack it all into a haul bag, often weighing many kilograms, and haul it up separately. The leader climbs the pitch, then hauls the bag up to the belay while the follower jumars the rope. Hauling is what makes multi-day walls possible, letting a team bring everything they need without climbing under its weight.

## The Haul Bag

The haul bag, affectionately called a "pig" for the way it drags and snags, is a heavy, armoured duffel designed to be dragged up rock. It holds the team's water, food, shelter, and gear, and it takes a beating against the wall. Packing it well — heavy items low, padding the contents, sealing it against spills — is an art, because a wall team lives out of the pig for days. A loaded haul bag can weigh as much as a person, which is why it must be hauled mechanically rather than carried.

## Mechanical Advantage

Hauling a heavy bag by brute strength is impossible, so haul systems use mechanical advantage: pulleys and rope rigged to multiply the hauler's force. The simplest is a 1:1 with body weight, where the climber attaches to the haul line and walks or rappels down to lift the bag. More complex systems — 2:1 or 3:1 with pulleys and progress-capture devices — reduce the effort at the cost of more rope pulled. The right system depends on the bag's weight and the wall's angle, and choosing well saves enormous energy over a long route.

## The Hauling Process

After leading a pitch and building the belay, the leader sets up the haul: rigging a pulley and progress-capture device at the anchor, attaching the haul line to the bag below, and then hauling — often by counterweighting with body weight, walking down the wall to lift the pig. The follower meanwhile cleans the pitch and jumars the rope. At the top, the bag is docked at the belay, the systems reset, and the team moves on. Repeated pitch after pitch, hauling is the steady labour that underlies the whole ascent.

## Efficiency and Suffering

Hauling is famously the hardest, least glamorous work on a big wall — heavy, repetitive, and exhausting, especially as water and food make the bag heaviest early on. Efficient systems and good technique make an enormous difference over a multi-day route, turning hours of struggle into manageable work. Experienced wall climbers obsess over haul efficiency, because a poorly rigged haul wastes energy a team cannot spare. Learning to haul well, and to pack a bag that hauls cleanly, is as much a part of wall craft as climbing itself.

## Learning the Craft

Hauling is a hands-on skill, learned by doing on real walls or in practice sessions, not from description. The systems must be rigged correctly, the progress capture must be reliable, and the whole sequence must flow, because a fouled haul high on a wall is a serious problem. Aspiring wall climbers learn hauling alongside aid climbing, ascending, and the other wall skills, building toward the integrated craft that a big wall demands. Mastered, hauling is what makes the great multi-day walls of the world achievable.
