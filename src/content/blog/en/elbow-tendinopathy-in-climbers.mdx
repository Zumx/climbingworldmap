Ask any group of climbers about their injuries and elbows come up again and again. The dull, persistent ache on the inside of the elbow, often called climber's elbow, is one of the sport's most common complaints, and it can linger for months. Unlike a torn pulley it rarely announces itself with a sudden pop; instead it creeps in, a faint soreness after sessions that gradually worsens until gripping a coffee cup hurts. It is a tendinopathy, a problem of overloaded tendon, and although it is stubborn it responds well to the right approach. Understanding why it happens is the first step to preventing it and to climbing through a long career without it derailing you.

## What Tendinopathy Actually Is

Tendinopathy is not a simple inflammation, despite the old name tendinitis. It is a degenerative change in the tendon caused by load that exceeds the tissue's ability to adapt. The collagen fibres become disorganised, the tendon thickens, and pain develops, often without much swelling. This matters because the old advice — rest and anti-inflammatories — often fails. A degenerated tendon does not heal through rest alone; it needs gradually applied load to stimulate the cells to lay down healthy, organised collagen. Recognising tendinopathy as a loading problem rather than an inflammation reframes the entire approach to treating it.

## Medial Versus Lateral Elbow

Two main tendinopathies affect climbers, on opposite sides of the elbow. The far more common is medial, on the inside, where the forearm flexor tendons attach — the muscles that grip and curl the fingers. This is climber's elbow, also called golfer's elbow, and it comes directly from the relentless gripping the sport demands. Less common is lateral epicondylitis, or tennis elbow, on the outside, affecting the extensor tendons. Climbers usually suffer the medial form because climbing is so heavily a flexing, gripping activity. Knowing which side hurts guides which muscles to load in rehab and which movements provoked the problem.

## Why Climbers Are So Prone

Climbing loads the forearm flexors enormously while the antagonist extensors do almost nothing, creating exactly the imbalance that overloads the medial elbow. Several specific factors make it worse: a rapid increase in training volume, heavy use of small crimps and pockets, lots of steep overhanging climbing, and hard pulling with a bent arm. Hangboard and campus training, which load the fingers and elbows hard, are frequent culprits when introduced too quickly. The common thread is the tendon being asked to absorb more than it has adapted to handle, whether through a sudden jump in difficulty, volume, or intensity.

## Catching It Early

Like most overuse injuries, elbow tendinopathy is far easier to manage early than once entrenched. The first signs are a vague ache on the inside of the elbow after climbing, a little stiffness in the morning, or discomfort when gripping. At this stage, reducing the aggravating load and starting gentle strengthening often resolves it within weeks. Ignored, it can progress to constant pain that makes everyday tasks uncomfortable and forces months off climbing. The climbers who avoid long layoffs are the ones who heed the early ache, adjust their training, and address it before it becomes chronic.

## The Role of Loading in Rehab

The cornerstone of treating elbow tendinopathy is progressive loading, not rest. Specific strengthening exercises load the affected tendon in a controlled, graded way to stimulate healing and rebuild capacity. For the medial elbow, wrist curls and grip work performed slowly, especially the lowering phase, are central. Many climbers also use a rubber bar that is twisted and slowly released, a simple tool that has become a standard rehab device. The key is to load enough to stimulate adaptation without flaring the pain badly, building up over weeks and months. Patience is essential; tendons are slow tissue and rushing rehab restarts the cycle.

## Building a Resilient Elbow

Prevention rests on the same principles that drive rehab. Antagonist training for the forearm extensors balances the overdeveloped flexors and is one of the most effective preventives — wrist extensions, reverse curls, and finger-extension work with a rubber band. Sensible progression matters even more: increasing climbing volume and intensity gradually, introducing hangboarding cautiously, and warming the elbows along with the fingers. Strong, well-balanced forearms with tendons accustomed to load are far more resistant. The climbers who train their antagonists and progress patiently are the ones who rarely meet climber's elbow at all.

## Returning to Hard Climbing

Coming back from elbow tendinopathy demands the same patience as the rehab itself. Resuming hard climbing too soon, before the tendon has rebuilt capacity, simply restarts the problem and lengthens the layoff. A graded return — starting on big holds and gentle angles, avoiding the steep crimping that provoked it, and building load slowly while continuing the strengthening work — gives the tendon time to keep adapting. Many climbers manage a niggling elbow for years by keeping up the rehab exercises as ongoing maintenance, climbing strong on the granite of Yosemite or the limestone of the Verdon without the ache ever returning to stop them.
