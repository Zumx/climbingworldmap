On the Andaman coast of Thailand, two neighbouring beaches sit beneath the same spectacular limestone karst, yet offer subtly different experiences. Railay is the more developed, with resorts and a polished beach scene, while Tonsai is the rougher, cheaper, climber-focused neighbour. Both share world-class limestone climbing above the warm sea, but the choice between them shapes the character of a Thailand climbing trip. Find them both on the [map](/map?country=th).

## The Shared Setting

Tonsai and Railay sit on the Railay peninsula, a cluster of beaches accessible only by boat, surrounded by towering limestone karst cliffs draped with tufas and stalactites. The two beaches are separated by a short walk over the headland or around the rocks at low tide. They share the same superb climbing, the same warm Andaman Sea, and the same dramatic karst scenery, but their atmospheres differ markedly.

## Railay: The Polished Side

Railay, particularly East and West Railay, is the more developed and upmarket side. It holds resorts, restaurants, and a polished beach-holiday atmosphere, attracting tourists alongside climbers. The climbing is excellent and accessible, with crags close to the beaches, and Railay suits those who want comfort, good amenities, and a mix of climbing and beach holiday. It is the more convenient and comfortable base.

## Tonsai: The Climber's Beach

Tonsai, just over the headland, is the rougher, cheaper, more bohemian neighbour, long the heart of the climbing scene. Cheaper bungalows, a laid-back atmosphere, and a focus on climbing draw the dedicated climbers, and the steep, overhanging walls around Tonsai hold some of the hardest and most powerful routes in the area. Tonsai is for those who prioritise climbing and atmosphere over comfort, and it has a devoted following.

## The Climbing

Both beaches access world-class limestone, but with different emphases. Railay's crags offer a wealth of accessible single and multi-pitch routes across the grades, ideal for a broad range of climbers. Tonsai's walls tend toward the steep and powerful, with hard test-pieces on overhanging tufa-draped rock. Deep water soloing is accessible from both by boat. Together they offer an enormous range, and many climbers move between the two to sample the full spread of climbing.

## Which to Choose?

The choice depends on what a climber wants. Railay suits those seeking comfort, amenities, and a mix of climbing and holiday, ideal for groups of mixed interests or those wanting an easier base. Tonsai suits dedicated climbers who prioritise the climbing scene, the steeper routes, and a cheaper, more laid-back atmosphere. Many visit both, basing themselves according to taste and walking over to climb at the other. Both share the same season — the cooler, drier months from roughly November to March.
