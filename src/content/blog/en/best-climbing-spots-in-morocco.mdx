Morocco compresses an astonishing amount of rock between the Mediterranean and the Sahara. The High Atlas carries North Africa's highest summits and, hidden in its folds, the canyon sanctuary of Taghia, whose limestone walls rank with Europe's best. Todra Gorge serves sun-soaked sport climbing beside palm groves; the Anti-Atlas around Tafraoute offers a lifetime of quartzite trad; and the country's cities are sprouting gyms from Agadir to Casablanca. Add mint tea, tagines and mountain hospitality, and Morocco is the natural first step for climbers looking beyond Europe. The places below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=ma).

## Todra Gorge

On the Saharan side of the High Atlas near Tinghir, the Todra Gorge is Morocco's most famous climbing destination. Orange and grey limestone walls rise up to 300 metres above a palm-lined river, with hundreds of bolted routes spread through sectors from the canyon mouth to the quieter upper gorge — names like Petite Gorge and the pillar sectors recur in every climber's logbook. Grades cluster in the friendly 5s and 6s with harder lines for the keen, and the morning-shade, afternoon-sun rhythm of the canyon makes route planning part of the fun. Gîtes and cafés sit minutes from the rock.

## Taghia: The Canyon Cathedral

Deep in the central High Atlas, reached by a mule trail from the roadhead at Zaouia Ahansal, the village of Taghia sits in a horseshoe of limestone walls up to 800 metres high. Faces like Oujdad and Tagoujimt n'Tsouiant carry some of the great multi-pitch routes of Africa — long, committing lines, often with bolted belays and serious climbing between them. There is no road, no shop beyond basics, and no rescue service: gîtes in the village, mules for loads and self-sufficiency for everything else. For experienced teams, Taghia is simply one of the finest big-wall venues on Earth.

## The Anti-Atlas: Tafraoute and Jebel el Kest

In the south, around the almond-growing town of Tafraoute, the quartzite of the Anti-Atlas spreads across hillsides in endless crags and buttresses. This is trad country — thousands of recorded routes and countless unclimbed lines on sound, friendly rock, mostly in the lower and middle grades, with British climbers having quietly developed the area over decades. Winter is the season, when northern Europe shivers and the Ameln Valley basks at 20 °C. The granite boulders of Tafraoute's painted-rock desert add an unexpected bouldering bonus.

## Toubkal and the High Atlas Summits

Jbel Toubkal, at 4,167 metres the highest peak in North Africa, anchors a massif of long ridges and stony cwms above Imlil, ninety minutes from Marrakech. The standard route from the Toubkal refuges is a non-technical but genuinely high ascent, snow-bound and crampon-worthy from late autumn to spring, while the surrounding ridges offer scrambles and alpine lines. Mountain guides and muleteers from Imlil keep logistics simple. Note that a guide is now required for Toubkal National Park — easy to arrange locally and well worth the support it provides village economies.

## Oukaïmeden and the Bouldering Plateaus

Above 2,600 metres in the High Atlas, the plateau of Oukaïmeden — Morocco's ski resort — doubles as a summer bouldering and cragging escape, its sandstone and granite blocks scattered among prehistoric rock carvings. The cool altitude makes it the answer to July and August, when the lowland crags bake. Closer to the cities, climbers train at venues from Azrarag in Agadir to urban walls in Casablanca and Rabat, the visible edge of a young Moroccan scene that increasingly produces its own strong climbers rather than only hosting visitors.

## Culture, Logistics and Etiquette

Climbing trips here run on Moroccan rhythms. Fridays are quiet, Ramadan changes village schedules, and a few words of Darija or Tamazight open every door. Cash rules outside the cities, hire cars handle the mountain roads better than their price suggests, and gîte half-board — tagine, bread, mint tea — is the default accommodation at Todra, Taghia and Tafraoute alike. Respect local dress norms in villages, ask before photographing people, and budget time generously: distances are modest but mountain roads are slow.

## Seasons

Morocco climbs year-round if you move with the seasons. October to April is prime for Todra, Tafraoute and the south, with winter the absolute sweet spot in the Anti-Atlas. Taghia's season is May to October, with midsummer hot but workable on shaded faces. Toubkal is a snow peak from November to May and a hot scree walk in August. The general rule: winter in the south, summer in the high mountains, spring and autumn nearly everywhere.
