Watch two climbers of similar strength on the same long route and the difference often shows at minute four, not move four. One climbs into the upper wall with arms still working; the other arrives wooden-fingered and pumped beyond saving. The gap was not created on the hard moves but between them — at the jug one climber draped over and recovered while the other merely paused and worried. Resting on route is a genuine technical skill: finding the positions, unloading the arms, managing breath and clock. On endurance routes it decides outcomes more often than crimp strength does, and unlike crimp strength it can improve in a single season.

## What the Pump Actually Is

The forearm pump is a plumbing problem. Gripping above roughly half of maximum force squeezes the forearm's blood vessels shut, so the working muscles burn fuel without resupply and flood with metabolic by-products. The swelling, burning and fading grip that follow are reversible — but only if pressure drops long enough for blood to flow again. That is the entire physiology of resting: get the gripping force of at least one arm near zero, hold it there, and circulation does the repair work. Every technique in this article is a way of buying low-effort seconds for one forearm at a time.

## Learning to See Rests

Rests hide in plain sight, and spotting them is a reading skill. The obvious ones are jugs and ledges, but the better currency is geometry: a stem across a corner that loads the legs and frees both hands, a slab breaking a steep wall where the feet take everything, a tufa pinched between knee and thigh, a hole that swallows a fist for a hands-free jam. Scan for them from the ground as part of reading the route, and mark them mentally against the cruxes — knowing a rest waits two moves above the hard section changes how boldly you climb it. On unfamiliar rock, suspect every corner, every big foothold, every change of angle.

## The Shakeout, Done Properly

The standard rest is the shakeout: hang on a straight arm from the best available hold and dangle the other, shaking it loosely. Details decide whether it works. Hang skeletally — straight arm, relaxed shoulder, hips close to the wall under the hold — so the resting position itself costs little. Alternate arms in generous intervals rather than frantic swaps. Many climbers use the G-tox method, alternating the dangling arm between hanging low and raising it overhead; the raised phase helps drain the congested forearm and has measurable support for speeding recovery. And shake with the hand genuinely open and soft: a clenched dangling fist recovers nothing.

## Kneebars and the Hands-Free Miracle

Where the rock allows it, the kneebar is the king of rests: foot on a hold, knee or lower thigh cammed against an opposing feature, leg locked into a strut that holds your weight with no hands at all. On the steep tufa walls of Kalymnos and the caves of Rodellar, whole grades hinge on finding and using them, and serious endurance climbers carry sticky rubber kneepads precisely for this. The technique rewards experimentation: the locking position is often higher or deeper than feels natural, and a kneebar that holds 90 percent of your weight still transforms recovery. Heel hooks and toe cams can serve the same role in smaller doses.

## Breathing, Pulse and the Mental Reset

A rest recovers more than forearms. Climbing hard raises heart rate and shortens breath, and fear compounds both; arriving at a shakeout gasping and wide-eyed wastes its value. Use the first seconds to breathe deliberately — long exhales, slower rhythm — and feel the pulse settle before you start counting recovery. The rest is also where you reread the route above, rehearse the next sequence and renegotiate with your doubts. Many strong redpoint climbers describe rests as scheduled mental checkpoints: arrive, breathe, shake, plan, commit. The ritual matters as much as the physiology.

## How Long to Stay

There is a point where a rest stops paying rent. On a good hands-off rest, stay until breathing settles and the forearms soften — often two to five minutes before a crux, and worth every second. On a marginal shakeout where holding the rest position itself costs energy, the calculus tightens: shake briefly, then move, because half-recovery now beats slow leakage into exhaustion. The honest test is trajectory — if you feel yourself recovering, stay; if you are merely postponing while seeping strength, go. This judgement sharpens with deliberate practice and attention, route after route.

## Training the Skill

Resting improves fastest when practised on purpose. On gym routes below your limit, stop at every plausible stance and force a shakeout, even when unnecessary — you are training the habit of seeing and using rests. Climb laps where the rule is recovering fully at a designated hold before continuing. Practise kneebars on steep boulders until the locking position is familiar, and try the G-tox on a pumpy circuit to feel the difference. On redpoint projects, choreograph the rests as precisely as the cruxes: where, how long, which arm first. Climbers who do this discover that their endurance was better than they thought; it was their resting that was weak.
