The harness is the climber's link to the rope, the piece of gear that distributes the force of a fall or a hang across the body and keeps you attached to the safety system. A good harness is comfortable enough to forget and safe enough to trust your life to, and the variety of designs reflects the variety of climbing. Choosing the right harness, and fitting and using it correctly, is fundamental. This guide explains the parts of a harness, the main types, and how to choose and fit one for your climbing. ## The Parts of a Harness

A climbing harness has several key components. The waist belt wraps around the waist above the hips and bears most of the load. The leg loops go around the upper thighs and spread the load comfortably. The belay loop, a strong sewn loop at the front, is the central attachment point for belaying and rappelling. The tie-in points — usually two loops where the waist belt and leg loops meet at the front — are where the rope is tied in. Gear loops around the sides hold racked equipment.

## Types of Harness

Harnesses vary by intended use. All-round harnesses, with moderate padding and several gear loops, suit most climbers for sport, trad, and gym. Sport-climbing harnesses are light and minimally padded for performance. Trad and alpine harnesses have more gear loops for a full rack and may have adjustable leg loops for layering clothing. Lightweight alpine and ski-mountaineering harnesses strip weight to a minimum. Big-wall harnesses add heavy padding for comfort during long hangs.

## Fit Is Everything

A harness must fit correctly to be both safe and comfortable. The waist belt should sit snugly above the hip bones, tight enough that it cannot be pulled down over the hips, because the hips are what stop it coming off in an upside-down fall. The leg loops should be snug but allow movement. A correctly fitting harness is comfortable to hang in and stays securely in place. Try a harness on, hang in it if possible, and ensure the waist genuinely cannot slip over the hips.

## Adjustability and Buckles

Modern harnesses use buckles that are either pre-threaded and self-locking or require doubling back manually — always check the manufacturer's instructions, as a buckle threaded wrong can fail. Some harnesses have adjustable leg loops, useful for fitting over varying layers in cold conditions, while others have fixed leg loops that save weight. The waist belt is always adjustable. Whatever the system, the buckles must be done up correctly every time, and checked as part of the pre-climb buddy check.

## Comfort and Padding

How much padding you want depends on how you climb. Sport climbers and boulderers, who hang less, often prefer light, minimally padded harnesses. Climbers who spend long periods hanging — working routes, big-wall climbing, or belaying many falls — value more padding in the waist and legs for comfort. More padding adds weight and bulk, so the choice is a trade-off between comfort and lightness, guided by the kind of climbing you do most.

## Choosing and Caring for Your Harness

For most climbers, a comfortable all-round harness with a few gear loops is the ideal first and main choice, specialising only as their climbing demands. Buy a properly certified harness from a reputable manufacturer, fit it carefully, and learn to put it on and check it correctly. Inspect it regularly for wear, especially at the tie-in points and belay loop, and retire it when worn or after its recommended lifespan. The harness is safety-critical, so never compromise on fit, condition, or correct use.
