The figure-eight follow-through is the most important knot in climbing, the one that ties the climber to the rope on virtually every roped ascent in the world. It is strong, secure, and — crucially — easy to inspect, so that a partner can verify it at a glance. Tied correctly every time, it is the trusted connection on which a climber's life hangs; tied wrong, it is a hidden hazard. Understanding how to tie, dress, and check this single knot is one of the first and most important things any roped climber learns. This guide explains it in detail. ## Why the Figure-Eight Is the Standard

Climbers tie in with the figure-eight follow-through for good reasons. It is very strong, retaining most of the rope's strength; it is secure and resists working loose under the cyclic loading of climbing; and it is easy to check visually, because a correct figure-eight has a recognisable, symmetrical shape that a partner can verify instantly. These qualities — strength, security, and checkability — make it the universally taught and trusted tie-in knot at gyms and crags worldwide.

## How to Tie It

The figure-eight follow-through starts with a single figure-eight tied in the rope about a metre from the end. The tail is then passed through both tie-in points of the harness, exactly as the rope itself attaches, and "followed through" — retracing the original figure-eight in reverse so the knot is doubled. The result is a neat, symmetrical figure-eight with the rope and tail running parallel through every curve. Pulling all four strands tight dresses and sets the knot.

## Dressing and Tightening

A knot is only as good as how it is dressed and tightened. The figure-eight should be neat, with the parallel strands running side by side and not crossed, and pulled firmly tight so it cannot slip or rearrange under load. A messy, loose, or crossed knot is weaker and harder to check. After tying, take a moment to dress the knot, tighten all the strands, and confirm it looks correct, with a tail of adequate length protruding.

## Checking the Knot

Every tie-in should be checked, by yourself and ideally by your partner. A correct figure-eight has the characteristic shape of two interlocking loops, with five visible pairs of strands when looked at from the flat side. The rope must pass through both harness tie-in points, the knot must be dressed and tight, and the tail must be long enough. This buddy check, done before every climb, catches the kind of tie-in error that has caused fatal accidents even to experienced climbers.

## Common Errors

The figure-eight is forgiving, but errors happen. Threading the rope through only one tie-in point, or through the wrong part of the harness, is a serious and not-uncommon mistake. An incompletely followed-through knot, a crossed or messy knot, or too short a tail all reduce security. Getting distracted partway through tying in — a frequent cause of accidents — can leave the knot unfinished. The defence is a consistent routine: tie in fully, dress, tighten, and check, every single time, without interruption.

## A Habit That Saves Lives

The discipline around the figure-eight is what makes it safe. Tie it the same way every time, never let yourself be interrupted while tying in, finish the knot before doing anything else, and always perform a partner check before leaving the ground. These simple habits, applied without exception, ensure that the one knot your life depends on most is correct every time you trust it. Familiarity must never breed carelessness with the tie-in.
