Belaying is the single most important skill in roped climbing: it is the act of managing the rope so that, if the climber falls, the belayer holds them. A competent belay is the difference between a controlled, harmless fall and a serious accident. Every roped climber must learn to belay properly, and the principles are universal whether you climb in a gym or on a remote crag. This guide covers the fundamentals, but there is no substitute for hands-on instruction from a qualified climber or instructor before you take a real fall. ## The Core Principle: Never Let Go of the Brake

The single rule that underpins all belaying is simple: the brake hand never leaves the rope. The belay device creates friction, but it only works when the rope below the device — the brake strand — is gripped and angled correctly. As long as the brake hand holds the rope below the device in the braking position, the climber is safe. Every other movement of belaying is built around preserving that grip. Internalising this rule until it is automatic is the first and most important step.

## The PBUS Method

The standard technique for tube-style devices is PBUS: Pull, Brake, Under, Slide. You pull rope through the device with the brake hand and guide hand, then move the brake hand down to the braking position, slide the guide hand under the brake hand to grip the rope, then slide the brake hand back up to start again. Throughout, one hand is always in the brake position. PBUS keeps the brake hand engaged at every stage and is the foundation taught in climbing courses worldwide.

## Belay Devices

Most belaying uses a tube-style device such as a Black Diamond ATC, which threads the rope through a slot and creates friction against the carabiner. Assisted-braking devices like the Petzl GriGri add a camming mechanism that helps lock the rope under load, but they still require the brake hand at all times — they assist, they do not replace good technique. Whatever the device, the belayer must understand exactly how it generates friction before trusting it with a life.

## Stance and Position

A good belayer stands close to the wall, slightly to one side of the first bolt or the fall line, ready to absorb a fall without being pulled off balance or slammed into the rock. For a heavier climber, the belayer may need to brace or use a ground anchor. Standing too far back invites being yanked forward and lifted; standing in the fall line risks a collision. The belayer should keep the rope neither too tight, which pulls the climber off, nor too slack, which lengthens a fall.

## Communication

Clear, agreed commands prevent accidents. Standard calls include "On belay?" / "Belay on," "Climbing" / "Climb on," "Take" to be held tight, "Slack" for more rope, and "Lower" to be brought down. Both partners must use the same terms and confirm them. Before leaving the ground, partners perform a buddy check: harness buckles, knot, belay device, and carabiner locked. A skipped check is behind a sobering number of accidents.

## Lead Belaying and Catching Falls

Belaying a lead climber is more demanding than top-rope: you feed rope out as they climb and clip, manage slack so it is neither dangerous nor restrictive, and give a soft catch on a fall by allowing a small amount of movement rather than locking off rigidly. A dynamic belay reduces the force on the climber and the top piece. This is an advanced skill that should be learned under supervision before belaying a leader on real terrain.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

The deadliest errors are letting go of the brake hand, threading the device wrong, and failing to check the system. Others include too much slack at the start of a lead, lowering too fast, and inattention while the climber rests. Distraction is a real hazard: a belayer who looks at their phone is not belaying. Treat every belay as holding a life in your hands, because that is exactly what it is.
