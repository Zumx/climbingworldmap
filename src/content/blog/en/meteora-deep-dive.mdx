In central Greece, the pillars of Meteora rise from the plain of Thessaly in one of the most extraordinary landscapes on Earth — a forest of rounded conglomerate towers, hundreds of metres tall, crowned by centuries-old monasteries perched impossibly on their summits. Climbing here is adventurous, traditional, and committing, on a strange pebbled rock unlike any other. Meteora combines world-class climbing with a setting of deep spiritual and historic significance. Find it on the [map](/map?country=gr).

## The Setting

Meteora — the name means "suspended in the air" — is a cluster of towering conglomerate pillars rising abruptly from the Thessalian plain near the town of Kalambaka. The monasteries built atop the pillars from the 14th century onward, reached historically by ladders and nets, give the landscape its unique character and a profound sense of history. Climbing among these towers, beneath the monasteries, is one of the most atmospheric experiences in the climbing world.

## The Rock

The rock of Meteora is conglomerate — a curious mix of rounded pebbles and cobbles cemented together, weathered into smooth, rounded towers. The climbing involves pulling on the pebbles and the pockets between them, with friction on the rounded surfaces, demanding a particular technique and a cool head. The rock can feel insecure to those used to positive holds, and the bold, adventurous nature of the climbing is part of what makes Meteora unique and challenging.

## The Bold Tradition

Climbing at Meteora carries a famously bold, traditional ethic, developed over decades by German and Greek climbers who established the routes in a committing, ground-up style. Protection can be spaced, and the rounded conglomerate demands confidence and judgement. The tradition values adventure and respect for the rock and the sacred landscape, and climbing here means engaging with this serious, committing style. It is not a place for those seeking convenience, but a place of genuine mountain adventure.

## The Towers and Routes

Meteora's towers hold a wealth of multi-pitch routes that ascend the rounded pillars to airy summits. The routes vary in difficulty and seriousness, but all share the adventurous character of the conglomerate and the committing positions among the towers. Topping out on a Meteora pillar, with the monasteries and the plain spread below, is an unforgettable experience that combines the climbing with the extraordinary spiritual landscape.

## Season and Respect

Meteora climbs best in the cooler months of spring and autumn, when temperatures are moderate and the rock is in condition. Summer can be hot, and the conglomerate is best avoided when wet. Beyond the climbing conditions, Meteora demands respect for its sacred character — the monasteries are active religious sites, and climbers must be mindful of the spiritual significance of the landscape and the need to climb sensitively in such a place.
