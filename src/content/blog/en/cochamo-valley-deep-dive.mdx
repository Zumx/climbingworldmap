In the Los Lagos region of northern Chilean Patagonia, the Cochamó valley hides one of the world's great granite sanctuaries. Often called the Yosemite of South America, it earns the comparison honestly: walls up to a thousand metres rise straight from temperate rainforest, water-streaked and glacier-carved, with routes that demand everything from runout slab to wide cracks. There is no road. Everything — ropes, food, racks — walks in twelve kilometres of mud and roots to the meadow of La Junta, and that approach is exactly why Cochamó still feels wild. It is big-wall climbing with the volume turned up and the crowds turned off.

## The valley and the comparison it earns

Cochamó sits at the head of the Reloncaví estuary, where fjord country meets the Andes. From the village of Cochamó, the valley runs inland beneath domes and towers of pale granite, polished by ancient ice and split by crack systems that run for hundreds of metres. The Yosemite comparison captures the scale and the rock quality, but the setting is different: instead of pines and asphalt, there is dripping Valdivian rainforest, alerce trees thousands of years old, and condors riding the updrafts. The result is a place that feels closer to an expedition than a road trip, even though the trailhead is only a few hours from Puerto Varas.

## Getting in: the trail to La Junta

The approach is part of the experience. From the road end near Cochamó village, a historic drovers' trail — once used to herd cattle over the Andes from Argentina — winds about twelve kilometres up-valley to La Junta, a grassy clearing where the climbing begins. The hike takes four to six hours with a heavy pack, longer when rain turns the trail into churned mud; pack horses can be hired to carry loads. At La Junta, organised campgrounds and a refugio cluster near the river, and a reservation system now manages visitor numbers, so book your spot before you travel. River crossings and approach scrambles to the walls add their own spice.

## The walls: Trinidad, Capicua and the Anfiteatro

The valley's compass points are unmistakable. Cerro Trinidad, the first great wall visible from the trail, carries some of the most famous routes, its three summits striped with water streaks. Across the valley, Cerro Capicúa throws down a sweep of slabs and corners approaching a kilometre in height, while the Anfiteatro — a horseshoe of steep granite above La Junta — and formations like Cerro Arco Iris, whose summit scramble is the valley's classic acclimatisation day, complete the cirque. Walls catch sun at different hours, and the long daylight of the austral summer gives generous climbing windows between weather systems.

## The climbing: cracks, slabs and commitment

Cochamó is overwhelmingly a traditional climbing area. Routes follow crack and corner systems protected by cams and nuts, linked by slab sections where bolts are sparse and the climbing is bold. Modern classics such as Al Centro y Adentro and Bienvenidos a mi Insomnio have made the valley famous among big-wall free climbers, with pitch counts in the teens and twenties and difficulties from moderate to world-class. Many parties fix lines or haul to ledge camps for multi-day pushes. Expect vegetated cracks on less-travelled routes, long descents by abseil, and grades that feel honest after the walk-in. A double rack and a taste for adventure are the minimum entry fee.

## Weather: the price of green walls

The rainforest is green for a reason. Cochamó's weather arrives off the Pacific, and even in high summer multi-day storms soak the valley, turning streaks to waterfalls and the trail to soup. The climbing season runs from roughly December to March, with January and February the most settled; outside those months, long wet spells dominate. Successful trips build in margin — two weeks gives a realistic chance at a big route, and patience is a core skill. When the granite dries, it dries fast, and locals read the cycles well: ask at La Junta before committing to a wall after rain.

## Logistics, reservations and ethics

A Cochamó trip starts in Puerto Montt or Puerto Varas, with bus or transfer to Cochamó village and the trailhead beyond. Reserve La Junta camping in advance through the valley's official system, carry cash for horses and camp fees, and plan food for the full stay — there is no resupply up-valley. The valley is private and community-managed wilderness: pack out all rubbish, use the composting toilets, respect fire bans and leave the bolting drill at home except where local consensus allows hardware on new terrain. The fragile meadow and forest are as much a part of Cochamó as the granite.

## Beyond the valley

Cochamó rewards those who linger in the region. The neighbouring Valle Trinidad and the upper basins hide quieter walls and unclimbed lines for exploratory parties, and the broader Río Puelo watershed offers trekking through lake-and-fjord country that few tourists see. Back toward the coast, Puerto Varas makes a comfortable staging town beneath the cone of Volcán Osorno, and sport climbers can warm up at Chilean crags on the journey south. For many visitors, though, the valley itself is the whole point: one basecamp, a fortnight of weather windows, and walls that will outlast every plan made beneath them.
