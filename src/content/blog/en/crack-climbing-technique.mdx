Crack climbing is a fundamental and uniquely satisfying style: instead of pulling on holds, the climber jams hands, fingers, and feet into fissures in the rock, using the body itself as the hold. It is the heart of traditional climbing, the discipline that built the granite reputations of Yosemite and the gritstone of England, and it demands a completely different skill set from face climbing. Learning to jam transforms blank-looking cracks into ladders. This guide covers the core techniques for every crack size and the gear that protects them. ## The Principle of Jamming

Crack climbing works by jamming: inserting part of the body into the crack and expanding or twisting it so that it locks against the rock. A jam is not about gripping but about creating a secure wedge or cam with the hand, fingers, or foot. Done well, a jam can feel utterly solid, even on a smooth, featureless crack. The technique is counterintuitive at first, can be hard on the skin, and rewards practice and a willingness to trust the lock.

## Finger Cracks

In a finger crack, the climber slots fingers into the fissure and twists to lock them. Thin finger cracks take just the fingertips, while wider ones allow fingers to sink deeper. The classic technique is to insert the fingers thumb-down and torque, camming them against the crack walls. Finger cracks are often the most technical and painful size, demanding precise placement and a tolerance for the bite of the rock on the knuckles and tips.

## Hand and Fist Cracks

The hand crack is the most beloved size, where the whole hand slots in and expands to form a secure jam by cupping or flexing. A good hand jam can be as solid as a jug, allowing climbers to move smoothly up perfect splitters. When the crack is too wide for a hand jam, the climber makes a fist jam, inserting a clenched fist that expands against the walls. Hand and fist cracks are the foundation of classic crack climbing.

## Offwidths and Chimneys

When a crack is too wide to jam with the hand but too narrow to climb inside, it becomes an offwidth — notoriously awkward and strenuous, climbed with arm bars, leg locks, and full-body technique. Wider still are chimneys, cracks big enough to climb inside, ascended by pressing the back against one wall and the feet or knees against the other. Both demand stamina, technique, and a tolerance for inelegant, full-contact struggle that crack climbers come to relish.

## Footwork in Cracks

Feet are jammed as well as hands. In thinner cracks, the climber turns the foot sideways, slots the edge of the sole in, and rotates it upright to cam it against the walls — a torque that can feel insecure until trusted. In hand-sized cracks the toe slots in directly. Good crack footwork takes weight off the hands and lets the climber rest and move efficiently. Taping the hands and wearing sturdy shoes protects the skin from the inevitable abrasion.

## Protecting Cracks

Cracks are the natural home of removable protection. The climber places cams, which expand to grip the crack walls, and nuts, which wedge into constrictions, then clips the rope and removes them on the way up or has the second clean them. Reading the crack to choose and place the right piece is a core trad skill. A well-protected crack is one of climbing's great pleasures; placing gear poorly is one of its real dangers, so practice and instruction matter.
