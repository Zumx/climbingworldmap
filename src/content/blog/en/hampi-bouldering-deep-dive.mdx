Hampi is what happens when a bouldering destination grows inside a wonder of the world. Across the Tungabhadra river from the ruined capital of the Vijayanagara empire, in the northern Karnataka scrublands of southern India, golden granite boulders stack in hills and ridges as far as the eye can see — eggs, blades and split cubes balanced on each other as if arranged by giants. Between them lie rice paddies, banana plantations and 500-year-old temples. Climbers discovered the place in the 1990s, mythology claimed it millennia earlier: this is said to be Kishkindha, the monkey kingdom of the Ramayana. Few climbing trips anywhere feel so otherworldly.

## A landscape of stacked granite

The Hampi region is a 2,500-million-year-old granite landscape eroded into one of the planet's great boulder fields. The stone is coarse, crystalline granite with crisp crimps, rough slopers and the occasional perfect crack, and problems range from gentle slabs to brutal compression testpieces. Most climbing happens on the north side of the river around the village of Virupapur Gaddi — the so-called Hampi Island — where sectors sit minutes from the guesthouses. The supply is effectively endless: developed problems number in the thousands, and virgin lines stare down at every visitor willing to clean and brush.

## The classic sectors

The Rishimuk plateau is the historic heart, a rolling shelf of boulders above the rice paddies where the first generations of visiting climbers established the area's classics. Nearby, the Egg Boulders cluster their improbable ovoid blocks, and sectors with names passed down through guesthouse logbooks fan out across the hillsides toward the Hanuman temple on Anjaneya Hill, whose white-painted stairway is the area's most famous landmark. Across the river, boulders crowd the slopes of Matanga Hill and the edges of the ruins themselves — though climbing on or near protected monuments is strictly off limits and rightly so.

## Style, grades and skin management

Hampi climbing is honest and sharp. The granite rewards body tension, precise feet and an appetite for top-outs that often finish on rounded, gritty domes. Grades cover the full spread, but the rock's texture is the great equaliser: three days on, one day off is an ambitious schedule, and skin management — sanding, balm, taping — becomes a daily ritual. Landings vary from flat sand to nests of blocks, so two pads and attentive spotters are the norm; pads can usually be rented through guesthouses and local climbing shops in season. Early morning and late afternoon are prime, when the stone cools and the light turns the hills amber.

## Goan Corner and climber life

Climber culture in Hampi centres on legendary guesthouses, none more so than the Goan Corner, tucked behind the rice paddies at the foot of the boulders — part hostel, part topo archive, part international team meeting. Life runs on a simple loop: dawn session, banana pancakes, midday swim or hammock, evening session, communal dinner. The scene is sociable and beginner-friendly, with partners and beta always available in season. Respect the village around you: dress modestly away from the crag, keep noise down at night and remember that the paddies and plantations are working farmland, not a campsite.

## Season, heat and the monsoon

The season is the dry, cool winter, roughly November to February, when days are warm, nights pleasant and the rock at its best; December and January are peak months. By March the heat builds fiercely, and the summer and monsoon months from April to September are largely unclimbable. Even in season, the midday sun empties the boulders — the rhythm of dawn and dusk sessions is not a lifestyle choice but thermodynamics. Carry water everywhere, watch for thorny scrub on approaches, and check holds for wildlife; the boulders are home to more than climbers.

## Heritage, ethics and access

Hampi is a UNESCO World Heritage site, and the climbing community is a guest within it. Never climb on temples, walls, carvings or any structure of the ruins; obey signage and the directions of heritage authorities; and avoid chipping, excessive chalk and loud music at the boulders. Access arrangements have shifted over the years as authorities balance tourism, conservation and village life, so follow current local guidance from guesthouses and resident climbers. The privilege of bouldering inside a sacred landscape is exactly that — a privilege that depends on every visitor's behaviour.

## Beyond Hampi: Badami and the south

Hampi pairs perfectly with Badami, a half-day's travel west, where red sandstone cliffs above ancient cave temples offer India's best sport climbing and a complete change of style. Bangalore, the usual gateway city, has a thriving gym scene for layovers, and the temples, river ghats and sunset points of Hampi itself can absorb every rest day of a long trip. Many climbers budget a month: two weeks of skin cycles in Hampi, a week in Badami, and the rest lost happily to South Indian thalis and train windows.
