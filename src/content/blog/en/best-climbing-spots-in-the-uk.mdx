The United Kingdom is, more than anywhere, the home of traditional climbing. Its relatively small crags have produced an outsized influence on the sport, defining a bold, ground-up, gear-placing ethic that climbers carry around the world. From the rough gritstone edges of the Peak District to the sea cliffs of Pembroke, the slate of Snowdonia, and the mountain crags of Scotland, British climbing is varied, atmospheric, and proudly committing. The areas below are the essentials; find them on the [map](/map?country=gb).

## The Peak District

The Peak District in central England is the heartland of British gritstone. Its edges — Stanage, Burbage, Froggatt, Curbar, and others — offer rough, friction-rich routes on coarse sandstone, protected by traditional gear and famous for their bold, technical nature. The grit is where generations of British climbers learned the craft, and its short, intense routes have an importance to the sport out of all proportion to their height. Spring and autumn bring the best friction.

## Snowdonia (Eryri), North Wales

The mountains of Snowdonia hold some of Britain's most storied climbing. The cliffs of the Llanberis Pass, the great mountain crag of Cloggy (Clogwyn Du'r Arddu) beneath Snowdon, and the disused slate quarries above Llanberis offer everything from classic mountain trad to the unique, atmospheric climbing of the slate. This is a landscape steeped in climbing history, where many of the boldest advances in British climbing were made.

## Pembroke, South Wales

The sea cliffs of Pembroke, on the southwest Welsh coast, offer some of the finest trad climbing in Britain. Limestone cliffs drop into the Atlantic, with routes accessed by abseil and climbed above the sea — a committing, atmospheric style on excellent rock. The combination of quality climbing, dramatic position, and the coastal setting makes Pembroke a beloved destination, best in the drier months and around the military firing-range access restrictions.

## The Lake District

The Lake District, England's mountain region, offers mountain trad climbing on crags set among fells and lakes. Routes here have a long history reaching back to the very origins of the sport, and the combination of adventurous climbing and beautiful, weather-prone mountain scenery defines the Lakes experience. The crags range from accessible valley venues to serious mountain cliffs, all carrying the bold British trad tradition.

## Scotland

Scotland offers the most adventurous climbing in Britain. The granite and gabbro of the Highlands, the sea stacks of the north and the islands, and above all the world-famous winter climbing on the frozen mountain faces give Scotland a unique character. Scottish winter climbing — bold, weather-dependent mixed and ice routes on the high mountains — is a discipline in itself, demanding experience, fitness, and a tolerance for harsh conditions.

## The Bouldering Scene

Britain also has a rich bouldering tradition, much of it on the same gritstone as its trad routes. The Peak District edges and the boulders scattered across the moors offer powerful, technical problems, and areas like the gritstone of Yorkshire add to the wealth. The bold, friction-dependent grit bouldering has its own distinctive character and a passionate following.

## The Ethic and the Weather

Two things define British climbing: the trad ethic and the weather. The tradition of climbing ground-up, placing your own protection, and leaving the rock as you found it is deeply held, and routes are graded with this bold style in mind. The maritime weather is a constant adversary, with rain and damp frequent, so British climbers become expert at reading forecasts and seizing dry windows. This shapes the entire experience of climbing here.
