Every climbing road trip begins the same way: a pile of gear on the living-room floor that looks far too big for the car, and a nagging feeling that something essential is still in the cellar. Whether you are heading to Fontainebleau for a week of bouldering, driving the long road to Kalymnos via the ferry from Kos, or stringing together a month of limestone from Siurana to Finale Ligure, the difference between a smooth trip and a frustrating one is rarely fitness — it is packing. A forgotten belay device can be replaced in Arco or Chamonix within an hour; a forgotten prescription, a missing adapter for the stove, or a rope that turns out to be 60 m at a 75 m crag can cost you days. This guide breaks the problem into systems, because systems are checkable and piles are not.

## Start with the venue, not the gear

The single biggest packing mistake is packing for climbing in general instead of for the crags you will actually visit. Before anything goes in a duffel, answer four questions. How long are the routes? Kalymnos, Céüse and the Verdon gorge regularly demand an 80 m rope, while most of Frankenjura is comfortable on 60 m. What is the style? A sport trip to Catalonia needs 16 to 18 quickdraws and little else; a trad week at Cadarese or in the Peak District needs a double rack of cams and a full set of nuts. What is the rock and season? Granite at altitude in Chamonix in June means a belay jacket even when the valley bakes; tufa caves on Kalymnos in October mean chalk, a brush and very little clothing. And finally: is there a gear shop nearby? Arco, Finale Ligure and Innsbruck have excellent shops; remote areas of Sardinia or northern Norway do not. Pack redundancy in proportion to remoteness.

## The rack: count it, then count it again

For a standard European sport trip the baseline is one 70 or 80 m single rope, 16 quickdraws plus two spares, two or three lockers, a belay device with guide mode, a 120 cm sling per person and a personal anchor solution. Write the numbers down. Counting quickdraws onto a sling in groups of five takes thirty seconds and catches the three draws still hanging in the garage. For trad, lay the rack out in size order — nuts 1 to 11, cams from finger size to fist — and photograph it; the photo doubles as an insurance record and a re-packing reference at the end of the trip. Multi-pitch objectives add a second half rope or a tagline, a small rack of maillons for retreats, and one emergency kit per party: knife, prusik cords, a few metres of cord, headtorch and a foil blanket. Helmets are non-negotiable on anything with a second pitch.

## Ropes wear out faster on the road

A four-week trip can put more pitches on a rope than a whole season at the home crag. Bring a rope bag or tarp — Catalan dust and Greek goat paths are brutally abrasive — and check the sheath every few days, especially the first five metres at each end. Many climbers on long trips carry an older second rope as a working-route rope and save the new one for redpoint attempts. Mark the middle clearly, know your rope length before every single lower-off, and tie a stopper knot in the free end as an unbreakable habit. Lowering accidents at unfamiliar crags with longer-than-expected pitches remain one of the most common serious incidents on climbing trips.

## Camp kitchen and the food system

Eating well is a performance issue on a multi-day trip, not a luxury. A two-burner stove transforms van life, but even a single gas canister stove works if you plan around one-pot meals: pasta with lentils, couscous with tinned fish, rice with vegetables and eggs. Carry a 10 to 20 litre water container — many southern European crags, including much of Siurana and Margalef, have no water at the parking — and refill at fuel stations and cemeteries (most French and Italian cemeteries have potable taps; check signage). Pack a small spice kit, olive oil in a screw-top bottle, and a proper sponge and biodegradable soap. The classic mistake is packing climbing food for day one only; budget roughly 3000 to 4000 calories per climbing day and shop every two or three days rather than hauling a fortnight of food across borders.

## Clothing: layers, not outfits

Resist packing by day and pack by layer instead. For a shoulder-season trip in October — prime time in Spain, Greece and southern France — the working set is two climbing T-shirts, one long-sleeve, climbing trousers plus shorts, a fleece or light insulated mid-layer, a belay jacket, a rain shell, a warm hat and a buff. Add camp shoes, two pairs of approach-shoe socks and underwear for five days, then plan to wash. Laundrettes exist in every sizeable town from Llíria to Leonidio, and a 5-litre dry bag with water and soap makes a serviceable washing machine on rest days. Two pairs of climbing shoes — one comfortable, one precise — outperform any single pair over a long trip, and a resole partway through is cheaper than a new pair.

## Repair, first aid and paperwork

A compact repair kit earns its place a hundred times over: finger tape (climbing's duct tape), actual duct tape wrapped around a lighter, a sewing kit, seam grip for ripped down jackets, spare stove parts, cable ties and a multitool. The first-aid kit should be skin-focused — climbing trips are won and lost on skin — with tape, a small file or sanding block, antiseptic, blister plasters, ibuprofen and any personal medication in original packaging. Paperwork lives in one zip pouch: passports or ID cards, driving licence, vehicle documents, insurance details including alpine rescue cover, and printed copies of campsite or refuge bookings. The European emergency number 112 works in every EU country; save local rescue numbers for destinations such as Greece or Turkey before you leave Wi-Fi.

## The vehicle is gear too

If the trip runs on four wheels, the car gets its own checklist. Check oil, coolant, tyre pressures and the spare before departure; carry warning triangles and high-visibility vests, which are legally required in most of Europe; and confirm vignette or toll requirements for Austria, Switzerland and Slovenia in advance. Inside, the golden rule is one box per system: a kitchen box, a food box, a gear duffel per climber, a clothing bag per climber, and a "daily" box with sunscreen, headtorches, wallet and snacks that always rides within reach. Crates beat loose bags because they stack, and because re-packing at 7 a.m. in the rain is when loose systems collapse. Keep the rack accessible without unloading the bed — you will be glad of it at every roadside crag.

## Checklists that actually work

The master checklist lives on your phone and gets edited at the end of every trip, while the lessons are fresh: add the item you missed, delete the three things you never touched. Structure it by system — rack, ropes, clothing, kitchen, sleep, repair, documents, vehicle — and do the final check by physically touching each item, not by memory. Two more habits pay off. First, weigh nothing but doubt everything: if you cannot name the day you will use an item, it stays home, with the exception of the repair and first-aid kits. Second, do a "reverse checklist" when leaving each crag and each campsite: rope, draws, shoes, brush, phone, trash. The European crag-side lost-property economy is built entirely on climbers who skipped that last sweep.
