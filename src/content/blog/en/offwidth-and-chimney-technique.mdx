There is a width of crack that humbles even strong climbers: too wide for a fist jam, too narrow to fit your whole body inside. This is the offwidth, the most reviled and revered size in climbing. There are no holds, no obvious method, just the grim physics of wedging an arm, a leg, a hip, or a shoulder into the slot and inching upward by friction and stubbornness. Grow it wider and the crack becomes a chimney, where you can fit your whole body and bridge between the walls. Both demand a craft utterly unlike face climbing, and both are gloriously old-school. ## Defining the Offwidth

An offwidth is any crack too wide to jam with hands or fists and too narrow to chimney — roughly the width of an arm or a leg. The classic offwidths of Vedauwoo in Wyoming, Yosemite's notorious wide cracks, and the desert towers of Utah have broken the spirits of generations of climbers. Because no single body part fits securely, you must stack and cam multiple parts at once, creating opposing pressure that holds you in place. It is strenuous, awkward, often painful, and uniquely satisfying when you finally unlock the rhythm.

## Arm Bars and Chicken Wings

The fundamental offwidth move is the arm bar: you insert one arm deep into the crack and press the palm against the far wall while the shoulder and tricep press against the near wall, creating a bar of opposing pressure. A variation, the chicken wing, bends the arm so the elbow and hand press against opposite walls — more secure in wider cracks. These positions are not holds you pull on but locks you push against, and learning to weight them and trust the friction is the first breakthrough every offwidth climber must make.

## Heel-Toe and Leg Locks

The legs do as much work as the arms in an offwidth. The heel-toe jam wedges the foot across the crack, heel against one wall and toes against the other, creating a stable platform to stand on. Higher up, you can cam a knee or calf against the walls or stack the legs to hold position. Coordinating an arm bar above with a heel-toe below, then shuffling each upward in turn, is the basic offwidth sequence. It is slow, methodical work, and rushing it is the fastest way to slide back down.

## Which Side In

A crucial early decision is which side of your body to put into the crack — left or right — because reversing it mid-pitch is nearly impossible. The choice depends on the angle of the crack, any lean or flare, and where the holds outside the crack might help. Committing to the wrong side can leave you fighting the rock inefficiently for an entire pitch. Experienced offwidth climbers read the feature from the ground and pick their side before they ever leave the deck.

## Chimney Technique

When the crack widens enough to fit your whole body, it becomes a chimney, and the technique shifts to bridging. You press your back against one wall and your feet or knees against the other, creating enough friction to hold position, then shuffle upward by alternating pressure between back and feet. Wider chimneys require stemming with hands and feet on opposite walls. The great chimneys of the Dolomites and the classic squeeze chimneys of Yosemite reward a calm, methodical approach far more than raw power.

## Protecting Wide Cracks

Protecting an offwidth is part of what makes it intimidating. Standard cams top out around fist size, so wide cracks demand big cams — the large sizes that are heavy and expensive — and historically climbers used bulky tube chocks or even stacked gear. Runouts above the last piece are common because placements are sparse and the big cams are limited. This combination of physical difficulty and serious protection challenges is why offwidth climbing retains a fearsome reputation and a devoted, slightly masochistic following.

## Learning to Love the Wide

Few climbers are born loving offwidths; most come to them reluctantly and then, perversely, grow to relish the struggle. The key is mileage on moderate wide cracks, learning the locks and the rhythm without the added stress of hard climbing. Vedauwoo, with its abundance of wide classics, is a finishing school for the discipline. Once the techniques click and the fear of slipping fades, offwidth climbing reveals itself as a deeply physical, problem-solving puzzle — and a skill that opens up some of the most striking lines in the world.
