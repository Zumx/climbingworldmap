Watch a beginner reach off two right-side holds and you can predict the next second: the body swings away from the wall like a gate on hinges, the left hand claws at air, and gravity wins. Climbers call it the barn door, and it is the most foreseeable fall in the sport. The cure costs nothing and weighs nothing — a leg extended into space, pressed against the rock or simply hung as a counterweight. Flagging, and the body tension that powers it, is how good climbers stay glued to steep walls while making moves that look like they should swing them off. It is the quiet technique that separates flailing from flowing.

## The Physics of the Barn Door

A climbing body rotates around the line between its points of contact. When your right hand and right foot hold the rock and your left side reaches, your centre of gravity hangs outside that line, and torque does the rest: you swing. The door opens fastest on steep terrain, where gravity pulls the hips away from the wall, and on sidepulls and layaways, where the holds themselves push you sideways. The solution is not stronger fingers but better geometry — moving mass to the far side of the pivot line until the forces cancel. That counterweight is almost always a leg, and using it deliberately is flagging.

## The Side Flag

The simplest flag extends the free leg out to the side, toe smearing against the wall or just hovering, weight shifted so the leg balances the reaching arm. Use it when your hand and foot are on the same side and the next hold is up and across: the extended leg moves your centre of gravity back inside the pivot line, and the reach that was a swing becomes a controlled extension. The flagging foot does not need a hold — pressing flat rubber against blank rock adds friction and stability, and even a leg held in space does its counterweight job. Think of it as a tightrope walker's pole, attached at the hip.

## The Back Flag and Inside Flag

Two refinements handle the cases the side flag cannot. The back flag sweeps the free leg behind the standing leg, crossing it into space on the far side — beautiful when your weighted foot is on the same side as the next reach and switching feet would waste energy. It twists the hips into the wall, lengthens the reach, and looks effortless precisely because it removes the swing. The inside flag passes the free leg between the body and the wall instead, useful midway through a sequence when there is no time or room to swap feet. Both flags trade a foot swap for a balance solution, and recognizing which one a move wants becomes instinct with practice.

## Body Tension: The Hidden Engine

Flagging positions the mass; body tension makes the position hold. On steep ground, the force that keeps your feet on footholds travels through a chain — fingers, shoulders, trunk, hips, toes — and the chain is only as stiff as its weakest link. Let the core relax and the hips sag away from the wall, and your feet pop off holds they were standing on perfectly. Tension is most obvious on roofs and overhangs, where climbers actively pull with their toes and brace the trunk like a plank, but it is present in every flag: the extended leg only counterbalances if the body connecting it to the handholds is rigid enough to transmit the force.

## Reading When to Flag

Flagging is not always right — sometimes the move wants a foot swap, a high step or a drop knee instead. The signals for a flag are specific: two contact points on the same side, a reach away from them, and no useful foothold for the free leg, or no time to use one. Sidepulls almost beg for it, since the sideways pull must be opposed by something. On vertical terrain flags tend to be subtle shifts; on steep ground they become full-body positions. The skill is diagnostic: before each move, ask where your centre of gravity must be for the reach to be static, and choose the leg position that puts it there.

## Training the Tension

Core work for climbing is about stiffness under load, not sit-up counts. Planks and side planks build the basic brace; hanging knee raises and toes-to-bar connect the chain from fingers to feet; front lever progressions are the gold standard for the straight-arm tension steep climbing demands. On the wall, steep board climbing — at home on a kicker-and-overhang woody or any training board — forces tension on every move, which is why board sessions transfer so directly to rock. Climbing with deliberately bad, small footholds on an overhang teaches the toes to pull and the trunk to hold the line.

## Drills That Build the Habit

Make flagging conscious until it becomes unconscious. Spend a session climbing easy steep routes where you may not swap feet — every same-side situation must be solved with a flag. Try the one-arm hover: before each reach, take the next hold, then hover your hand a centimetre off it for two seconds; if you swing, your flag was missing or your tension soft. Climb slow on purpose, feeling where the free leg wants to go. Film yourself: barn doors you never noticed in the moment are glaring on video. A few weeks of deliberate practice and the leg starts moving to the right place before you think about it.
