In traditional climbing, the anchor is everything. At the top of a pitch, on a multi-pitch belay, or when setting up a top rope, the climber builds an anchor from their own gear and the natural features of the rock — and that anchor must hold the entire weight of the team in any direction. A solid anchor is invisible insurance; a poor one is a hidden catastrophe waiting to happen. Building anchors is a fundamental and serious trad skill, governed by clear principles. This guide introduces those principles, though it cannot replace hands-on instruction. Explore trad climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Why Anchors Are So Critical

An anchor is the single point on which the safety of a roped team can depend entirely. On a multi-pitch route, the belayer's anchor must hold a fall from the leader above or catch the second climbing below. At the top of a single pitch, the anchor holds a top-roping climber's repeated falls. If an anchor fails, there is nothing else, so it must be built to a far higher standard than any single piece of protection on the route below.

## The Principles: Solid, Redundant, Equalised

Anchor building is guided by a set of principles often summarised in acronyms like SRENE or ERNEST. The core ideas are: the anchor should be Solid, built on bombproof placements in good rock; Redundant, with multiple independent points so that no single failure is catastrophic; Equalised, so the load is shared between the points; and have No Extension, so that if one point fails the others are not shock-loaded. A good anchor satisfies all of these, leaving no single point of failure.

## Choosing the Placements

A trad anchor is built from individual gear placements — cams, nuts, and slings around natural features like blocks, threads, and trees — each of which must be solid in its own right. The art is in finding two, three, or more genuinely good placements close together, in solid rock, oriented to take the expected direction of load. The anchor is only as strong as its placements, so the same judgement that protects the climbing below applies, with even less margin for error.

## Equalising the Anchor

Once solid placements are found, they are connected and equalised so the load is distributed between them, usually with slings or the rope, tied to bring the load to a single master point. The master point should be oriented to take the direction the load will pull, whether holding a fall from above or a second from below. Equalisation ensures no single piece bears the whole load, and a well-tied anchor keeps the system simple, strong, and easy to check.

## Using Natural Features

Natural features often make excellent anchor points: a solid tree, a large immovable block, a thread where the rope passes behind a rock bridge, or a horn over which a sling can be draped. These can be stronger and simpler than gear placements, but they demand judgement — a tree must be alive and well-rooted, a block truly immovable, a thread solid. Combining natural and placed protection, when each is sound, builds the most reliable anchors.

## Learning to Build Anchors

Anchor building is a serious skill that must be learned properly, ideally from a qualified instructor or experienced mentor, and practised on the ground before being trusted with weight. Building anchors badly is one of the most dangerous mistakes in climbing, and the consequences of failure are severe. Take a course, practise the systems until they are second nature, and always check your anchor critically before trusting it. The principles are simple; applying them well takes experience and care.
