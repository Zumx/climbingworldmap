The campus board is one of climbing's most iconic and most demanding training tools: a steeply overhanging board fitted with horizontal rungs, climbed using only the hands, with the feet hanging free. It builds the explosive contact strength and power that let climbers latch holds dynamically and pull through hard, snatchy moves. Invented by one of the sport's legends, it is also one of the riskiest pieces of training equipment, reserved for strong, experienced climbers. This guide explains what the campus board does, the main exercises, and the cautions that come with it. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Origins of the Campus Board

The campus board was devised by Wolfgang Güllich, the German climber who established the world's first 9a, Action Directe, in the Frankenjura in 1991. He built the board to train the explosive one-arm contact strength that route demanded, training in the campus room of a university sports centre — which gave the tool its name. Action Directe famously requires powerful moves between mono-finger pockets, and the campus board was Güllich's answer to building that specific power.

## What It Trains

The campus board develops contact strength — the ability to grip a hold instantly and forcefully at the moment of contact — and explosive pulling power, especially the recruitment of fast-twitch muscle and the coordination to move dynamically between holds. Because the feet hang free, the arms and fingers bear everything, and the dynamic movement between rungs trains power in a way static hangboarding cannot. It is a tool for building the snappy, explosive strength of hard bouldering.

## Basic Exercises

The classic campus exercise is the ladder: moving up the rungs hand over hand, then matching or moving back down, building basic campus fitness. More advanced is the "1-5-9" and similar bumping sequences, where the climber moves explosively between distant rungs, often catching with one hand. Touches and max reaches train the deadpoint catch. These exercises are demanding and should be approached progressively, starting with controlled laddering before any explosive throwing.

## Why It Is High-Risk

The campus board is notorious for causing injury because it loads the fingers, elbows, and shoulders with explosive, dynamic force and no help from the feet. The fingers take huge contact loads, and the elbows are prone to tendon strain from the repeated hard pulling. Dynamic moves can shock-load a finger and rupture a pulley in an instant. This is why the campus board is considered an advanced tool, unsuitable for climbers who have not built years of finger and tendon resilience.

## Who Should Use It

Campus training is appropriate only for strong, experienced climbers with a solid base of finger strength and a history of injury-free training — generally climbers operating at a reasonably high level with several years of consistent climbing. Beginners and intermediates gain far more from climbing and conservative hangboarding, and risk serious injury on the campus board. Even for those ready, it should be a small, carefully managed part of training, not a centrepiece.

## Using It Wisely

If campus training is appropriate, it must be done with great care: a thorough warm-up, controlled movement before explosive movement, low volume, and full recovery between sessions and between hard efforts. Stop immediately at any twinge in the fingers or elbows. Many strong climbers reach a high level without ever touching a campus board, building power through hard bouldering instead. When used, it is a precise tool for a specific gap, not a measure of toughness.
