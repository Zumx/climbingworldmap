Projecting is how climbers send routes that are far beyond their onsight ability: by working the climb over many attempts, rehearsing every move, refining the sequence, and gradually linking it until a clean ascent comes together. It is a discipline of patience, problem-solving, and self-management as much as physical strength. A well-managed project is a deeply rewarding process; a badly managed one is a frustrating grind. This guide explains the tactics — breaking the route into sections, dialling in beta, managing rests and attempts — that turn an impossible-seeming line into a redpoint. ## Choosing a Project

A good project is hard enough to demand real effort but realistic enough to be achievable in the time and conditions you have. Choosing a route at the right level — challenging but not hopeless — is the first tactical decision, because a project that is years beyond you will frustrate, while one too easy will not teach you much. Consider the style too: a project that suits your strengths is more achievable, while one that targets your weaknesses, though harder, will make you a better climber.

## Working the Moves

The first phase of projecting is figuring out how to do each move. The climber goes bolt to bolt, hanging on the rope to rest and experiment, trying different hand and foot sequences until each section has a solution that works reliably. This is detective work: small changes in body position, a different foothold, or a subtle shift in timing can transform a desperate move into a manageable one. Time spent finding the most efficient beta pays off across every future attempt.

## Refining the Beta

Once the moves are possible, the work shifts to making them efficient and repeatable. The climber refines the exact sequence, body positions, and clipping stances, eliminating wasted movement and finding the easiest way through each section. Good beta turns a thuggy struggle into smooth, energy-saving movement. Refining beta is where many redpoints are won, because saving energy on the lower sections is what leaves enough in the tank for the crux.

## Linking Sections

With the moves dialled, the climber begins linking: climbing longer and longer chains of moves without resting, joining the sections into ever-bigger pieces. A common tactic is to practise the upper crux when fresh, then work on climbing into it from lower down, so the body learns to perform the hard moves while tired. Linking from a high point to the top — and from the ground to the crux — builds toward the full ascent.

## Managing Rest and Attempts

Projecting is physically demanding, and managing recovery is a tactic in itself. Each redpoint attempt is taxing, so climbers rest enough between burns — often twenty minutes or more — to climb fresh, and avoid too many attempts in a session, which leads to sloppy, energy-wasting tries that reinforce failure. Skin, fatigue, and conditions all factor in. Knowing when to commit to a real attempt and when to rest is the difference between sending and grinding.

## The Mental Game

Projecting tests patience and belief as much as fitness. Many attempts will end in failure, often at the same move, and staying motivated and positive through the inevitable setbacks is essential. The best projectors treat each attempt as information, learning something even from a fall, and trust that consistent, focused work will eventually pay off. When the redpoint finally comes together after days or weeks of effort, the reward is one of climbing's greatest satisfactions.
