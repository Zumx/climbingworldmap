When a climber says they "did" a route, the way they did it matters as much as the grade. Climbing has a precise vocabulary for ascent styles — redpoint, onsight, flash, and others — that describes how much prior knowledge and how many attempts went into a clean ascent. These distinctions are central to how climbers measure achievement and compare themselves, and they explain why the same person can "climb" several different grades depending on the style. This guide defines the main styles and explains why the difference is so meaningful. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## What Counts as a Clean Ascent

A clean ascent in free climbing means climbing the route from bottom to top without falling, resting on the rope, or pulling on gear — using only the natural holds for progress, with the rope and protection there purely for safety. Hanging on the rope, grabbing a quickdraw, or weighting a piece of gear all "break" the ascent. The styles below all describe clean ascents; what differs is how much the climber knew and how many tries it took.

## Onsight

The onsight is the purest and most prized style: climbing a route cleanly on the very first attempt, with no prior knowledge of the moves, no watching others, and no beta. The climber arrives at the base, ties in, and climbs it first go, reading the rock as they go. Because it allows no rehearsal and no information, the onsight is the hardest style and the truest test of all-round climbing ability, demanding fitness, technique, and on-the-spot problem-solving.

## Flash

A flash is also a clean first-attempt ascent, but with prior knowledge: the climber has watched someone else climb it, received detailed beta, or studied the moves beforehand. Like the onsight, it must be done first try without falling, but the information makes it easier. The flash sits just below the onsight in prestige, rewarding the ability to execute known sequences perfectly on the first attempt.

## Redpoint

A redpoint is a clean ascent after prior practice: the climber has tried the route before, rehearsed the moves, perhaps fallen many times, and finally links it from bottom to top without falling or resting. The term comes from the German Rotpunkt, coined in the Frankenjura, where climbers painted a red dot at the base of routes climbed in this style. The redpoint allows unlimited practice, so it is the style in which climbers achieve their hardest grades.

## Why the Distinction Matters

The style transforms the meaning of a grade. A climber's redpoint grade — what they can do after practice — is typically several letter-grades above their onsight grade — what they can do first try, blind. Saying you "climb 7c" usually means redpoint; your onsight might be 7a. Being clear about style prevents confusion and lets climbers compare like with like. It also shapes goals: some climbers chase hard redpoints, others prize the adventure and purity of the onsight.

## Other Terms

The vocabulary extends further. A "pinkpoint," now largely merged into redpoint, originally meant leading with the quickdraws pre-placed. "Headpoint" describes practising a dangerous trad route on top-rope before leading it cleanly. "Greenpoint" means leading a bolted route on trad gear. These terms reflect climbing's deep concern with how a route is climbed, not just whether it is — a culture of honesty about style that defines the sport.
