Half Dome is the unmistakable silhouette of Yosemite, a granite dome cleaved as if by a giant axe, its northwest face dropping six hundred metres in a sheer sweep of pale stone. The Regular Northwest Face route, first climbed in 1957 by Royal Robbins, Jerry Gallwas, and Mike Sherrick, was the first grade VI big-wall ascent in the United States and remains one of the most coveted classics in Yosemite. It is a route that combines history, scale, and superb granite climbing into a single unforgettable day or two on the wall.

## The Dome Above the Valley

Half Dome rises at the eastern end of Yosemite Valley, its rounded back sloping gently up to a summit that drops away on the northwest into a vertical wall. The famous cables route allows hikers to reach the top from behind, but the face itself is the realm of climbers. From the valley floor, the wall looks impossibly smooth, a great curtain of granite catching the afternoon light. The approach to its base is a steep hike through forest and talus, and the wall reveals its cracks and corners only as you draw near.

## A Landmark in Climbing History

The first ascent of the Regular Route in 1957 was a watershed moment. Over five days, Robbins and his partners pushed up the face using the techniques that would define American big-wall climbing, fixing ropes, hauling, and sleeping on ledges. It was the first time a wall of this scale and steepness had been climbed in the United States, and it announced the arrival of Yosemite as the centre of world big-wall climbing. The route's history gives every ascent a sense of connection to the pioneers who first dared the face.

## The Climbing on the Face

The Regular Route follows a system of cracks and corners up the northwest face, roughly twenty-three pitches of varied granite climbing. It is most commonly climbed today as a mix of free and aid, with sections that can be freed at a high standard and others where aid speeds progress. The climbing includes classic Yosemite cracks, technical face sections, and the famous Robbins Traverse. The Zig-Zags, a series of linked corners high on the wall, are among the most memorable pitches, leading toward Thank God Ledge and the final headwall.

## Thank God Ledge and the Summit

Near the top of the wall, Thank God Ledge offers one of the most photographed positions in climbing: a narrow shelf that the climber shuffles across, heels over six hundred metres of air. It is a moment of pure exposure that distils the experience of the whole route. Above it, the final pitches lead to the summit visor, where climbers top out onto the broad, gently sloping dome. The contrast between the vertical face and the easy summit slabs, with their sweeping views over Yosemite, is part of what makes the route so satisfying.

## Free Climbing the Face

While most parties climb the Regular Route with some aid, it has also been freed at a very high standard, and free ascents are a serious objective for elite climbers. The free version demands sustained hard crack and face climbing over the full height of the wall, and only a small number of climbers complete it free in a day. Whether climbed free or with aid, the route rewards efficiency, route-finding, and the stamina to keep moving through a long, committing day on the granite.

## Logistics, Permits, and Hazards

Half Dome sits within Yosemite National Park, and climbers must be aware of park regulations, water, and the very real hazard of afternoon thunderstorms, which can strike the exposed face with little warning. The northwest face holds shade in the morning and catches sun later, and parties typically start before dawn to make the most of the day. Many climb the route in a single long day, descending the cables route on the far side, while others bivouac on the wall. Rockfall, heat, and the long descent all demand respect.

## When to Climb and How to Prepare

The prime season runs from late spring through autumn, when the face is dry and the days are long, though summer afternoons bring the storm risk. Preparation means mastering Yosemite crack technique, efficient aid and hauling if needed, and the fitness to sustain a long day at altitude. Many climbers serve an apprenticeship on shorter Yosemite walls before attempting Half Dome, and the route rewards that experience. It is a climb that sits at the heart of the Yosemite big-wall tradition, accessible to committed climbers yet never trivial.
