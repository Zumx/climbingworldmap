Dry tooling is the practice of climbing bare rock using ice axes and crampons rather than hands and feet. It grew out of mixed climbing, where routes link sections of ice with stretches of rock, and it has evolved into a discipline of its own — a strange, athletic, and sometimes controversial branch of the climbing family. Hooking a pick on a tiny edge and torquing it into a crack lets climbers ascend overhanging rock no ice could ever form on. ## From Mixed Climbing to Dry Tooling

Mixed climbing has always involved crossing rock to reach or link ice, and climbers naturally used their tools on that rock. As they pushed steeper and more featured rock, a discipline emerged where the rock sections became the point rather than the connector. Pure dry tooling involves no ice at all — the climber uses axes and crampons on rock alone, often on steep, bolted routes. It is a logical extension of mixed technique into a year-round sport.

## The Core Techniques

Dry tooling has its own vocabulary of movement. Climbers "hook" the pick over edges and flakes, "torque" or "stein-pull" the tool by jamming it into a crack and levering, and use figure-four and figure-nine moves, hooking a leg over the tool to gain reach on steep ground. The crampon front points stand on tiny edges and smears. It demands grip strength, body tension, and a precise, controlled style, since a tool can skate off a hold without warning.

## Mixed Grades

Mixed routes are graded on the M-scale, running from M1 to M13 and beyond. Roughly, M4–M5 corresponds to vertical climbing with some technical tooling, M6–M8 involves steep and overhanging rock, and M10 and above is severely overhanging, gymnastic terrain. The scale runs parallel to rock and ice grades but reflects the specific demands of tool-and-crampon climbing. Many of the hardest modern mixed routes are largely or entirely dry tooling with perhaps a short ice section.

## Specialised Gear

While dry tooling uses ice-climbing tools and crampons, dedicated practitioners often use specialised equipment: lightweight competition tools with grip rests and ergonomic handles, and crampons with mono points for precision on small holds. On bolted dry-tooling crags, quickdraws protect the climber as in sport climbing. The picks and points take heavy wear from rock, so dedicated dry-tooling gear is often kept separate from the kit used on real ice.

## The Ethics Debate

Dry tooling is controversial because steel tools and crampons damage rock, scratching holds and breaking edges over time. Many climbing areas prohibit dry tooling on routes that are also rock climbs, to protect the rock for hand-and-foot ascents. Responsible dry tooling is confined to dedicated venues — often quarries, caves, or crags unsuitable for normal climbing — where the impact is accepted. Respecting these boundaries is central to the discipline's place in the wider climbing community.

## Why Climbers Do It

Dry tooling offers a unique athletic challenge and a way to train and climb through the off-season when ice is out of condition. It builds the strength, precision, and tool technique that transfer directly to hard mixed and ice routes in the mountains. For some it is a winter training tool; for others it is a sport in its own right, with competitions and a growing community. Either way, it expands what is climbable far beyond where ice forms.
