The crash pad is the foundation of bouldering safety: a portable foam mattress that cushions the climber's fall to the ground. Since boulderers climb without ropes, the pad — together with spotters and good judgement — is what turns a fall from short, hard problems into a controlled landing rather than an injury. Choosing the right pad and, crucially, using it well are skills as important as the climbing itself. This guide explains how crash pads work, the types available, and how to position and use them for the safest possible landing. Explore bouldering destinations on the [map](/map).

## How a Crash Pad Works

A crash pad absorbs the energy of a fall by compressing under the climber, slowing the body over a short distance and spreading the force. The foam construction is the key: a typical pad layers a thin, firm closed-cell foam on top, which spreads the impact and stops a foot from punching through, over a thicker, softer open-cell foam beneath that absorbs the energy. This combination cushions the landing while preventing the pad from bottoming out on hard ground.

## Types of Crash Pad

Pads come in several styles. Standard folding pads, the most common, fold in half or thirds for carrying and open flat at the boulder. Taco pads fold without a hinge, avoiding a gap but being bulkier; hinge pads fold along a seam, packing flatter but creating a potential weak line. Smaller "satellite" or "highball" pads supplement a main pad to cover awkward landings. The right choice depends on the terrain, how far you carry it, and how many you stack.

## Positioning the Pad

Placing the pad well is as important as having one. The pad should cover the likely landing zone directly beneath the hardest moves and the spot where a fall is most probable, accounting for how the climber will come off and swing. On uneven ground, pads are arranged to cover holes, rocks, and roots, and multiple pads are overlapped to avoid dangerous gaps between them. Reading the fall and laying the pads accordingly is a skill that develops with experience.

## Stacking and Covering Gaps

For highball problems or awkward landings, boulderers stack and arrange multiple pads to build a continuous cushioned surface. The danger is the gaps between pads, where an ankle can roll or a foot can punch through to the ground; overlapping the edges and assigning someone to shift pads as the climber moves keeps the landing safe. The goal is an unbroken, level surface beneath wherever the climber might land.

## Spotting

The crash pad works hand in hand with the spotter. A spotter does not catch a falling climber but guides them onto the pad and protects their head and spine, steering the body to land safely and preventing a backward topple onto rocks. Good spotting means watching the climber, hands up and ready, moving with them, and knowing when to step in. On highballs, spotting and pad management together are what make a serious fall survivable.

## Care and Carrying

A crash pad is a significant investment and a bulky one. Most pads have shoulder straps so they can be carried like a backpack, often stuffed with gear, shoes, and water for the walk in. Keep the foam dry, as moisture degrades it, and store the pad flat or folded as designed rather than crushed. Over years of hard falls the foam compresses and loses its protective qualities, so a well-used pad should eventually be replaced before it stops doing its job.
