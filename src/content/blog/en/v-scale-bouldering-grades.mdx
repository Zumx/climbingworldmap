Walk into almost any climbing gym in the English-speaking world and the boulder problems will wear V-grades: V0, V1, V2, and on up. The V-scale is the dominant bouldering grading system in North America and increasingly worldwide, born in the Texas boulders of Hueco Tanks in the 1980s. It is simple, open-ended, and focused purely on the difficulty of movement over a handful of moves. Understanding it helps you track progress in the gym and read boulder guides across continents. ## Where the V-Scale Came From

The V-scale was created by John "Vermin" Sherman, who developed it while compiling a guidebook to Hueco Tanks in West Texas in the late 1980s. The "V" stands for his nickname, Vermin. Before the V-scale, American bouldering had no consistent system, and Sherman's scale gave climbers a shared language for the explosively growing discipline. It spread from Hueco across the United States and then internationally, becoming the default in gyms worldwide.

## How the Numbers Work

The V-scale is a single open-ended sequence of whole numbers: V0, V1, V2, and upward with no theoretical ceiling. Below V0 you may see VB ("V-Basic" or "V-beginner") for the easiest problems. There are no letter sub-grades as in the French system, though climbers informally speak of a problem feeling "soft" or "hard" for its grade. As of the late 2020s the hardest confirmed problems sit at V17, a level reached by only a handful of climbers.

## Where the Grades Land

A first-time boulderer typically manages VB to V1; V2 and V3 mark steady early progress. Climbing V4 and V5 represents solid intermediate ability, the level many committed gym climbers reach. V6 to V8 is strong amateur territory, and V9 and above marks advanced and expert climbing. The very top — V15, V16, V17 — belongs to a tiny elite; problems such as Nalle Hukkataival's Burden of Dreams in Finland defined the cutting edge of the grade.

## Bouldering Grades Concentrate Difficulty

A key feature of bouldering grades is that they pack difficulty into very few moves. A boulder problem might be only three to eight moves long, so its grade reflects intense, concentrated difficulty rather than the sustained effort of a long route. This is why a V5 boulderer is not necessarily a French 6c+ route climber and vice versa — the demands differ. Power, finger strength, and explosive movement matter far more in bouldering than the aerobic endurance that long routes reward.

## Comparing the V-Scale to Font

The V-scale's main rival is the Fontainebleau scale used across Europe. Rough equivalences: V0 is near Font 4–5, V1 near 5+, V3 near 6A+, V5 near 6C+, V7 near 7A+, V10 near 7C+, and V13 near 8B. The conversions are approximate and hotly debated, partly because Font grades are often considered to reward technique and subtlety while V-grades can reward raw power. Travellers learn to treat the first sessions in a new area as calibration.

## Highball and Danger

The V-scale measures difficulty of movement, not danger. A "highball" — a tall boulder where a fall from the top would be serious — carries the same number whether it is one metre or eight metres tall. Boulderers manage this risk with stacks of crash pads and spotters, but the grade alone will not warn you. Reading a problem's height and landing is a separate judgement that every boulderer must make before committing.
