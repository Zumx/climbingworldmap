Ask a climber how their last six weeks of training went and you will usually get an impression: felt strong, felt weak, climbed a bit. Ask a climber who keeps a log and you get an answer: fourteen sessions, two finger-strength benchmarks improved, endurance untouched since March, left shoulder grumbling after roof sessions. The difference between impressions and answers is the difference between hoping for progress and engineering it. A training log is the cheapest coaching tool in existence — a notebook or an app and three minutes a session — and it routinely uncovers truths about your climbing that no amount of trying harder will reveal.

## Why Memory Fails and Logs Do Not

Human memory is a terrible training partner. It overweights last week, forgets the easy mileage that built a base, remembers the glorious send but not the six sessions of conditions that preceded it. Worse, it bends to mood: on a bad day, the whole season feels wasted. A log replaces this fog with a record. Trends become visible — the volume that actually preceded your best climbing, the warning signs that preceded your last tweaky elbow, the embarrassing truth that you have bouldered three times as often as you trained endurance while complaining about your endurance. Self-knowledge is the log's real product; the numbers are just its alphabet.

## What to Record

The useful minimum is small. Date, venue, session type, roughly what you did, how hard it felt, and anything notable — a new personal best, a tweak, terrible sleep. For projects, record attempts and high points; nothing motivates like watching a high point creep upward. Add periodic benchmarks: a hangboard test at fixed edge size and added weight, a max boulder circuit, a route grade climbed in a session. Some climbers also track sleep, body weight or stress, which often explains mysteriously bad days. The cardinal rule is sustainability: a log you fill in for years at three lines a session beats a beautiful spreadsheet abandoned in February.

## Paper, Apps and Spreadsheets

The medium matters less than the habit, but each has a character. A paper notebook is frictionless and private, and many coaches still prefer it for the freedom of its margins. Spreadsheets excel at benchmarks and graphs — finger strength over a year is a revelation as a chart. Climbing apps and platforms like 8a.nu or Vertical-Life log ascents with grades and dates and add a social layer, though they capture sends better than training. Many climbers settle on a hybrid: an app for the tick list, a notebook or sheet for sessions and benchmarks. Choose whatever you will still be using in two years.

## Reading the Trends

A log pays its rent when you review it, and the review needs scheduled attention — monthly is a good rhythm. Look for ratios first: how much bouldering against endurance, how much climbing against supplemental training, how many rest days against hard days. Look for trajectories: are benchmarks moving over months, or has training become a plateau ritual? Look for correlations: what did the weeks before your best performances have in common, and what preceded each injury or stale patch? Most climbers who do this discover one or two glaring imbalances within the first quarter — usually too much of what they enjoy and too little of what they avoid.

## From Data to Decisions

The log earns its keep when readings become changes. If benchmarks stall for two months, the training stimulus needs to change — more intensity, different exercises, or more rest, and the log usually hints which. If every tweaky elbow follows a spike in volume, cap the spikes. If your project attempts always collapse at the same point, the problem is specific and trainable: endurance at that intensity, or a rehearsable sequence. Set one or two measurable goals per cycle — a benchmark number, a grade in a style you avoid — and let the log referee. The loop of record, review, adjust is the entire science of self-coaching.

## Honest Logging and Its Pitfalls

Logs have failure modes. Grade chasing is the classic: when the logbook becomes a trophy cabinet, climbers quietly avoid hard-for-them styles and flattering numbers replace useful truth. Log failures and abandoned attempts as faithfully as sends — they carry more information. Overlogging is the opposite trap: tracking twenty variables daily collapses under its own weight within weeks. And beware the log becoming the goal; data serves climbing, not the reverse. The healthiest practitioners treat the log like brushing their teeth — a small, automatic act of maintenance, reviewed with curiosity rather than judgement.

## The Long Game

The deepest value of a log emerges after years. It becomes the memory of your climbing life: the season everything clicked, the injury you rehabbed and what actually worked, the unrepeatable trip ticked route by route. Veterans who have logged for a decade can answer questions most climbers cannot — how long their peaks last, how much detraining a month off really costs, what their personal early-warning signs of overuse look like. That accumulated self-knowledge compounds like interest, and it is why many lifelong climbers name the humble logbook, not the hangboard, as the training tool they would keep if allowed only one.
