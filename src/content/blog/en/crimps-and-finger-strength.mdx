Finger strength is the single most distinctive physical demand of rock climbing. No other sport asks the fingers to hold the entire bodyweight on edges a few millimetres deep. The crimp — gripping a small edge with the fingers bent — is the position that makes this possible, and developing the strength and tendons to crimp hard is what unlocks the higher grades. But finger strength is also the source of climbing's most common injuries, so understanding how to grip and how to train is essential. ## What a Crimp Is

A crimp is a way of holding a small edge where the fingers are bent at the middle knuckle and the fingertips pull down on the hold. There are two main variants: the half crimp, where the fingers bend to roughly ninety degrees and the thumb stays off, and the full crimp, where the fingers bend harder and the thumb wraps over the fingernails to add force. The full crimp is the most powerful grip on tiny edges but also the most stressful on the finger tendons and pulleys.

## The Open-Hand Grip

The alternative to crimping is the open-hand grip, where the fingers drape over a hold with little or no bend at the middle knuckle. Open-handing is gentler on the finger pulleys and is the natural grip for slopers and rounded holds. Many coaches encourage climbers to develop a strong open-hand grip both because it is safer and because it builds the kind of tendon strength that transfers well. A complete climber is comfortable in both crimp and open-hand positions.

## How Finger Strength Develops

Finger strength comes from the muscles in the forearm that pull the tendons running to the fingertips, and from the connective tissue — tendons and pulleys — that transmits that force. Muscle strength adapts relatively quickly, but tendons and pulleys strengthen far more slowly. This mismatch is why many climbers can pull harder than their connective tissue can safely handle, and why finger injuries are so common, especially among those who progress quickly or train aggressively.

## The Risk of Injury

The fingers are climbing's most-injured area, and the most common serious injury is a pulley strain or rupture, often in the A2 pulley at the base of the finger. Full crimping puts the highest load on the pulleys, and a sudden slip or dynamic load on a crimped finger can pop a pulley with an audible crack. Warming up thoroughly, avoiding maximal crimping when cold or tired, and respecting pain are the keys to staying healthy.

## Training Fingers Safely

Finger strength is best built gradually and patiently, because tendons need time to adapt. The hangboard is the standard tool: hanging from edges in controlled positions to load the fingers progressively. Beginners should not hangboard at all for the first year or more of climbing, letting strength build naturally through climbing itself. When training does begin, it must be progressive, well warmed up, and balanced with rest, since the connective tissue lags behind the muscle.

## Crimping Smart on the Rock

On real climbs, smart finger use means choosing the right grip for each hold, not crimping everything by default. Open-hand where possible, crimp when necessary, and avoid loading a crimp dynamically or with the body off-balance. Keeping weight on the feet reduces the load on the fingers, and good footwork is the cheapest way to climb harder without overloading the tendons. The strongest fingers in the world still benefit from a body that takes weight off them.
