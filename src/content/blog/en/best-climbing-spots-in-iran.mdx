Iran is one of the great unsung climbing countries. The Alborz range walls off the Caspian Sea behind Tehran, carrying both Damavand — at 5,610 metres the highest volcano in Asia — and the granite big walls of Alam-Kuh. In the west, the Zagros holds Bisotun, a limestone face of staggering scale rising above a UNESCO-listed archaeological site. Iranian climbers are numerous, fiercely capable and famously hospitable, with a mountaineering culture more than a century old and city crags that begin literally at the end of Tehran's metro lines. The places below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=ir).

## Bisotun: The Great Wall of the Zagros

Above the Kermanshah plain in western Iran, the wall of Bisotun rises more than a kilometre over the ancient Royal Road, directly above the famous rock relief and inscription of Darius the Great. For climbers, it is the country's biggest limestone face: long multi-pitch routes, both bolted and traditional, climb tier after tier of compact rock, with new lines still being added by Iranian and visiting teams. The combination is unique — belays with a view over one of the cradles of civilization, and a wall that would be a national landmark in any country on Earth.

## Alam-Kuh and the Takht-e Suleyman Massif

In the central Alborz, the Takht-e Suleyman massif gathers Iran's most alpine terrain around Alam-Kuh, the country's second summit at about 4,850 metres. Its north face is the legend: a granite wall of roughly 800 metres, often compared to the great north faces of the Alps, carrying serious rock and mixed routes first climbed in the mid-twentieth century and still demanding today. Approaches run through the Sardab valley meadows, huts serve the standard routes, and the massif's ridges offer summits in every grade of commitment.

## Damavand: The Roof of Iran

Northeast of Tehran, the snow-streaked cone of Damavand dominates the skyline, a dormant stratovolcano of 5,610 metres steeped in Persian myth. The south route from the Polour hut is a non-technical but genuinely high climb, with sulphur vents near the summit reminding climbers this is a volcano; other flanks offer harder, quieter lines and superb ski mountaineering in spring. Acclimatization matters more than difficulty here, and the summit view — Alborz ridges to the Caspian haze — repays every slow step.

## Tehran's Mountain Doorstep

Few megacities sit this close to real mountains. From northern Tehran, the valleys of Darband and Darakeh funnel hikers and climbers straight into the Alborz foothills, where crags like the Band-e Yakhchal wall and the rocks of the Oson area provide roped climbing within sight of the city's towers, and the 3,964-metre summit of Tochal — served by its own gondola — hosts year-round mountaineering. Weekend mornings see thousands moving uphill, tea houses line the trails, and the city's climbing clubs train on walls scattered across the capital, from the Mega Pars climbing complex to neighbourhood gyms.

## Sport Climbing and the Wider Scene

Bolted sport climbing has spread across the country: the Qaleh Dokhtar climbing site and crags in the Zagros and Alborz foothills carry modern routes, while the limestone around cities like Kermanshah, Shiraz and Mashhad continues to be developed. Indoor climbing is booming, with competition walls and bouldering gyms in most provincial capitals, and Iranian competitors — male and female — increasingly visible internationally. Visiting climbers consistently report the same thing: partners are easy to find, and invitations to climb, eat and stay multiply faster than rest days.

## Culture and Practicalities

An Iranian climbing trip runs on hospitality and paperwork in roughly equal measure. Most nationalities need a visa, some require guides or tours, and regulations shift — check current rules well before booking. In the mountains, dress codes are interpreted practically but should be respected, and women climbers will find a strong female climbing community, especially in Tehran. Costs in-country are low, long-distance buses and domestic flights work well, and a few words of Farsi plus unlimited patience for tea make everything smoother.

## Seasons

Iran's climbing calendar is wide. Damavand and Alam-Kuh are summer peaks, best from mid-June to September, with ski mountaineering from February to May. Bisotun and the Zagros limestone climb best in spring and autumn, while winter brings serious cold and superb snow to the Alborz. Tehran's valley crags work most of the year, with summer afternoons hot and winter mornings icy. Whatever the season, somewhere in the country is in condition.
