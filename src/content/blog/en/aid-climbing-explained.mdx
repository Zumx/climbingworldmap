Aid climbing is the practice of making upward progress by pulling on gear placed in the rock, rather than by gripping holds and climbing free. Where free climbing uses equipment only for protection, aid climbing uses it to support body weight: the climber places a piece, hangs from it, stands up, and places the next. It is slow, technical, and essential to climbing the blankest, hardest big walls, where free climbing is impossible. ## How Aid Climbing Works

In aid climbing, the climber progresses by weighting gear rather than holds. They place a piece of protection — a cam, nut, piton, or specialized aid gear — clip an etrier (a ladder of webbing steps) to it, and step up. From the top of the etrier they place the next piece higher, clip in, and move up again, leapfrogging upward one placement at a time. The rope protects against a placement failing. It is methodical, gear-intensive work, utterly different in rhythm from free climbing, advancing slowly but allowing passage where there are no usable holds.

## The Gear of Aid

Aid climbing uses a large and specialized rack. Beyond standard cams and nuts, aid climbers carry etriers and daisy chains, plus specialized placements: copperheads for shallow seams, hooks that perch on tiny edges, beaks and pitons hammered into cracks, and offset cams and nuts for flaring placements. A big aid pitch may require dozens of placements, each carefully chosen and tested. The rack is heavy and the skills exacting, because an aid climber's progress and safety depend entirely on the gear they place in often marginal rock.

## Aid Grades

Aid climbing has its own grading system, separate from free grades, running from A0 to A5 (or C0 to C5 for "clean" aid using no hammer). A0 is pulling on the occasional piece; A1 is straightforward, solid placements; and the scale climbs through A2, A3, and A4 to A5, where placements are so marginal that long, dangerous falls are likely if one fails. Clean-aid (C) grades describe the same difficulty without pitons or hammering. The grade reflects both difficulty and danger, with high grades demanding nerve as much as technique.

## Clean Aid and Ethics

Historically, aid climbing relied heavily on pitons and copperheads hammered into the rock, which scarred cracks over repeated ascents. Modern ethics favour "clean aid" — using removable gear like cams, nuts, and hooks that leave no trace — wherever possible, reserving hammered gear for routes that require it. The shift to clean aid has preserved many classic cracks from destruction. Responsible aid climbers minimize their impact, choosing clean placements first and treating the rock as a resource to protect for those who follow.

## Aid and Big Walls

Aid climbing is inseparable from big-wall climbing. The great walls — El Capitan in Yosemite, the towers of Patagonia, walls in Baffin and beyond — were first climbed largely by aid, and many of their routes still require it. A big-wall ascent typically mixes free and aid climbing, with the team aiding the blank or hard sections that cannot be freed. Aid skills, combined with hauling, ascending, and portaledge living, make up the integrated craft of multi-day wall climbing, where aid is often the key that unlocks the line.

## Learning to Aid

Aid climbing is a deep, technical discipline learned through apprenticeship and practice, not from text. Beginners start on easy aid pitches, learning to place and test gear, manage etriers and daisy chains, and move efficiently, before progressing to harder and more dangerous grades. The skills overlap with the rest of wall craft, and most aid climbers build toward big walls over years. Demanding, methodical, and historically rich, aid climbing remains the means by which climbers ascend the world's blankest and most committing walls.
