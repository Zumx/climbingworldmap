Climbing gear is life-support equipment, and all of it has a finite safe life. Knowing when to retire a piece of gear — before it fails, not after — is one of the most important safety skills a climber has. Gear degrades through use, age, damage, and exposure, sometimes invisibly, and trusting a worn-out piece can have catastrophic consequences. The good news is that the cost of replacing gear is trivial against the cost of an accident. This guide explains when to retire the main pieces of climbing equipment. ## Why Gear Has a Lifespan

All climbing gear degrades over time, even with perfect care. Textiles — ropes, slings, harnesses — break down through use, UV exposure, abrasion, and simple ageing of the fibres, losing strength gradually and often invisibly. Metal gear wears, develops sharp edges, and can be damaged by impacts. Manufacturers specify maximum lifespans precisely because the materials degrade whether used or not. Retiring gear is not waste but a recognition that life-support equipment has limits, and that those limits matter.

## Ropes

A rope should be retired after a severe fall (a high fall factor), any core damage felt as flat or soft spots, sheath damage exposing the core, contact with chemicals, or visible degradation. Even without these, a rope has a maximum lifespan from first use, and an even shorter one in heavy use — manufacturers give guidance, often a few years for a regularly used rope. A rope that has been stored unused still ages. When a rope fails inspection or reaches its age limit, retire it without hesitation.

## Harnesses

A harness should be retired when the tie-in points, belay loop, or webbing show wear, fraying, fading, or damage, after a major fall, or simply with age. The tie-in points and belay loop take the most wear and should be inspected closely. Like all textiles, a harness degrades with UV, abrasion, and time, with a maximum lifespan even if lightly used. A harness with worn or damaged load-bearing parts is a serious hazard and should be replaced immediately.

## Carabiners and Hardware

Metal gear lasts longer than textiles but is not eternal. Retire a carabiner if it is cracked, deeply grooved or sharp-edged from wear (which can damage the rope), has a gate that does not close or lock properly, or has taken a hard impact or a fall onto a hard surface, which can cause invisible damage. Belay devices, cams, and other hardware should be retired when worn, damaged, or no longer functioning correctly. When metal gear is suspect, retire it rather than risk a hidden flaw.

## Slings, Cord, and Soft Goods

Slings, cord, and other soft goods are textiles and degrade like ropes and harnesses. Retire them after a significant fall, any cut, fray, or abrasion, contact with chemicals, fading from UV, or stiffness indicating degradation, and after their maximum lifespan regardless of use. Soft goods used outdoors, exposed to sun and weather, age faster. Because they are cheap and critical, slings and cord should be replaced readily at the first sign of wear rather than trusted past their prime.

## A Culture of Honest Retirement

The key to gear safety is a culture of honest, proactive retirement: inspecting regularly, knowing the lifespans, and retiring gear before it fails rather than pushing it to destruction. The instinct to get more life from expensive equipment must be overridden by the understanding that this is life-support gear. When in doubt, retire it — no piece of gear is worth a life. Building this discipline into your climbing is as important as any skill on the rock.
