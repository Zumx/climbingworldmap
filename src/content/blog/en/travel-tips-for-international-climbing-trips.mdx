The first time you check a haul bag full of cams onto an international flight, you learn that climbing travel is its own discipline. The rope sails through security in Munich without comment, then gets pulled aside in Atlanta; the chalk bag triggers a swab test in Singapore; and the friendly question of what, exactly, those metal spring-loaded devices are for can go either way depending on the officer. Beyond the airport, every climbing country runs on its own unwritten rules — when to climb, where to park, whom to greet, what never to do — and the climbers who learn them quickly are the ones invited back. This guide collects the practical knowledge that separates a smooth international trip from an expensive lesson.

## Flying with gear: what goes where

The basic split is simple: soft goods and textiles can fly in the cabin, anything that could conceivably be a tool or weapon goes in the hold. Ropes, harnesses, slings, quickdraws, shoes and chalk are all technically cabin-legal in most jurisdictions, but security officers have wide discretion, and cams, nuts, ice screws and crampons belong in checked luggage without exception. Practical approach: pack the rack, helmet and rope in a sturdy duffel inside the hold bag, and carry shoes, harness, chalk bag and one set of climbing clothes in the cabin — if the checked bag goes missing, you can borrow a rope and climb while you wait. Weigh everything at home: a sport rack with an 80 m rope runs 8 to 10 kg before clothing, and a trad or alpine kit easily doubles that. Many climbers fly budget airlines and pay for one heavy checked bag of 25 to 32 kg rather than two standard ones; check the airline's sports-equipment policy, as some count climbing gear under generous special-baggage rules.

## Chalk, stoves and the things that cause trouble

Loose chalk looks exactly like a suspicious powder on a scanner, so keep it in its original labelled packaging or in a transparent bag, and expect a swab. Liquid chalk over 100 ml goes in the hold. Gas canisters can never fly, in cabin or hold — plan to buy fuel on arrival, and check the local standard first: screw-thread canisters dominate Europe and North America, while some destinations mainly stock clip-on or pierceable types. Camping stoves themselves are allowed if scrupulously clean and fuel-free, but a stove that smells of petrol will be confiscated. Knives, including the small one on your multitool, go in the hold. Finally, print or save a gear list with replacement values: it speeds up both customs questions and any insurance claim if the bag disappears between connections.

## Permits, access and red tape

Plenty of the world's best climbing sits behind paperwork. Some classic examples: peaks above 6500 m in Nepal require a permit and, for expedition peaks, a liaison arrangement; climbing in many United States national parks such as Yosemite requires free wilderness permits for overnight walls; the Cirque of the Unclimbables in Canada involves float-plane logistics booked months out; and several Greek and Spanish crags close seasonally for bird nesting, typically from January or February to June or July. None of this is hard to comply with — all of it is hard to fix on arrival. Two weeks before departure, check the local access organisation: the Access Fund in the USA, the BMC's Regional Access Database in Britain, the DAV's felsinfo in Germany, and local climbing federations elsewhere usually publish current closures online. Respecting a raptor closure you find tedious is the price of the crag staying open at all.

## Insurance that actually covers climbing

Standard travel insurance usually excludes climbing, or covers it only below an altitude or grade threshold buried in the policy. Read the activity list, not the headline. Membership organisations are often the best value: the Austrian Alpine Club's worldwide rescue cover is famously cost-effective, the DAV and many national federations offer similar packages, and dedicated providers cover expedition altitudes. The crucial distinctions are rescue cost (a helicopter evacuation in the Alps can exceed several thousand euros, and rescue in parts of the Andes or Himalaya is charged at market rates), medical treatment abroad, and repatriation. In Europe, the 112 number works EU-wide, but rescue is not free everywhere — in much of Austria, Italy and Switzerland, uninsured callouts are billed to the climber. Carry your policy number physically, not just on a phone that may be dead, lost, or shattered at the base of the route.

## Health, food and the unglamorous logistics

International climbing breaks more trips through stomach trouble and dehydration than through injury. In destinations like El Potrero Chico in Mexico, Tonsai in Thailand or rural Morocco around Taghia, drink bottled or treated water, be careful with ice and raw salads for the first days, and pack a basic course of rehydration salts. Check vaccination recommendations early — some need weeks of lead time — and carry prescription medication in original packaging with a doctor's letter for anything controlled. Skin care is climate care: tropical limestone destroys skin through humidity and sharp tufa, desert sandstone through dryness, so adjust your file-tape-balm routine to the venue. And respect rest-day hygiene: most multi-week trip illnesses trace back to the day everyone shared one water bottle at the crag.

## Local etiquette: the unwritten guidebook

Every climbing culture has rules that no topo prints. In Kalymnos and most of Greece, leave fixed draws on hard projects alone and lower off through the rings only when the anchor is built for it. On Czech and Saxon sandstone around Adršpach and the Elbe valley, metal protection and chalk are restricted or banned and climbing after rain is taboo — sandstone absorbs water and breaks. In Fontainebleau, brushing off your shoes before each problem and never climbing wet sandstone are core etiquette, as is leaving no trace of pof or excessive tick marks. In the United States, dogs, music and large groups attract more friction than in southern Europe; in Japan, small crags near villages run on quiet voices and impeccable parking. The universal rules: greet locals, ask before jumping on someone's project, take all trash including tape, use toilets or carry waste out, and buy something in the village shop — access often survives on the goodwill of one farmer or one mayor.

## Money, transport and connectivity

Card payments now work almost everywhere, but crag life still runs on cash in surprising places: refuge fees in the Alps, camping in rural Spain, taxis in Kalymnos, entry fees at some Thai and Mexican crags. Carry a modest cash reserve in local currency from day one. For transport, the rental-car question depends on destination: essential in Catalonia, Sardinia or South Africa, unnecessary on Kalymnos where a scooter rules, and counterproductive in Tonsai where everything moves by longtail boat and on foot. An eSIM bought before departure removes the airport-SIM scramble and matters more than it seems: topos, closures, weather and rescue all live online now. Download offline maps and the relevant guidebook app sectors before leaving Wi-Fi — the best crags are routinely the ones without signal.

## Build in slack, climb the classics

The most common planning error on international trips is scheduling like an optimist: arrival day plus fourteen climbing days plus departure day. Budget one rest or weather day for every two or three climbing days, and treat the first two days as calibration — grades, rock and risk culture differ, and onsight confidence at home does not transfer automatically to polished Verdon limestone or sandbagged British trad. Use the slack for the things that make the trip: ferry to a neighbouring island, a market morning, the long lunch the locals take. And climb the three-star classics even when they are busy; they are the routes you will still describe in detail a decade later.
