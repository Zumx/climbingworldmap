Speed climbing is the sprint of the climbing world: a head-to-head race up a standardised 15-metre wall, decided in seconds. Unlike the puzzle-solving of bouldering or the endurance of sport climbing, speed climbing is pure explosive power and perfected, memorised movement, with the world's fastest athletes reaching the top in around five seconds. Long a discipline of its own, it gained huge visibility as part of climbing's Olympic debut. ## What Speed Climbing Is

Speed climbing is a timed race up a fixed, standardised route, usually contested head-to-head on two identical lanes. Climbers are belayed from above by an auto-belay, allowing them to climb all-out without managing a rope. The discipline rewards explosive power, speed, and the flawless execution of a sequence the climber has rehearsed thousands of times. It is the most athletic and least improvisational of climbing's competitive disciplines, more akin to a sprint than to traditional climbing.

## The Standardised Route

What makes speed climbing unique is its standardised route. Since the IFSC standardised the speed wall, the holds, their positions, and the 15-metre, slightly overhanging wall are identical at every official competition worldwide. This means athletes climb the exact same sequence everywhere, allowing them to memorise and perfect every movement and to compare times and records directly. The standardisation transforms speed climbing into a measurable, record-driven sport like track athletics.

## The Athletes and Records

Speed climbing's elite athletes have driven times down to astonishing levels. The men's world record has dropped under five seconds for the full 15-metre wall, and the women's record continues to fall too, as athletes refine their explosive power and the precise choreography of the route. These times represent extraordinary athleticism, the climbers exploding up the wall in a blur of perfectly memorised movement, every hold hit without hesitation.

## Training for Speed

Speed climbing demands a very different kind of training from other climbing disciplines. Athletes drill the standard route relentlessly, building the explosive power, reaction time, and muscle memory to execute it perfectly at maximum pace. The training resembles that of a sprinter as much as a climber, with a focus on power, speed, and the flawless repetition of a fixed sequence. Specialist speed climbers often focus on this discipline alone, so different are its demands.

## Speed at the Olympics

Speed climbing gained global attention through climbing's Olympic debut. At first it was controversially combined with bouldering and lead into a single combined event, forcing all-rounders to compete in a discipline very different from their strengths. By the following Games, speed was separated into its own medal event, recognising it as a distinct discipline. Its inclusion brought this explosive, spectator-friendly form of climbing to a vast new audience.

## A Distinct Discipline

Speed climbing sits apart from the rest of climbing, more athletics than adventure, more sprint than puzzle. Some traditional climbers debate whether it belongs alongside bouldering and lead, given how different its skills and culture are. Yet its athleticism is undeniable, and as its own Olympic event it has found recognition and a dedicated community. Whatever one's view, speed climbing showcases a remarkable expression of human power and precision on the wall.
