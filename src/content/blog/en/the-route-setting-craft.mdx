Every climb in a gym and every problem in a competition is designed by a route setter, a skilled craftsperson who arranges holds on a blank wall to create movement, challenge, and beauty. Route setting is part art, part engineering, and part choreography, and good setting is what makes indoor climbing engaging, varied, and fun. Behind every great session at the gym is a setter who imagined the moves, chose the holds, and built the route. This guide explores the craft of route setting and the skill it demands. ## What Route Setters Do

A route setter designs and builds the climbs on artificial walls, choosing and arranging holds to create routes and boulder problems of a particular difficulty and style. They work to a brief — a range of grades, a variety of styles, a target audience — and translate it into climbs on the wall. Setting involves imagining a sequence of moves, selecting holds that create it, fixing them to the wall, and then climbing and refining the result until it works as intended.

## The Art of Movement

At its heart, route setting is about creating movement. A great setter does not just place holds; they choreograph a sequence that flows, challenges, and feels satisfying to climb. They think about body position, balance, and the rhythm of the moves, crafting problems that demand specific techniques — a dyno here, a delicate balance move there, a powerful lock-off elsewhere. The best routes feel like a conversation between the setter and the climber, a puzzle designed to be discovered and solved.

## Building Difficulty and Variety

Setters must build a spread of difficulty and a variety of styles to serve all the climbers who will use the wall. A gym needs easy routes for beginners, hard ones for experts, and everything between, in styles from technical and balancy to powerful and dynamic. Achieving the right grade is a craft in itself, as is creating variety so that climbers are challenged in different ways. A well-set gym offers something for everyone and keeps climbers coming back.

## The Tools and Physical Work

Route setting is physical, skilled labour. Setters work on ropes or from lifts, hauling heavy holds, drilling and bolting them to the wall, and climbing the routes repeatedly to test and adjust them. It demands strength, climbing ability, an eye for movement, and the stamina for long days of physical work. Holds come in endless shapes, and choosing and orienting each one to create the intended move is a craft learned over years.

## Competition Setting

Competition route setting is a specialised, high-pressure discipline. Setters must create problems that test the world's best climbers, separate a field of elite athletes, and produce dramatic, watchable competition, all while ensuring the problems are fair and the difficulty is precisely judged. Competition setters are among the most skilled in the craft, designing the boulders and routes that decide World Cups and Olympic medals, and their work shapes the cutting edge of the sport.

## A Craft Worth Appreciating

Route setting is often invisible to the climbers who enjoy its results, but it is a genuine craft that shapes the whole experience of indoor climbing. The next time a problem flows beautifully, challenges you in a clever way, or makes you laugh with a surprising move, a setter imagined and built it. Appreciating the skill behind the setting deepens the enjoyment of every gym session and competition, and reveals the artistry hidden in plain sight on every wall.
