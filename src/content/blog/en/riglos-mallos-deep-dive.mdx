Few crags announce themselves like Riglos. Driving north from Huesca across the wheat plains of Aragon, you see them long before you arrive: a row of rust-red towers, the Mallos de Riglos, bulging nearly 300 metres above a village so small its streets end at the rock itself. The stone is conglomerate — millions of rounded cobbles set in a sandstone matrix like raisins in a cake — and the climbing is like nothing else in Europe: wildly steep, improbably juggy, and shared with one of the largest griffon vulture colonies in the Pyrenean foothills. This is one of Spain's great climbing pilgrimage sites.

## A village beneath the towers

Riglos the village is a handful of stone houses, a church and a refuge pressed against the base of the walls, in the foothills where the plains of the Ebro meet the first waves of the Pyrenees. The towers each carry names and personalities: the massive Mallo Pisón and Mallo Firé anchor the line, the slender spire of El Puro leans against its parent wall, and the great prow of La Visera overhangs the village football pitch. Climbers stumble out of routes directly into the bar, and the soundscape is permanent: jackdaws, wind, and the whump of vulture wings overhead.

## Stone made of cobbles

Conglomerate demands its own technique. The walls are studded with cobbles from pebble to football size, and the climbing is a constant exercise in trust: you pull on rounded bulges, bridge between protruding stones and learn to read which cobbles ring solid and which sound hollow. Polish on the classic lines adds spice lower down, and the steepness means pump management matters more than pure finger strength. Most routes are equipped as long sport climbs, with bolted belays and generally good fixed gear on the trade routes, though older or wilder lines deserve respect and a few slings for cobble threads.

## Fiesta de los Biceps and the great classics

The most famous route in Riglos — and one of the most famous in Spain — is Fiesta de los Bíceps on La Visera: nearly 300 metres of continuously overhanging jug-hauling, where each pitch leans further over the village and the rests come as knee-bars between bulges. Its neighbours on the Pisón and Firé, including time-honoured lines like the normal routes and the airy traverses to El Puro's summit needle, fill out the wish list. Grades on the big classics cluster in the accessible extreme range, but the length, exposure and steepness make every tick feel earned. Summit registers and abseil descents crown the experience.

## Vultures on the wind

Riglos is as much a wildlife spectacle as a crag. The towers host a major colony of griffon vultures, and climbers share the upper pitches with birds whose wingspans approach three metres, swooping past close enough to hear the air tear. Red kites, choughs and the occasional lammergeier join them. The colony is protected, and some sectors carry seasonal nesting restrictions — observe signed closures and give occupied ledges a wide berth. For most visitors, belaying in a sea of circling vultures becomes the trip's defining memory, stranger and better than any photo suggests.

## Seasons and conditions in the foothills

Riglos faces broadly south and bakes in summer, when the conglomerate radiates heat well into the evening; July and August are best avoided. Spring and autumn are prime, with long dry spells, friendly temperatures and stable rock, while winter often serves brilliant sunny windows between cold snaps — many locals climb here all through the cold months. The towers dry almost instantly after rain, but seepage can linger in the steepest caves. Wind is the wildcard: the exposed upper pitches catch every gust, and a calm day on La Visera is a gift to be seized.

## Logistics: getting there and staying

The village lies about 45 minutes by road from Huesca and a little over an hour from Zaragoza, with its own tiny train halt on the Zaragoza–Canfranc line. Accommodation means the village refuge, guesthouses and casas rurales in Riglos and nearby Murillo de Gállego, with camping options along the Gállego river — also Spain's white-water hub, handy for rest days. Take a 70- or 80-metre rope, twenty quickdraws and helmets without debate: cobbles do occasionally pull. The bar at the foot of the towers handles post-route analysis with grilled meat and cold beer.

## The wider Aragon circuit

Riglos anchors a superb circuit. Twenty minutes away, the tower of Peña Sola at Agüero offers quieter conglomerate adventures; an hour east, the canyons of the Sierra de Guara around Rodellar hold world-class limestone sport climbing on tufas above turquoise pools; and the southern walls of the Pyrenees, from Ordesa's giants to roadside crags, fill out a fortnight effortlessly. Many climbers pair Riglos' long routes with Rodellar's steep single pitches, letting forearms and fingers recover in alternation. Aragon remains one of Europe's most underrated climbing regions.
