Every climber eventually meets the same wall: weeks of hard training that suddenly stop paying off. The grades stall, the fingers ache in the mornings, sleep gets ragged, and motivation drains away. This is not a sign to try harder — it is the body asking for a break. The connective tissue of the fingers, the tendons of the elbow, and the nervous system all adapt far more slowly than muscle, and they need scheduled rest to catch up. A deload week, a planned reduction in training load, is the tool that lets the body absorb the work you have done and come back stronger.

## What Overtraining Actually Is

Overtraining is not simply being tired after a session. It is a chronic state in which the cumulative stress of training, climbing, work, and life exceeds the body's capacity to recover. The early stage, often called overreaching, is normal and even useful when followed by rest. The danger comes when the load never relents. Performance declines despite more effort, resting heart rate creeps up, sleep deteriorates, appetite changes, and small injuries refuse to heal. In climbers the warning signs are often local before they are systemic — a finger that stays sore, an elbow that twinges on every crimp, a shoulder that aches at night.

## Why Tendons Lag Behind Muscle

Muscle responds to training within days, but the collagen-rich tissues that climbing loads hardest — finger pulleys, forearm tendons, and the connective tissue around the elbow — turn over far more slowly. This mismatch is the root of most climbing injuries. A climber can build the muscular strength to pull on a tiny crimp long before the pulleys in the fingers are ready to handle that force. Deload weeks exist largely to give these slow tissues a chance to adapt without the constant micro-damage of maximal climbing. Skipping them is how strong climbers tear pulleys.

## Recognising Your Own Fatigue Signals

The most reliable warning system is your own honest attention. Track a few simple markers across a training cycle: how your fingers feel in the first hour after waking, how warmed up you need to feel before hard moves, your enthusiasm for climbing, and the quality of your sleep. When several of these drift in the wrong direction at once, fatigue has accumulated. Many climbers keep a brief training log noting perceived effort and any niggles. Over a few weeks the pattern becomes obvious, and you learn to read the signal before it becomes an injury rather than after.

## Structuring a Deload Week

A deload is a deliberate reduction, not a total stop. A common approach cuts volume by roughly half while keeping some intensity, so the body stays sharp without accumulating fatigue. You might climb two easy sessions instead of four hard ones, drop all hangboard and campus work, and avoid anything maximal on the fingers. Another model keeps a little intensity but slashes the total number of hard moves. Either way the goal is the same: enough stimulus to maintain coordination and strength, far less stress on the tissues that need to recover. Most climbers benefit from a deload every fourth to sixth week.

## Active Recovery and What to Do Instead

A deload week is a chance to do the things hard training crowds out. Easy mileage on big holds keeps movement fluid without taxing the fingers. Mobility work for the shoulders and hips, light antagonist training for the elbows, and gentle aerobic exercise all support recovery while keeping you engaged. Many climbers use the week to climb purely for fun — long moderate routes, slabs, or a relaxed day outdoors at somewhere like Fontainebleau or the Peak District, where the climbing is technical rather than savage. The point is to move, enjoy the sport, and let the deep tissues catch up.

## Periodisation and the Long Game

Deloads make most sense inside a larger plan. Periodisation organises training into blocks — building volume, then intensity, then peaking for a trip or project — with deloads punctuating the transitions. This rhythm of stress and recovery is how the body actually gets stronger; adaptation happens during rest, not during the session. Climbers chasing a specific goal, such as a hard route at Céüse or a boulder project at Magic Wood, often time a deload in the week or two before, arriving fresh rather than fried. Thinking in seasons rather than sessions is the mark of a climber who lasts.

## Building Rest Into Your Identity

The hardest part of deloading is psychological. Driven climbers equate rest with falling behind, and a planned easy week can feel like a betrayal of ambition. Reframing helps: the rest week is when the previous block's training is converted into strength. The climbers who progress for decades are rarely the ones who train hardest in any single month — they are the ones who never get badly injured, because they respect recovery as part of the work. Treating deloads as non-negotiable, like the hard sessions themselves, is what separates a long climbing life from a short, intense, injury-shortened one.
