Women have shaped climbing profoundly, pushing standards, breaking barriers, and redefining what is possible on the rock. From Lynn Hill's historic free ascent of the Nose to the boulderers, sport climbers, and Olympic champions of today, women have repeatedly led the sport forward, sometimes outclimbing their male peers and always expanding the horizons of the discipline. Their achievements are not a footnote to climbing history but central to it. This guide celebrates some of the women who shaped climbing and their lasting influence. ## Lynn Hill and the Nose

Among the most celebrated achievements in climbing history is Lynn Hill's free ascent of the Nose of El Capitan in 1993, and her one-day free ascent the following year. The Nose had been climbed countless times with aid, but freeing it — climbing every move using only the rock — had defeated the world's best, all of them men. Hill's ascent, and her famous remark that it "goes, boys," was a landmark moment that redefined what free climbing could achieve and stands among the sport's greatest feats.

## Pioneers Across the Eras

Women have been part of climbing's leading edge across its history. Early female alpinists challenged the conventions of their day to climb in the great ranges. Through the twentieth century, women established hard routes, made bold ascents, and pushed standards in every discipline. Figures across generations and countries broke barriers both physical and social, often with less recognition than their male peers, yet their ascents drove the sport forward and opened the way for those who followed.

## The Bouldering and Sport Revolution

As bouldering and sport climbing grew, women pushed the hardest grades alongside the men. Climbers established cutting-edge boulder problems and sport routes, repeatedly raising the bar of female performance and, in many cases, climbing grades that few men had touched. The narrowing gap between the hardest male and female ascents in these disciplines reflects how completely women have come to drive climbing's elite, redefining expectations with each new benchmark.

## Competition and the Olympics

Competition climbing has been a stage for extraordinary female athletes, and the sport's Olympic debut brought women's climbing to a global audience. Olympic and World Cup champions have showcased the strength, skill, and athleticism of the world's best female climbers on the biggest stage, inspiring a new generation. The visibility of women's competition climbing has done much to grow the sport and to make clear that climbing is a discipline where women compete and excel at the very highest level.

## Breaking Barriers Beyond the Rock

Beyond their ascents, women have shaped climbing's culture, broadening who climbs and how the sport sees itself. They have founded organisations, led on access and conservation, built communities, and challenged the sport to be more inclusive and welcoming. The growing presence of women at every level of climbing, from the gym to the world's hardest routes, has changed the face of the sport and continues to inspire new climbers to take up the rock.

## A Continuing Legacy

The influence of women on climbing is not history but an ongoing force. Each generation builds on those before, pushing grades, breaking new ground, and inspiring others, and the leading women of today are the pioneers the next generation will follow. From the boldest alpine first ascents to the hardest boulders and the Olympic podium, women continue to shape climbing's present and future as profoundly as its past.
