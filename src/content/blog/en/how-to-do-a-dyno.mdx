The dyno is climbing's most spectacular move: a dynamic leap where the climber launches off the holds, flies through the air, and catches a distant hold beyond static reach. It looks like raw athleticism, and there is plenty of that, but a good dyno is mostly technique and timing — a coordinated explosion of legs, hips, and arms aimed at a precise target. Many climbers fear and avoid dynos, but learning to move dynamically expands what is possible and makes climbing more efficient even on static terrain. This guide breaks down how to launch, catch, and train the dyno. Explore bouldering destinations on the [map](/map).

## What a Dyno Is

A dyno, short for dynamic movement, is any move where the climber generates upward or sideways momentum to reach a hold they cannot reach statically. In a full dyno, both hands and sometimes both feet leave the rock at once, the body flying to a target hold. Smaller dynamic moves, where one hand stays on, are sometimes called "deadpoints." The principle is the same: use momentum, not static pulling, to span a gap.

## The Setup

A good dyno begins with a solid setup. The climber gets the feet high and the body coiled, hands on the launching holds, and eyes locked on the target. The body lowers and loads like a spring, then the climber generates a smooth countermovement — sinking down before driving up — to build momentum. The setup determines everything; a rushed or unbalanced launch sends the body off-line and the dyno fails before it begins.

## The Launch

Power for a dyno comes mostly from the legs, not the arms. The climber drives explosively through the feet, extending the legs and hips to propel the body upward, while the arms guide and add a final pull. Timing is everything: the push from the legs, the pull from the arms, and the release of the hands must coordinate into one fluid motion. Throwing with the arms alone is the classic mistake and rarely generates enough height.

## The Deadpoint

The key concept in catching a dyno is the deadpoint: the instant at the top of the trajectory where the body is momentarily weightless, neither rising nor falling. The skilled climber times the catch so the hand reaches the target hold exactly at this deadpoint, when there is no momentum to fight. Catch too early or too late and the bodyweight rips the hand off. Learning to feel and hit the deadpoint is what separates a controlled dyno from a desperate lunge.

## The Catch and the Stick

Catching the hold is only half the battle; sticking it is the rest. As the hands meet the target, the climber must instantly engage the fingers, tense the core, and bring the feet back onto the wall to absorb the swing. A precise hit on the deadpoint makes the catch soft; a mistimed one loads the fingers violently. Bringing the feet back quickly and controlling the body's swing is what turns a catch into a successful move.

## Training Dynos

Dynos are trained like any athletic skill: with practice and progression. Start with small dynamic moves and deadpoints on a bouldering wall, building the coordination and confidence before attempting big leaps. Practising the same dyno repeatedly teaches the body the timing. Explosive leg power, core tension, and a willingness to commit all improve with repetition. As with all powerful moves, warm up thoroughly and progress gradually to avoid finger and shoulder injury.
