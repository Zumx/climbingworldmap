Multi-pitch climbing is how climbers ascend cliffs taller than a single rope-length. The route is broken into pitches, each roughly the length of the rope, and the team leapfrogs upward: one climber leads a pitch, builds an anchor, and belays the second up, then the process repeats. It transforms climbing from a single hard effort into a sustained journey up a face, often with spectacular position and commitment. It also demands a system of anchors, transitions, and planning that single-pitch climbing never requires. Explore great multi-pitch destinations on the [map](/map).

## What a Pitch Is

A pitch is a single segment of a multi-pitch climb, bounded by belay stations and limited by the length of the rope and the available anchors. A 300-metre face might be split into eight or ten pitches. The leader climbs a pitch placing or clipping protection, reaches an anchor, and secures themselves; the follower then climbs while belayed from above. The team moves up the cliff one pitch at a time, never both moving unprotected at once.

## Building and Using Anchors

The anchor is the heart of multi-pitch safety. At the top of each pitch the leader builds a secure anchor — using bolts, gear placements, or natural features — that can hold a fall in any direction and is equalised across multiple points. The principle of redundancy is paramount: never trust a single point. The leader clips in, belays the second up to the anchor, and the team reorganises for the next pitch. A poor anchor is one of the most serious hazards in the mountains.

## Swapping Leads and Following

Teams commonly "swing leads," alternating who leads each pitch, which is efficient because the gear ends up at the right end of the rope. Alternatively one strong climber leads every pitch while the other follows and cleans the protection. The follower's job is to climb steadily, remove gear placed by the leader, and arrive at the anchor organised. Smooth transitions at the belay — exchanging gear, restacking the rope, swapping the belay — are what make a team fast and safe.

## Communication on Big Routes

On long pitches, wind and distance can make voice commands useless, so teams agree on rope signals and clear systems in advance. Standard practice is for the leader to call "off belay" on reaching the anchor and "on belay" when ready to bring the second up. Many teams use a fixed sequence of rope tugs as backup. Misunderstood communication on a multi-pitch route is dangerous; clarity and pre-agreed systems prevent the most serious mistakes.

## Descent and Retreat

Getting down is half the climb. Some routes walk off the top; many require a series of rappels back down the face, which carries its own hazards — rope management, anchor checks, and avoiding rockfall. Retreating from partway up, perhaps due to weather or a slow pace, demands the skills to build rappel anchors and descend safely. Always know the descent before you start, and carry the gear and knowledge to bail if conditions turn.

## Planning and Pacing

Multi-pitch routes demand planning that single-pitch climbing does not: studying the topo, judging how long the route will take, watching the weather window, and carrying water, food, and layers for hours on the wall. Speed is safety in the mountains, so efficient systems and a realistic pace matter as much as climbing ability. Start with short, well-protected multi-pitch routes well within your grade before committing to long or remote objectives.
