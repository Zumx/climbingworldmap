The rope is the climber's lifeline, and how you care for it directly affects both its lifespan and your safety. Ropes are expensive, and good care can extend their life considerably, but more importantly, a well-maintained rope is a safe rope, while a neglected one can hide damage that compromises its strength. Caring for a rope is simple — keeping it clean, stored properly, handled carefully, and inspected regularly — yet often overlooked. This guide covers how to care for your rope and protect this most critical piece of equipment. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## What Damages a Rope

Several things degrade a climbing rope. Dirt and grit are surprisingly harmful: fine particles work into the fibres and abrade them from the inside with every flex, weakening the rope invisibly over time. Sharp edges can cut or damage the sheath; chemicals, including some solvents and acids, can destroy the fibres without visible signs; and prolonged UV exposure degrades the material. Heat, repeated hard falls, and simple age all take their toll. Understanding these threats guides how to protect the rope.

## Keeping It Clean

Keeping a rope clean is one of the most effective ways to extend its life. Use a rope bag or tarp to keep it off the dirt at the crag, preventing grit working into the fibres. When a rope becomes dirty, it can be washed in lukewarm water — by hand or in a machine on a gentle cycle without spinning — using plain water or a dedicated rope cleaner, never harsh detergents. Rinse it well and air-dry it away from direct heat and sun. A clean rope handles better and lasts longer.

## Storing It Properly

Proper storage protects the rope between climbs. Store it loosely coiled or flaked in a cool, dry, dark place, away from direct sunlight, heat sources, and any chemicals — never in a hot car, near a battery, or with solvents. A rope bag is ideal. Avoid leaving it tightly coiled under tension for long periods. Good storage prevents UV and chemical degradation and keeps the rope ready and reliable for its next use.

## Inspecting for Damage

Regular inspection catches damage before it becomes dangerous. Run the whole rope through your hands, feeling for flat spots, soft or mushy sections, lumps, or stiffness that indicate core damage, and looking for sheath wear, cuts, fraying, or discolouration. Pay attention to the ends, which take the most wear. If you feel or see anything concerning, treat the rope with suspicion. A rope that fails inspection should be retired, because the core damage you cannot see is what matters most.

## Handling It Carefully

How you handle a rope day to day affects its life and safety. Keep it off sharp edges, where a loaded rope can be cut; avoid stepping on it, which grinds grit into the fibres; and protect it from chemicals and heat. Flake it out rather than dropping it in a heap, and uncoil a new rope properly to avoid kinks. Careful handling, a habit built into every session, prevents the avoidable damage that shortens a rope's safe life.

## When to Retire It

Even a well-cared-for rope has a finite life. Retire it after a severe fall, any visible or felt damage, contact with chemicals, or simply old age, following the manufacturer's guidance on lifespan even for a lightly used rope. The cost of a new rope is trivial against the consequences of trusting a compromised one. When in doubt, retire it. Caring for a rope is about both extending its safe life and knowing, with confidence, when that life is over.
