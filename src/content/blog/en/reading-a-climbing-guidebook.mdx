The climbing guidebook is the key that unlocks a crag, the collected knowledge of the climbers who came before, distilled into maps, photos, grades, and route descriptions. Learning to read a guidebook well lets you find a crag, choose routes suited to your level, understand the approach and the descent, and climb safely in an unfamiliar place. Yet guidebooks are dense with symbols, conventions, and information that can baffle the newcomer. This guide explains how to decode a climbing guidebook and use it to plan and climb. ## What a Guidebook Contains

A climbing guidebook gathers everything you need to climb at an area: an overview of the crags, maps and approach directions, photos or diagrams of the cliffs (topos) showing the lines of routes, and for each route its name, grade, length, style, and protection. Many add notes on the rock, the seasons, access, history, and ethics. The guidebook is the distilled local knowledge that lets a visitor climb where the locals climb, find the classics, and avoid the pitfalls.

## Decoding Grades and Style

The most important information for any route is its grade, which tells you the difficulty in the local system — French, YDS, UIAA, or another. A good guidebook explains its grading system and may add style notes — "sustained," "bouldery," "technical," "pumpy" — that reveal what kind of climb it is. Some routes also carry danger or commitment ratings. Reading the grade alongside these notes lets you judge whether a route suits your ability and the kind of climbing you enjoy.

## Reading the Symbols

Guidebooks use a dense visual language of symbols. Stars or other marks indicate quality, flagging the classics worth seeking out. Symbols show whether a route is sport or trad, the number of bolts or the gear needed, the presence of a lower-off or walk-off descent, and hazards. Every guidebook has a legend explaining its symbols, and reading it first is essential, because conventions vary between books and regions. A few minutes with the legend unlocks the whole guide.

## Finding the Crag and the Route

Beyond the climbing itself, a guidebook helps you get there and find the route. Approach descriptions and maps guide you from the parking to the base of the cliff, often the trickiest part of a day. Once at the crag, the topo — a photo or drawing of the cliff with the route lines marked — lets you identify which route is which. Matching the topo to the rock in front of you, using features and the position of routes, is a skill that comes with practice.

## Approach, Descent, and Access Notes

A guidebook's value extends well beyond the grades. Approach notes save you from getting lost or walking the wrong way; descent information — whether you lower off, rappel, or walk off — is essential for getting down safely, and not reading it has stranded many climbers. Access notes on parking, seasonal closures, and local rules are vital for climbing responsibly and keeping the crag open. Reading all of this before you go prevents the most common day-ruining mistakes.

## Guidebooks and Apps

Increasingly, printed guidebooks are joined or replaced by apps and online databases offering the same information digitally, often with user updates and GPS. Each has advantages: a book needs no battery and gives an overview, while an app can update and locate you. Whichever you use, the skill is the same — decoding the grades, symbols, and descriptions to plan and climb safely. The guidebook, in print or pixels, remains the climber's essential key to the crag.
