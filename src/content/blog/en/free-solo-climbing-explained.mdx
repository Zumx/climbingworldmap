Free soloing is the most serious and consequential form of climbing: ascending a route with no rope, harness, or protection of any kind, where a fall almost always means death. It is climbing stripped to its absolute essence, the climber and the rock alone, and it occupies a place of fascination and controversy unlike anything else in the sport. Brought to global attention by Alex Honnold's free solo of El Capitan, it remains a rare and deeply personal pursuit. This guide explains what free soloing is, how it differs from related styles, and the mindset it demands. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## What Free Soloing Is

Free soloing means free climbing — using only hands and feet on the rock — entirely without ropes, protection, or any safety equipment. The climber is utterly unprotected: a fall from any significant height is fatal. This makes free soloing fundamentally different from every other form of roped or padded climbing, where falls are caught. It is not a discipline most climbers practise, but a rare extreme pursued by a small number who accept its absolute consequences.

## How It Differs from Other Ropeless Climbing

Free soloing is often confused with other ropeless styles, but the difference is the consequence of a fall. Bouldering is ropeless but stays close to the ground over pads. Deep water soloing climbs above water that catches the fall. Free soloing has no safety net at all: the climber is high on the rock with nothing to catch them. This is what sets it apart and makes it the most serious form of climbing, where there is no margin for any error.

## Alex Honnold and El Capitan

Free soloing entered global consciousness with Alex Honnold's 2017 free solo of Freerider on El Capitan, a 900-metre granite wall, climbed without a rope and captured in the film Free Solo. The ascent, the culmination of years of meticulous preparation, was hailed as one of the greatest athletic feats ever and brought free soloing to a vast audience. Honnold's careful, almost clinical approach to managing risk became central to how the pursuit is understood.

## The Mindset and Preparation

Free soloing at a high level is not reckless; the climbers who do it survive through extraordinary preparation and mental control. A serious free soloist typically rehearses a route exhaustively with a rope, memorising every move until it is utterly secure, climbs far below their physical limit so the moves carry no doubt, and waits for perfect conditions. The mental discipline to stay calm and execute flawlessly, with no margin for error, is as remarkable as the physical skill.

## The Controversy

Free soloing is deeply controversial within climbing. Some see it as the purest expression of the sport, others as an unjustifiable risk that romanticises death and can influence less experienced climbers. The deaths of accomplished free soloists are a sobering reminder of its absolute consequences. The climbing community largely treats it as a personal choice for a very few, while cautioning strongly against emulating it, and the debate over its place in the sport continues.

## Why It Remains Rare

Free soloing remains, and will remain, a rare pursuit practised by a tiny minority. The combination of physical mastery, mental control, and acceptance of absolute consequence that it demands is beyond what almost all climbers seek or should attempt. For the overwhelming majority, climbing is and should be a roped, protected, survivable pursuit. Free soloing stands apart at the extreme edge, a fascinating and sobering reminder of where the sport's boundaries lie.
