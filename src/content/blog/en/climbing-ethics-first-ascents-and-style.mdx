Walk beneath any famous crag and you stand under decades of decisions. Every route was first climbed by someone who chose how to do it: from the ground up with no prior knowledge, or rehearsed top-down until the moves were memorised. Climbing has no referee and no rulebook, only ethics agreed by the community and enforced by reputation. The result is a sport where how you climb a piece of rock can matter as much as whether you reach the top. This is the long, quarrelsome, fascinating story of first ascents and style.

## What a First Ascent Means

A first ascent is the first complete, successful climb of a route by fair means, and the person who makes it earns the right to name it and, by convention, to record the style in which it was done. On established crags a first ascent might be a new line squeezed between existing routes; on remote walls it can mean weeks of effort on rock no one has touched. The first ascensionist sets the benchmark: the grade, the gear, and the manner. Later climbers measure themselves against that standard, and the history of a crag is really the accumulated record of who did what, and how.

## Ground-Up, the Original Ethic

The oldest and purest ethic is ground-up: you start at the bottom with no foreknowledge and climb upward, placing protection as you go, accepting falls onto whatever gear you have placed. If you fall, you lower off and try again, but you do not inspect the moves from above first. This was how the great traditional routes of the Lake District, the Llanberis Pass, and the Yosemite cracks were established. Ground-up climbing rewards boldness and on-sight problem-solving, and its defenders argue that it preserves the adventure and uncertainty that make climbing meaningful rather than merely athletic.

## The Headpoint and Rehearsal

As routes grew harder and more dangerous, a different method emerged: the headpoint. A climber abseils down the line, works out and rehearses every move on a top-rope, memorises the sequence, and only then leads it with minimal or marginal protection. The hardest traditional routes on British grit and slate, climbs like those pioneered on Gogarth and the Peak District edges, were almost all headpointed, because leading them on sight would be suicidal. Headpointing is controversial: critics call it a dilution of adventure, while its practitioners argue it is the only sane way to climb extreme, poorly protected rock.

## Bolts and the Sport Compromise

Sport climbing resolved the danger problem by accepting pre-placed bolts, and with it came a different ethic entirely. On a bolted route the question of ground-up versus rehearsed becomes the question of on-sight versus redpoint: climbing it first try with no knowledge, or after practice. The redpoint — leading cleanly after rehearsal — is the accepted standard for hard sport routes worldwide, from the tufas of Kalymnos to the limestone of Céüse in the French Alps. The on-sight, by contrast, remains the prized achievement, the closest sport climbing comes to the uncertainty that trad climbers cherish.

## Chipping, Glue and the Limits

Style debates turn bitter when climbers alter the rock itself. Chipping holds, gluing loose flakes, or manufacturing finger-pockets is almost universally condemned, because it permanently changes a resource that belongs to everyone and to the future. The line between acceptable cleaning — brushing lichen, removing genuinely dangerous loose blocks — and unacceptable manufacturing is fiercely debated, and a route discovered to be chipped is often stripped of its grade and its standing. The community polices this through naming and shaming, because the rock cannot defend itself and there is no authority but consensus.

## Why Style Survives

In a sport with no governing body, style is the mechanism by which climbers keep meaning in what they do. The summit is trivially reachable by ladder or helicopter; what gives a climb value is the self-imposed constraint of doing it by fair means. That is why a clean on-sight of a modest route can carry more respect than a heavily rehearsed ascent of something harder, and why first ascensionists guard their routes and reputations so carefully. Style is climbing's conscience, the agreement that the journey, honestly made, is the whole point.

## Ethics in Practice Today

Modern climbers inherit all of these traditions at once and must choose among them. A weekend at a crag might involve on-sighting easy trad, redpointing a bolted project, and bouldering a problem worked over many sessions, each governed by its own honest standard. The ethics are not rules imposed from outside but habits of honesty: claiming only what you genuinely did, respecting the first ascensionist's vision, and leaving the rock as you found it. Carry these and you join a long, argumentative, but fundamentally shared tradition.
