Weather and conditions are among the most decisive factors in climbing, often mattering as much as fitness or skill. The temperature, humidity, wind, sun, and recent precipitation all shape how the rock feels, how well it grips, and whether a climb is even possible or safe. Climbers learn to read conditions and time their efforts for when the rock is "in" — at its best. This guide explains the main factors that govern climbing conditions and how climbers work with the weather to climb well and safely. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Temperature

Temperature is perhaps the single biggest factor in rock conditions. Cool, dry air gives the best friction: hands and rubber grip better, holds feel more positive, and many climbers perform their hardest in cold conditions. Heat does the opposite, making skin sweat and rubber go greasy, so that holds feel slippery and hard climbs become much harder. But extreme cold brings its own problems — numb fingers, stiff bodies, and the risk of frostbite. The ideal window for hard rock climbing is often cool but not freezing, when friction is high and the body still works.

## Humidity and Sweat

Humidity strongly affects friction. Damp, humid air leaves a film of moisture on the rock and the climber's skin, killing grip, while dry air lets skin and rubber bite. This is why hard climbers favour cool, dry, low-humidity days and avoid muggy ones. High humidity also makes skin soft and prone to wearing through. Climbers watch the dew point and humidity closely, and some destinations are famous for the dry, friction-rich conditions that make them prime venues for hard sending.

## Wind and Sun

Wind and sun shape conditions in complementary ways. A breeze dries sweat and rock, keeping friction high, which is why a windy day can feel grippier even at the same temperature. Sun warms cold rock to a workable temperature on a winter day but bakes it unpleasantly in summer. Climbers chase or avoid sun depending on season — seeking shade and "the shade going on" in summer, and sun-warmed rock in winter. Aspect, whether a crag faces the sun or away from it, becomes a key consideration in planning when and where to climb.

## Rock State and Drying

The state of the rock itself matters as much as the air. Wet rock is slippery, weaker, and sometimes off-limits for ethical reasons, and after rain different rock types dry at very different rates. Sandstone can take days and is fragile when wet; limestone and granite dry faster. Seepage from inside the rock can keep holds wet long after rain. Climbers learn how their local rock behaves, how long it takes to come back into condition after weather, and when it is dry and strong enough to climb well and safely.

## Season and Climate

Over the year, season governs which areas are in condition. Many hard sport and bouldering areas have a clear season — autumn through spring in hot climates, summer in cold ones — when temperatures and humidity align for good friction. Alpine and mountain climbing has its own narrow windows tied to snow, ice, and storm patterns. Experienced climbers plan trips around these seasons, travelling to where conditions are best at a given time of year, and treating the calendar as a key tool for finding good climbing.

## Reading and Timing Conditions

Putting it together, skilled climbers read conditions and time their efforts accordingly. They check forecasts, watch the temperature and humidity, choose sunny or shady crags by season, and pick the right time of day for an objective. On a hard project, they may wait days or weeks for the perfect cool, dry, windy window. Learning to read conditions — and to be patient for the right ones — is part of climbing well, and it turns the weather from an obstacle into a tool.
