Of all the equipment on a climbing harness, the cheapest item may be the most important: a short loop of thin accessory cord. Wrapped around the rope in the right way, it becomes a friction hitch — a knot that slides freely when pushed but grips the rope hard when loaded. The most famous of them was described in 1931 by Karl Prusik, an Austrian music professor and mountaineer, and his name has become both noun and verb. Friction hitches back up rappels, climb ropes, hold loads in rescue systems and escape belays, and a climber who can tie three of them from memory carries a toolkit that no powered gadget replaces.

## Why Friction Hitches Matter

The friction hitch solves a fundamental problem: how to attach something to a loaded rope so it can be moved when you want and locks when you need. Mechanical devices do this too, but hitches do it with gear you already carry, weigh almost nothing, work on icy or muddy ropes where some devices struggle, and can be improvised from slings in a pinch. They are the backbone of self-rescue — almost every technique for escaping a belay, ascending a rope or hauling an injured partner is built from friction hitches plus ordinary carabiners. That is why instructors treat them as core curriculum, not optional extras.

## The Classic Prusik

The original prusik is tied by passing a girth hitch of cord around the rope and feeding the loop through itself two or three more times, keeping the wraps neat and parallel. Its character is symmetric: it grips equally hard in both directions, which makes it the standard choice for rescue systems and for any situation where the load might reverse. Its weakness is the same grip — once heavily weighted, a prusik can be stubborn to loosen, and it must be fully unweighted to slide. Three wraps is the starting point; add a fourth on slick, skinny or icy ropes when the hitch slips under load.

## The Klemheist

The klemheist is wrapped upward around the rope in a spiral, with the tail fed through the top loop and pulled down. It grips strongly in one direction only, sets and releases more easily than a classic prusik, and has one killer feature: it works acceptably with a nylon sling when no cord is available, something the prusik does poorly. That makes it the improviser's hitch — the one you reach for when your cord is already in use. Because it is directional, orient it deliberately, and remember that loading it the wrong way can cause it to slide rather than catch.

## The Autoblock

The autoblock, also called the French prusik or Machard, is the gentlest of the family: cord wrapped around the rope in simple coils with both ends clipped to the same carabiner. It grips when loaded yet can be released under load with a squeeze — exactly the behaviour you want in a rappel backup, where the hitch must hold you if you let go but slide as you descend. Rigged below an extended rappel device on a leg loop or belay loop, it has become the standard third hand. The same releasability makes it the wrong choice as a primary attachment: never use an autoblock where its failure would be unbackable.

## Cord, Diameter and Materials

A friction hitch works because thin cord deforms around a thicker rope, so diameter ratio is everything. The rule of thumb is hitch cord around 60 to 80 percent of the rope diameter: 5 to 6 millimetre cord suits modern 8.5 to 9.8 millimetre ropes, with 7 millimetre reserved for fat ropes and heavy rescue loads. Nylon cord is the all-rounder; aramid-cored cords grip well and resist heat but are stiffer. Pre-sewn loops sold as friction hitch cords are tidy and strong, while a hand-tied loop closed with a double fisherman's bend is the classic, inspectable option. Retire any cord that glazes, stiffens or shows core through the sheath.

## Where the Hitches Earn Their Keep

The everyday application is the rappel backup: an autoblock below the device guards every descent, from a single-pitch lower-off retreat to the long abseils of an alpine ridge. The second is rope ascent — two prusiks or klemheists, one for the harness and one for a foot, will climb a free-hanging rope slowly but surely, the skill that gets you back up after rappelling past an anchor. The third is rescue: friction hitches transfer loads, hold a fallen climber while you escape the belay, and serve as progress capture in hauling systems. One hitch, practised well, multiplies into a dozen techniques.

## Limits and Failure Modes

Friction hitches are not magic. They can melt — a hitch dragged down a rope under load generates heat fast, and nylon weakens long before it visibly burns. They slip on wet, icy or glazed ropes, and a sloppy hitch with crossed wraps grips far worse than a neat one. A hitch tied with cord too thick for the rope may slide under body weight; too thin, and it can weld itself shut. And every hitch depends on the human who tied it: dress the wraps, test the grip with a sharp tug before trusting it, and check it again whenever the system changes. Treat the hitch as a component to inspect, not a talisman.

## Learning Them Properly

Friction hitches are a ground-school skill. Tie all three on a hanging rope at home or at the base of the crag until your hands know them without your eyes. Then practise the applications in safe settings: back up a rappel on a quiet afternoon, ascend three metres of rope in a gym or off a tree branch, run through a belay escape with the system backed up at every stage. Many climbing clubs and guide-led self-rescue courses drill exactly these scenarios, and one structured day turns the hitches from trivia into usable tools. The time to learn a prusik is never while hanging from one.
