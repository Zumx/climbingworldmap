Volcanic rock gives climbing some of its most striking and varied terrain, from the geometric columns of cooled basalt to the sharp, pocketed tuff of ancient ash deposits and the grippy, abrasive surfaces of lava flows. Formed by the earth's fire, volcanic rocks differ enormously in texture and character, offering everything from elegant crack lines up basalt columns to bizarre, holey faces on tuff. Found on volcanic islands and ranges worldwide, this rock rewards adaptability and offers climbing unlike the great sedimentary and granite areas. This guide explores volcanic rock and the climbing it offers. Explore volcanic climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## How Volcanic Rock Forms

Volcanic, or igneous extrusive, rock forms when magma erupts and cools at or near the earth's surface, far faster than the slow underground cooling that creates granite. This rapid cooling produces a range of rock types: basalt, the dark, fine-grained rock that often forms columns; tuff, made of compacted volcanic ash; and various lavas with rough, abrasive surfaces. The way each cooled and the materials it contains give volcanic rocks their distinctive and varied climbing characters.

## Basalt Columns

One of volcanic rock's most striking forms is the basalt column: as thick lava flows cool and contract, they fracture into geometric, often hexagonal columns standing in dramatic ranks. These columns offer superb crack climbing up the joints between them, with parallel-sided cracks taking gear, alongside the edges and faces of the columns themselves. Basalt is generally solid and grippy, and columnar basalt crags, found from Iceland to the western United States, offer striking, geometric climbing.

## Tuff and Pocketed Rock

Volcanic tuff, formed from consolidated ash, weathers into pocketed, featured rock that can be sharp and abrasive. The pockets and holes give a climbing style similar to limestone but often on softer, sharper rock, and the texture can be brutal on the skin. Some of the world's distinctive sport climbing is on tuff and similar volcanic rock, which can range from solid to surprisingly fragile, demanding the climber assess the rock as they go.

## Lava and Abrasive Surfaces

Lava flows create rough, sharp, highly abrasive surfaces that offer remarkable friction but punish the skin. Climbing on rough lava rock is grippy and secure underfoot but can shred fingertips, and the holds are often sharp edges and rough pockets. This abrasive character means volcanic lava rock rewards solid footwork and toughened skin, and it gives a distinctive, grippy climbing experience found on volcanic islands and recent flows.

## Variety and Adaptation

The defining feature of volcanic rock is its variety. Because it forms in so many ways, no two volcanic areas are quite alike, and climbers must adapt to whatever the local rock offers — geometric basalt cracks, pocketed tuff, abrasive lava, or something else entirely. This demands the same adaptability as moving between rock types, learning the friction, holds, and solidity of each volcanic area. For the climber who enjoys variety, volcanic rock offers endless distinctive experiences.

## The Great Volcanic Destinations

Volcanic rock shapes climbing on islands and ranges worldwide. The Canary Islands, Réunion, and other volcanic islands offer climbing on basalt and tuff in spectacular settings. Iceland's basalt columns, the volcanic crags of the western United States, and the tuff and lava of countless volcanic regions each give their own character. Each rewards adaptability and offers a climbing experience distinct from the sedimentary and granite mainstream, broadening the world a climber can explore.
