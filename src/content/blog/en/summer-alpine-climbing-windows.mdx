The great alpine routes of the world open for only a short window each year. As summer warms the high mountains, snow consolidates and melts, faces come into condition, and a brief season — often just a few months, narrowed further by weather — offers the chance to climb. Within that season, climbers wait for short windows of good weather, sometimes only a day or two, to make their attempts. Reading and seizing these windows is at the heart of alpinism. ## Why the Season Is Short

High mountains are hostile for most of the year, locked in winter snow and storm. Only in summer do conditions ease enough for most alpine climbing: temperatures rise, days lengthen, the worst storms abate, and snow and ice reach a workable state. But the high-alpine summer is brief — a matter of months in the European Alps, even shorter in greater ranges — and within it the mountains remain serious and changeable. The short season concentrates alpine climbing into a narrow part of the year, when conditions and weather briefly align.

## Conditions Through the Summer

Within the summer season, conditions evolve constantly. Early summer often has more snow, firmer névé, and better-frozen ice, which suits snow and mixed routes but leaves some rock buried. As summer progresses, snow melts to reveal rock, glaciers open up and crevasses widen, and rockfall increases as the ice that binds faces together melts. Late summer can bring dry rock but dangerous, melted-out conditions on snow and ice routes. Alpine climbers match their objectives to where the season is — choosing snow and ice routes early, rock routes later — to find each in its best condition.

## The Weather Window

Even in season, the weather rules everything. Alpine weather is fierce and fast-changing, and a storm high on a peak is deadly. So climbers watch the forecast for windows of stable, settled weather — sometimes a stretch of several fair days, sometimes only a single clear day between storms — and time their climbs to them. A big alpine route may demand a window of good weather long enough to climb it and descend safely. Much of alpinism is waiting, watching the forecast, and being ready to move the moment a window opens.

## Reading the Forecast

Modern alpine climbers rely heavily on detailed mountain weather forecasts, watching pressure systems, precipitation, freezing levels, and wind. The freezing level matters enormously: it governs whether snow stays frozen and safe or turns to dangerous slush, and whether rockfall is locked up or loosed. Climbers learn to read forecasts for their specific objective, judging whether the window is long and stable enough, and whether the freezing level suits the route. Good forecasting has transformed alpinism, but the mountains still surprise, and judgement remains essential.

## Timing the Climb

With a window identified, climbers time the climb itself carefully. Many alpine routes are climbed with an "alpine start" — setting off in the small hours of the night — to cross snow and glaciers while they are frozen and firm, to reach the summit and descend before afternoon storms build, and to be off dangerous ground before the day's warmth loosens rock and snow. The discipline of the alpine start, and of turning back when the window closes, is central to climbing safely in the mountains, where timing is as important as technique.

## Living by the Window

For alpine climbers, the rhythm of the summer season — waiting for windows, seizing them, and respecting their limits — becomes a way of life. A summer in the Alps or a greater range is spent watching weather, getting fit and acclimatized, and being ready to commit when conditions align. The great routes are climbed not just by the strong and skilled, but by those patient enough to wait for the right window and bold enough to seize it. Learning to live by the window is the essence of summer alpinism.
