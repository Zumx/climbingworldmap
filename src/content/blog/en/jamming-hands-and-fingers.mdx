Crack climbing turns the rock inside out. Instead of grabbing edges and pockets, you slot parts of your body into the gap between two faces and wedge them tight, then trust that the friction and bone-on-stone contact will hold your weight. It feels alien the first time, even painful, and most face climbers flail badly on their first splitter. But the technique is learnable, and on the granite of Yosemite, the desert sandstone of Indian Creek, or the gritstone of the Peak District, it is the only way up. ## Why Cracks Demand a Different Skill

A crack offers no holds in the conventional sense — just two parallel walls separated by a gap. To climb it you create your own holds by jamming a body part into the fissure and camming it so it locks. The size of the crack dictates the technique: a thin seam takes only fingertips, a wider one swallows a whole hand, and a fist crack needs a clenched fist. Reading the crack and choosing the right jam for each section is the heart of the craft, and it is a completely separate discipline from face climbing.

## Finger Jams

The thinnest cracks accept only fingers. You slot them in thumb-down, twisting the hand so the fingers cam against the walls — a move that feels counterintuitive until the lock engages and suddenly feels bombproof. Thumb-down finger jams are the standard, but thumb-up locks work in flares and on certain sequences. Finger cracks are notoriously strenuous because the contact area is small and the position torques the wrist and elbow. The classic finger splitters of Indian Creek, on Wingate sandstone, are a brutal and beautiful proving ground.

## Hand Jams: The Heart of the Craft

The hand jam is the crack climber's bread and butter and the most secure jam of all. You slide your hand into a crack roughly the width of your palm, then expand it by cupping the palm and flexing the thumb across toward the base of the fingers, swelling the hand until it locks. A good hand jam in a perfectly sized crack feels more secure than any face hold — you can hang from it completely relaxed. Hand cracks are where climbers fall in love with the discipline, and splitters like those on El Capitan's routes reward the technique endlessly.

## Fist Jams and Wider Sizes

When the crack grows too wide for a hand jam, you clench the hand into a fist, which expands the width of the hand and locks across the gap. Fist jams can be oriented palm-in or palm-out depending on the crack and the direction of pull. They are secure but punishing on the skin and knuckles, and verifying the lock before committing weight is essential. Above fist size the climbing becomes offwidth — the awkward, full-body struggle of cracks too wide to jam and too narrow to chimney.

## Footwork in Cracks

Hands are only half the system; the feet do enormous work in a crack. Foot jams involve rotating the foot to slot the toe and edge of the boot into the fissure, then twisting it upright to cam the foot in place. In thinner cracks you torque the toe; in wider ones you can get the whole foot in. Good crack climbers move smoothly off solid foot jams, taking weight off the arms. Sloppy footwork is why beginners pump out so fast — they hang entirely from their hands instead of standing on their feet.

## Taping and Skin Protection

Jamming is hard on the hands. The back of the hand grinds against rough rock, and long crack pitches can leave the skin raw and bleeding — the so-called gobies that mark every crack climber. Many climbers tape their hands, building tape gloves that protect the back of the hand and the knuckles while still allowing the hand to expand and jam. Taping is a personal art, and on a long desert crack day it is the difference between climbing and bleeding. Some purists climb tapeless and simply toughen their skin over seasons.

## Building Crack Skills

Crack climbing rewards patient, deliberate practice on easy terrain before you tackle anything hard. Find a moderate hand crack and climb it repeatedly, learning what a solid jam feels like and trusting it. Mileage on splitters like the moderate classics of Yosemite or the Utah desert builds the body knowledge that no amount of face climbing provides. The discomfort fades as skin toughens and technique sharpens, and crack climbing eventually becomes some of the most satisfying movement in the sport — efficient, secure, and utterly distinct.
