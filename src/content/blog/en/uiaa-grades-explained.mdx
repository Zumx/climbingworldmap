Open a classic alpine guidebook or climb in the sandstone of Saxon Switzerland and you will meet a grading scale written in Roman numerals: the UIAA scale. Developed by the Union Internationale des Associations d'Alpinisme, the international mountaineering federation, it was once intended as a universal standard. Today it has been largely displaced by the French system for sport climbing, but it endures in Germany, Austria, the wider Alps, and certain proud regional traditions. ## The Roman Numeral Scale

The UIAA scale runs upward in Roman numerals: I, II, III, IV, V, VI, VII, and onward through XII and beyond. Each numeral can be refined with a plus or minus, so the sequence reads V-, V, V+, VI-, VI, VI+, VII-, and so on. Grade I is the easiest scrambling; grade VI was long considered the absolute limit of human possibility until climbers proved otherwise. Like the French scale, it is now open-ended, growing as standards rise.

## A Brief History

The scale grew out of early twentieth-century alpinism, codified to bring order to the wildly varying difficulty descriptions of the day. For decades VI was regarded as the ceiling of free climbing — a near-mythical barrier. When climbers such as those in the Dresden sandstone and the Eastern Alps began climbing harder, the scale had to open at the top, and the modern open-ended UIAA scale was the result. This history is why older guidebooks rarely mention anything above VI.

## Where the UIAA Scale Dominates

While sport crags across Europe now use French grades, the UIAA scale remains the standard in several important contexts. Classic alpine routes in Austria, Germany, and the Dolomites are often graded in UIAA numerals. The sandstone towers of Saxon Switzerland near Dresden — one of the birthplaces of free climbing — maintain their own strict ethic and use the UIAA scale, often alongside a separate Saxon scale for the hardest climbs. German-language guidebooks still favour the system.

## Reading a UIAA Grade

In practice, the everyday climber meets UIAA grades most often on alpine multi-pitch routes. A route described as "V, A0" tells you the free climbing reaches grade V with a short section of aid. Because alpine grading also factors in seriousness, length, and remoteness through separate overall ratings, the UIAA technical number is only one part of the picture in the mountains. On a single-pitch crag in Germany, the number alone usually suffices.

## Comparing to French and YDS

Rough equivalences help when crossing systems. UIAA VI sits near French 5c and YDS 5.8/5.9; UIAA VII is around French 6a–6a+ and YDS 5.10; UIAA VIII corresponds to French 6c+/7a and YDS 5.11+; UIAA IX aligns with French 7b and beyond. These conversions are approximate, and because the UIAA scale is most used in alpine and traditional settings, the same number can feel bolder than its French equivalent at a bolted crag.

## The Saxon Scale

A close relative deserves mention: the Saxon (Sächsische) scale used in the Elbe sandstone around Dresden. It runs in Roman numerals to VII, then continues with Arabic-style subdivisions VIIa, VIIb, VIIc, VIIIa, and so on, reflecting the region's fierce traditional ethic that bans chalk, metal protection, and bolts on lead. It is a reminder that grading scales are cultural artefacts, shaped by the rock and the rules of the place that created them.
