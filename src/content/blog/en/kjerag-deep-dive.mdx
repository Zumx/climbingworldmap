Above the Lysefjord in southwestern Norway, the cliff of Kjerag rises in a sheer wall of granite more than a thousand metres above the deep blue water. This is one of Europe's most dramatic settings for big-wall climbing, where long routes ascend the steep granite directly above the fjord. Famous too for the Kjeragbolten — a boulder wedged in a crevice high above the void — and for BASE jumping, Kjerag is a place of vertical scale and serious adventure. ## The Setting

Kjerag sits at the head of the Lysefjord, a long, narrow fjord cutting deep into the mountains of Rogaland. The cliff plunges directly into the fjord, its granite walls rising over a thousand metres from the water. The setting is one of overwhelming scale and beauty, with the dark water below, the steep walls, and the open mountains around. It is a place that demands respect, both for the climbing and for the sheer exposure of the position above the fjord.

## The Rock and the Walls

The granite of Kjerag is steep, clean, and immense. The great walls above the fjord hold long multi-pitch routes, demanding big-wall skills and commitment. The rock is solid Norwegian granite, offering crack and face climbing on a grand scale, and the routes ascend directly above the void of the fjord, giving an exposure that few other walls can match. This is serious climbing for experienced parties, in a position of extraordinary drama.

## The Kjeragbolten

Kjerag is famous worldwide for the Kjeragbolten, a large boulder wedged in a mountain crevice, suspended above a thousand-metre drop to the fjord. Reached by a hiking route, it has become an iconic photo spot, where the bold can stand on a boulder above the void. While the Kjeragbolten itself is a hiking objective rather than a climb, it symbolises the vertical drama of Kjerag and draws visitors to this spectacular place.

## Lysefjord and the Wider Area

The Lysefjord region holds more than Kjerag. Nearby Preikestolen (the Pulpit Rock), a famous flat-topped cliff above the fjord, is one of Norway's most visited natural attractions, and the wider area offers dramatic walls, hiking, and the spectacular fjord scenery. For climbers, the combination of big-wall granite and the beauty of the fjord makes this one of the most rewarding adventure-climbing regions in Norway.

## Season and Conditions

Kjerag is a summer destination. The high walls come into condition once the snow has cleared, typically through the short Norwegian summer, when the long days allow time on the rock. The maritime climate brings frequent rain and rapidly changing weather, and the big-wall routes demand stable conditions and careful timing. The exposure above the fjord and the serious nature of the climbing mean Kjerag rewards experience, patience, and respect for the mountains.
