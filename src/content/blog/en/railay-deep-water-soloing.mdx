Deep water soloing — climbing ropeless above deep water, with the sea as the only protection — is one of the most exhilarating and accessible forms of adventure climbing. From the limestone karst of Thailand's Railay and Tonsai, where climbers ascend sea cliffs straight out of the warm Andaman Sea, to the coves of Mallorca where the discipline was popularised, deep water soloing offers a unique blend of freedom, commitment, and the safety net of a deep-water landing. ## What Is Deep Water Soloing?

Deep water soloing, often abbreviated DWS or called psicobloc, is climbing without ropes on cliffs above deep water. The climber ascends with no gear, and a fall ends in a splash rather than on the ground. This removes the weight and complexity of ropes and protection while keeping the falls relatively safe, provided the water is deep enough and free of hazards. It combines the purity of free soloing with a forgiving landing, making bold climbing accessible.

## Thailand: Railay and Tonsai

Thailand's Railay and Tonsai, on the Andaman coast, are among the world's premier deep water soloing venues. The limestone karst cliffs rise straight out of the warm turquoise sea, and climbers reach them by boat to ascend tufa-draped and pocketed rock above deep water. The warm sea, the spectacular karst scenery, and the accessibility — climbing from a longtail boat — make Thailand a dream destination for the discipline, combining adventure with a tropical setting.

## Mallorca and the Mediterranean

The Spanish island of Mallorca is where deep water soloing was popularised and given its Spanish name, psicobloc. The limestone coves and cliffs of the island offer superb deep water soloing above the Mediterranean, with hard test-pieces and accessible classics alike. Mallorca's combination of quality rock, warm sea, and beautiful coastline has made it a global centre of the discipline, drawing climbers each summer to its famous coastal walls.

## Technique and Commitment

Deep water soloing demands its own approach. Climbers must read the rock, manage the pump without the rest a rope allows, and commit to moves knowing a fall means a plunge into the sea. Falling cleanly into deep water is a skill in itself, and climbers must judge the water depth, tides, and hazards carefully. The combination of physical climbing and the mental commitment of ropeless movement, softened by the water, gives DWS its distinctive thrill.

## Season and Safety

Deep water soloing is a warm-season activity, dependent on calm seas, warm water, and good conditions. Tides, water depth, swell, and submerged hazards all matter, and climbers must assess each venue carefully. The forgiving landing makes bold climbing accessible, but falls from height into water can still injure, and the sea demands respect. Climbing with others, knowing the venue, and judging conditions are all essential to enjoying the discipline safely.
