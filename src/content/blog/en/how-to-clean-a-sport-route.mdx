You have clipped the last bolt, breathed out, and reached the anchor at the top of a sport route. The hard part is over, but the most accident-prone moment of the day is still ahead: cleaning. Cleaning means retrieving your quickdraws, threading the rope through the fixed anchor, and getting safely back to the ground. It is a sequence of small, deliberate steps where complacency, not difficulty, causes most mishaps. Done methodically every single time, it becomes second nature. This guide walks through the standard methods, the gear involved, and the habits that keep them safe.

## Lowering Versus Rappelling

There are two ways down from an anchor: lowering, where your belayer pays the rope out through a device while you hang, and rappelling, where you descend the rope yourself on a device. For most single-pitch sport climbing, lowering is the norm because it is fast, keeps the belayer in control, and avoids the rope manipulation that rappelling demands at the anchor. Rappelling spares the fixed hardware from wear and is sometimes required by local ethics or where anchors lack lowering-friendly hardware. Whichever you choose, the cardinal rule is to decide before you leave the ground and communicate it clearly, because the cleaning sequence differs in important ways.

## Going In Direct

Before you touch the rope at the anchor, secure yourself to it. This is called going in direct. Clip a personal anchor — a dedicated tether such as a Petzl Connect Adjust, a sewn sling, or a quickdraw-and-locker combination — from your belay loop to one of the anchor points. Many climbers use two independent connections for redundancy while working at the chains. Once you are weighting that tether and your belayer has taken you off tension, you can work hands-free. Never unclip the rope or untie until you are confident your direct connection is holding you. This single discipline prevents the most catastrophic cleaning errors.

## Threading the Anchor

Most modern sport anchors have one of two forms: open hardware such as mussy hooks or captive carabiners that you simply clip and lower from, or closed rings and chains that require threading. With mussy hooks or lowering rings, cleaning is simple — clip the rope directly through them, have your belayer take, weight the system to test it, then unclip your draws and direct tether and lower. With closed chains or rings, you must pass a bight of rope through the hardware. Pull up a loop of slack, push it through the rings, tie a figure-eight or overhand on a bight, clip it back to your belay loop with a locking carabiner, then untie your original knot and pull the tail through. Only now is the rope running through the fixed anchor.

## Testing Before You Commit

The threading sequence ends with a step that must never be skipped: testing the system before you remove your direct connection. Once the rope is threaded and you are tied or clipped back in, ask your belayer to take in tight, then gradually weight the rope while still backed up by your personal anchor. Watch the rope run through the rings and feel it come tight to your harness. Only when you are fully hanging on the threaded rope, with your belayer holding you, do you remove the direct tether. This redundancy — staying clipped to the anchor until the rope itself is proven to hold you — is what makes a fundamentally exposed task safe.

## Retrieving Your Quickdraws

With the anchor handled and your weight on the rope, you clean the draws on the way down. As your belayer lowers you smoothly, unclip each quickdraw from its bolt, but keep the rope clipped through the draw until the last possible moment, then unclip the rope and rack the draw. On steep or traversing routes a draw can swing you out from the wall, so on overhanging terrain it helps to clip the rope back into a lower draw or pull yourself in before unclipping. Communicate constantly — a short "stop" lets your belayer hold you steady while you fight a stiff gate. The goal is to reach the ground with every draw retrieved and nothing left on the wall. A useful habit is to count your draws before you set off and again at the base, so you know immediately if one was left clipped above. On long pitches it also pays to keep your weight settled and let the belayer set a steady lowering speed rather than dropping in jerks, which makes each draw far easier to unclip cleanly.

## Communication and Common Errors

Cleaning accidents almost always trace back to a breakdown in communication or assumption. The classic fatal error is the climber who unties to thread the anchor while the belayer, expecting a rappel, has taken them off belay — leaving nothing holding them when they lean back. Agree on the plan before climbing, use unambiguous commands, and never assume. If you and your partner are out of earshot on a noisy or windy crag, prearrange your sequence in detail. When in doubt, stay in direct and sort it out. No quickdraw, no lowering speed, and no audience is worth rushing the one moment where the whole system passes through your hands.

## Practising the Sequence

Like any safety-critical skill, cleaning rewards rehearsal in a low-consequence setting. Practise threading an anchor at ground level, or on a route you can easily reach, until the order of operations is automatic: go in direct, take off tension, thread, tie back in, test, untie the old knot, remove the tether, call to lower. Build in your own redundancies — a backup knot in the rope end so it cannot pass through your device, double connections at the chains — and keep them consistent. The mark of a competent leader is not climbing hard but cleaning every anchor the same careful way, on the hundredth route as on the first. It helps to verbalise the steps out loud the first dozen times, turning the checklist into muscle memory, and to have an experienced partner watch you thread an anchor before you do it unsupervised. Many gyms and clubs run lead-and-clean courses precisely because this transition — from following to managing the top of the route yourself — is where most beginners' near-misses occur.
