The belay device is the small but vital piece of gear that lets a belayer hold a falling climber with one hand. It works by creating friction on the rope, multiplying the belayer's grip so they can arrest a fall many times their own strength could not. Several types exist, from simple tube devices to sophisticated assisted-braking units, each with its own strengths, weaknesses, and ideal uses. Choosing and using the right device correctly is fundamental to safe climbing. This guide compares the main types and explains how each works. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## How Belay Devices Work

All belay devices work on the same principle: they create friction on the rope, amplifying the force the belayer's brake hand applies. By bending the rope sharply and pressing it against a carabiner or the device body, they multiply the holding power so a relatively small grip can hold a large load. The brake hand remains essential — it controls the rope below the device and is what ultimately holds the fall — and no device removes the need for correct, attentive belaying.

## Tube-Style Devices

The tube-style device, such as the classic ATC, is the simplest and most versatile. The rope is bent through a slot and over a carabiner to create friction, controlled entirely by the belayer's brake hand. Tube devices are light, affordable, work with a wide range of rope diameters, and many can take two ropes for multi-pitch and rappelling. They have no moving parts to fail, but they offer no assistance: if the brake hand fails, the rope runs free, so they demand attentive, correct technique at all times.

## Assisted-Braking Devices

Assisted-braking devices add a mechanism that helps lock the rope under a sudden load, providing a backup if the brake hand is momentarily compromised. The best-known is the Petzl GriGri, which uses a camming mechanism that pinches the rope when loaded sharply. Such devices add a margin of safety, are popular for sport climbing and working routes, and make holding a hanging climber easier. But they are not hands-free or foolproof — the brake hand is still required, and misuse, such as overriding the cam, has caused accidents.

## Figure-Eight Devices

The figure-eight device, an older design, is shaped like the numeral and creates friction by running the rope through its two loops. Once common for both belaying and rappelling, it is now mostly used for rappelling, especially in caving and rescue, where its smooth feeding suits heavy loads and big descents. It twists the rope and offers less control for catching falls than modern devices, so it has largely been replaced for belaying by tube and assisted-braking units.

## Choosing the Right Device

The right device depends on your climbing. A tube-style device is a versatile, affordable all-rounder, ideal for trad, multi-pitch, and learning, and the only choice when you need to rappel on two ropes or use half ropes. An assisted-braking device adds a safety margin valued in sport climbing and when belaying lots of falls or hangs. Many climbers own both. Whatever you choose, the device must match your rope diameter and, crucially, you must learn to use it correctly.

## Using Any Device Safely

No device is safe without correct technique. Whatever you use, the brake hand never leaves the rope, the device must be loaded correctly and the carabiner locked, and you must understand exactly how your device generates friction and how it can fail. Practise with each device until its use is automatic, and never assume an assisted-braking device removes the need for vigilance. The device amplifies your grip; your attention and technique remain the foundation of every safe belay.
