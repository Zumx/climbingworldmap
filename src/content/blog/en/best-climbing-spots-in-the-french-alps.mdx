The French Alps compress an entire climbing universe into one arc of mountains. At the top sits Chamonix, where the granite spires of the Mont Blanc massif gave alpinism its vocabulary; further south, the Écrins wrap the village of Ailefroide in slabs and big mountain faces; and along the western edge, the limestone ramparts of the Vercors and Chartreuse drop into green valleys. Cable cars put glaciers within minutes of coffee, hut wardens cook dinner at 3,000 metres, and the spectrum runs from roadside sport crags to the most serious alpine terrain in Western Europe. Few regions reward a climbing road trip so richly.

## Chamonix and the Mont Blanc granite

Chamonix is the capital of alpinism, and the Aiguilles de Chamonix — a sawblade of golden granite above the town — are its trademark. Classics like the ridges of the Aiguille du Peigne, the slabs of the Envers des Aiguilles and the perfect cracks of the Grand Capucin on the Mont Blanc du Tacul side draw climbers from everywhere. Mont Blanc itself, at 4,810 metres the highest summit in Western Europe, anchors the massif, but for rock climbers the joy lies in the satellite spires, where protection is good, the granite is impeccable and every belay looks across the Vallée Blanche.

## The Aiguille du Midi: altitude on demand

The Aiguille du Midi cable car hoists climbers from Chamonix to 3,842 metres in about twenty minutes, opening the highest playground in the Alps without a single approach march. From the ice tunnel, parties drop onto the snow arête and reach the Cosmiques Arête, the south face of the Midi, the Pointes Lachenal and the granite of the Tacul triangle. The price of convenience is altitude: many visitors underestimate how hard climbing feels at 3,800 metres without acclimatisation. Done sensibly, though, the Midi gives more world-class climbing per rest-day hour than anywhere else in the range.

## Écrins and Ailefroide: the granite village

South of the Mont Blanc crowds, the Écrins National Park hides France's wildest high mountains, with the Barre des Écrins reaching 4,102 metres. At its heart sits Ailefroide, a hamlet beneath hundreds of metres of glacier-polished granite slabs, where generations of French families have learned to climb. Single-pitch sport, ten-pitch slab outings and serious alpine faces all start from one of the most loved campgrounds in the Alps. Nearby, the south-facing crags of the Briançonnais — around Briançon, Europe's highest town — give sun-soaked sport climbing when the high peaks are storm-bound.

## Vercors, Chartreuse and the limestone walls

The pre-Alps along the western rim swap granite for limestone on a grand scale. In the Vercors, the 300-metre walls of Presles above the Gorges de la Bourne carry classic multi-pitch routes on pocketed rock, while the plateau's cliffs stretch for kilometres. The Chartreuse adds atmospheric walls like the Dent de Crolles above Grenoble, and further south the Dévoluy's huge faces, including the Pic de Bure, are for connoisseurs of remoteness. Grenoble makes a perfect base here — a university city ringed by crags, with the cliffs of Comboire and Saint-Égrève for after-work laps.

## High-mountain sport and valley crags

The French Alps are stacked with sport climbing at every altitude. The Gorges du Verdon, on the southern fringe, remains one of the world's great limestone venues, its 300-metre grey walls climbed from the rim downwards. Céüse, two hours from the Écrins, is regularly called the best sport crag on earth for its blue-streaked pocketed headwall. Closer to the glaciers, crags like Les Gaillands in Chamonix and the Fournel valley near L'Argentière-la-Bessée give quality limestone and granite cragging for stormy-afternoon escapes and family days between bigger objectives.

## Seasons, glaciers and mountain sense

The alpine rock season runs roughly mid-June to mid-September, when the high faces shed their snow; valley and southern crags climb from spring to autumn, and the Verdon is best in shoulder seasons. Glaciers are shrinking and rockfall is increasing — lines that were safe decades ago, including some Chamonix classics, now demand cold nights and early starts. Check conditions with the Office de Haute Montagne in Chamonix or hut guardians, carry glacier kit where needed, and treat afternoon storms as a certainty to plan around, not a possibility.

## Huts, lifts and logistics

What sets the French Alps apart is infrastructure. Refuges like the Cosmiques, the Envers des Aiguilles and the Glacier Blanc in the Écrins let climbers sleep at the base of their routes with dinner cooked; reservations are essential in summer. Lifts in Chamonix, La Grave and elsewhere cut approaches dramatically, and trains and buses link the valleys well. Chamonix, Briançon and Grenoble all have superb gear shops and guides' bureaus. Budget for hut nights and lift passes, and remember that a rest-day swim in a Briançonnais lake is part of the culture.
