France holds two of the most famous limestone destinations in the world, and they could hardly be more different. Céüse, high in the Alps above Gap, is a sport climber's dream of blue-grey rock and endurance routes. The Verdon, a vast canyon in Provence, is the birthplace of modern sport climbing and a place of airy multi-pitch adventure. Comparing them reveals two faces of French limestone, each defining a different style of climbing. Find them both on the [map](/map?country=fr).

## The Settings

Céüse is a mountain crag, its band of limestone curving across a hillside at around 2,000 metres, reached by a famously steep 45-minute approach. The Verdon is a deep canyon, its walls plunging hundreds of metres to a turquoise river, with many routes reached by rappelling in from the rim. One is a high alpine sport venue; the other a vast, airy gorge. The contrast in setting shapes the entire character of climbing at each.

## The Rock

Both are limestone, but of different character. Céüse offers compact blue-grey rock with pockets, crimps, and water-worn features, ideal for technical, sustained sport climbing. The Verdon's grey and orange limestone, often polished by traffic, gives vertical technical face climbing with the famous gouttes d'eau pockets. Céüse rewards endurance on perfect rock; the Verdon rewards calm, precise climbing in exposed positions. The rock quality is superb at both, but the experience differs.

## The Style of Climbing

Céüse is above all a single-pitch sport crag, where climbers project endurance routes on immaculate steep rock. The Verdon, while it has single-pitch climbing, is famous for its long multi-pitch routes and the committing rappel-in approach. Céüse is about pulling hard on perfect holds; the Verdon is about managing exposure, route-finding, and the airy commitment of climbing hundreds of metres above the river. The two demand and reward different skills.

## The Seasons

The settings dictate contrasting seasons. Céüse, at altitude with a southwest aspect, is a summer crag, offering cool air and good friction from roughly June to September when lowland crags bake. The Verdon, in Provence, climbs best in spring and autumn, with summer often too hot except in shade. A climber following the French limestone can move between them through the year, chasing the conditions from the mountain to the canyon.

## Which to Choose?

The choice between Céüse and the Verdon depends on what a climber seeks. For pure sport-climbing performance on perfect rock, Céüse is the pilgrimage. For airy multi-pitch adventure and a place of deep climbing history, the Verdon is unmatched. Many climbers visit both, treating them as two essential and complementary experiences of French limestone, each one of the finest of its kind in the world.
