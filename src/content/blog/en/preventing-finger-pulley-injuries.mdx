The finger pulleys are small bands of tissue that hold the flexor tendons against the finger bones, and they are the site of climbing's most common serious injury. A pulley strain or rupture, often signalled by a sudden pop and pain at the base of a finger, can sideline a climber for months. Because finger strength is the engine of hard climbing, the pulleys are loaded heavily, and understanding how they get injured is the first step to protecting them. ## What the Pulleys Are

The flexor tendons that bend the fingers run along the palm side of each finger, and a series of fibrous bands called pulleys hold them close to the bone so that the tendon transmits force efficiently. The most important for climbers is the A2 pulley, near the base of the finger, followed by the A4 pulley further out. When you crimp hard, these pulleys take enormous load as they restrain the bowstringing tendon, and they are the structures most likely to fail.

## How Pulleys Get Injured

Pulley injuries happen when the load on the tendon exceeds what the pulley can hold, causing it to strain or rupture. The classic scenario is full crimping on a small hold, especially when a foot slips and the bodyweight suddenly shock-loads a single finger. A pop or crack at the base of the finger, followed by pain, swelling, and sometimes bruising, is the hallmark. The A2 pulley is the most commonly injured, often during powerful, crimpy moves.

## Warning Signs and Risk Factors

A pulley injury often announces itself with an audible pop and sharp pain, but lesser strains may build gradually as a nagging ache at the base of a finger. Risk rises with full crimping, with cold or unwarmed fingers, with fatigue late in a session, and with rapid increases in training load. Climbers who progress quickly, train aggressively, or favour the full crimp are most at risk. Listening to early aches rather than pushing through them is crucial.

## Warming Up to Protect the Pulleys

A thorough warm-up is one of the best defences against pulley injury. Begin with easy movement and gentle climbing, gradually increasing intensity so the tendons and pulleys are prepared before you load them hard. Cold, unwarmed fingers are far more vulnerable, so never jump straight onto small crimps. A proper warm-up of progressively harder holds primes the connective tissue and dramatically reduces the chance of a sudden failure.

## Smart Crimping and Load Management

Because full crimping loads the pulleys most, climbing with a smart mix of grips protects them: favour the open-hand and half-crimp where possible, and avoid loading a full crimp dynamically or with the body off-balance. Manage training load by progressing gradually, since the pulleys strengthen far more slowly than the muscles. Keeping weight on the feet through good footwork takes load off the fingers entirely, which is the cheapest pulley protection there is.

## If You Get Injured

If you feel a pop or sharp pain, stop climbing immediately; continuing can turn a strain into a full rupture. Most minor pulley strains heal with rest and a gradual, careful return to climbing, often supported by taping the finger to relieve the pulley. More serious injuries need professional assessment and structured rehabilitation. Patience is essential: returning too soon is the most common reason a pulley injury becomes a chronic problem. Respect the healing time.
