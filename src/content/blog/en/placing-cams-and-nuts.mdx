Traditional climbing demands a skill that sport climbing does not: placing your own protection. Instead of clipping pre-placed bolts, the trad leader carries a rack of removable gear — primarily cams and nuts — and slots it into cracks and features as they climb, building the protection that will catch a fall. Placing gear well is a craft that takes years to master and one on which the leader's life directly depends. This guide explains how cams and nuts work and the principles of placing them, though it is no substitute for hands-on instruction. Explore trad climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## How Nuts Work

A nut, also called a wire or chock, is a metal wedge on a wire loop that protects by jamming into a constriction in a crack. The leader finds a spot where the crack narrows, slots the nut above the constriction, and gives it a firm downward tug to seat it so that a downward load wedges it tighter. Nuts come in a range of sizes and shapes, and choosing the one that fits the constriction snugly, in all dimensions, is the art. A well-placed nut is simple, light, and bombproof.

## How Cams Work

A camming device, or cam, uses spring-loaded lobes that rotate to grip the walls of a crack. Pulling a trigger retracts the lobes so the cam can be inserted, and releasing it lets the lobes expand against the rock; a downward load forces them to grip harder. Cams protect parallel-sided cracks where nuts cannot wedge, and they place quickly, making them the workhorse of modern trad. Their range of expansion means each size covers a band of crack widths.

## Placing a Cam Well

A good cam placement has the lobes in the middle of their range — not too open (tipped out and weak) and not too closed (over-cammed and hard to remove). The cam should be in solid, parallel rock, oriented so the stem points in the direction of likely loading, and set back from the edge of the crack. Avoid flaring cracks where the lobes can walk or slip, and loose or hollow rock that could fail. Reading the crack to choose the right size and placement is the core skill.

## Placing a Nut Well

A nut is placed by finding a constriction, selecting a size that fills it in both the width and depth dimensions, and seating it with a firm tug. The best placements have the nut sitting in a definite narrowing so that a load can only drive it deeper, with maximum metal in contact with the rock. A nut tool helps the second remove it. Nuts are lighter and cheaper than cams and, well placed, are extremely strong, which is why experienced trad climbers use them extensively.

## Judging a Placement

The hardest part of trad is judging whether a placement is good. The rock must be solid, not loose, hollow, or flaky; the gear must be the right size and well seated; and it must be oriented to take the expected direction of pull. Climbers learn to assess these factors quickly and to place gear before the moves get hard, not after. A bad placement gives false confidence; honest judgement about gear is what separates safe trad climbing from dangerous.

## Building a Rack and Learning Safely

A trad rack is built from a range of cams and nuts covering the crack sizes of your chosen routes, plus slings, carabiners, and a nut tool. Beginners should learn from experienced mentors or instructors, practising placements on the ground and on top-rope before ever trusting gear on lead. Trad is a serious discipline where mistakes carry real consequences, so build skill and judgement gradually and never lead beyond your ability to protect yourself.
