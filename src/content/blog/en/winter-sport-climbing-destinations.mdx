When winter shuts down climbing across the cold north, climbers head for warmer, sunnier rock where the cool season brings the best conditions of the year. From the limestone of southern Spain to the cliffs of the Mediterranean and the deserts of the American Southwest, a whole circuit of winter sport climbing destinations offers dry, cool, friction-rich climbing while the home crags sit under snow. This guide tours the great winter sport climbing venues and what makes them special. Explore these destinations on the [map](/map).

## Why Winter Means South

For climbers in cold northern climates, winter is the off-season at home — too cold, snowy, or wet to climb on local rock. But in warmer regions, winter is prime time: temperatures that are scorching in summer become perfect in the cool months, with crisp air, high friction, and dry, stable weather. So climbers travel south and toward the sun, chasing the cool-season window when the great southern crags are in their best condition. The winter climbing migration is one of the rhythms of the climbing year.

## Spain: The Winter Capital

Spain is the heart of European winter sport climbing, with a wealth of limestone areas that come into their own in the cool months. The Costa Blanca around Alicante offers sun-warmed crags ideal for winter, while inland and to the north are world-class venues. Above all, [Spain](/explore/spain) is home to El Chorro and the legendary cave of Margalef and Siurana in Catalonia, dense with hard routes on superb limestone. With reliable winter sun, cheap travel, and endless rock, Spain draws climbers from across Europe through the cold months.

## The Mediterranean Arc

Beyond Spain, the wider Mediterranean offers a string of winter venues. Kalymnos, the Greek island famous for its tufa-streaked limestone, has a long season that stretches into the cool months and is a beloved winter destination. Southern Italy, Sicily, Sardinia, and the crags of southern France and the Turkish coast all offer winter or shoulder-season sport climbing on sun-warmed Mediterranean limestone. These venues combine good rock, mild winter weather, and the appeal of a sunny escape from the northern cold.

## The American Southwest and Beyond

In North America, climbers escape winter by heading to the desert Southwest and the warm south. Areas like Red Rock near Las Vegas, the crags of Arizona, and the limestone of Texas offer dry, cool winter climbing, while further afield, destinations like Mexico's El Potrero Chico provide warm-season sport climbing through the northern winter. Across the world, climbers seek out the latitudes and altitudes where winter brings the dry, cool conditions that make for great sport climbing.

## Planning a Winter Trip

A winter climbing trip rewards planning. Climbers choose a destination by the conditions they want — sun-warmed crags for cold spells, shadier ones for warm ones — and time the trip to the local season. They check that the area is genuinely in condition in winter, plan for short daylight and the occasional storm, and build flexibility for weather. A well-planned winter trip can deliver the best climbing of the year, trading a snowed-in home crag for warm rock and long days of sport climbing in the sun.

## The Winter Circuit

For many dedicated climbers, the winter sport climbing circuit is a highlight of the year: a migration to warm, sunny rock, often shared with a community of climbers from across the cold north doing the same. Whether it is a few weeks in Spain, a trip to Kalymnos, or a desert road trip, chasing winter sun keeps climbers climbing year-round and opens up some of the world's best sport venues at their finest. Winter need not mean a break from the rock — just a change of address.
