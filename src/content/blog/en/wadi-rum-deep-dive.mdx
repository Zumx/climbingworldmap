In the far south of Jordan, the desert floor of Wadi Rum runs red beneath sandstone mountains that rise like ships becalmed in sand. Jebel Rum, Jebel Um Ishrin and their neighbours lift domes and prows up to 500 metres above the valley, seamed with cracks, chimneys and water-carved corridors. T. E. Lawrence called this place vast, echoing and god-like, and the climbing matches the rhetoric: adventurous, route-finding-heavy and unforgettable. Uniquely, the first ascensionists were not Europeans with ropes but Bedouin hunters, whose barefoot ibex-hunting lines thread improbable weaknesses that modern climbers still follow with respect and a rack of cams.

## A protected desert of towers

Wadi Rum is a protected area and UNESCO World Heritage site, managed from the visitor centre at its entrance, with the village of Rum sitting at the foot of Jebel Rum itself. The sandstone stands on granite plinths, and millennia of erosion have carved the massifs into labyrinths: summit plateaus split by hidden siqs, giant bridges like Burdah Rock Arch, and canyons whose walls glow orange at dawn and crimson at dusk. Climbers share the desert with camels, 4x4 tours and Bedouin camps, but a ten-minute walk past any roadhead restores total silence.

## The Bedouin routes

The desert's most distinctive climbing is on the Bedouin routes — lines like Hammad's Route and Sheikh Hamdan on Jebel Rum, pioneered generations ago by hunters moving barefoot and ropeless to high ibex terrain. They weave across slabs, through chimneys and along ledge systems with an instinct no topo fully captures, typically at moderate technical grades but with serious commitment: loose rock, scant fixed gear and descents that demand as much skill as the climbing. Done with a local guide or solid adventure experience, they are among the most profound mountain days in the world — climbing as inherited knowledge.

## Modern classics and big walls

From the 1980s onward, visiting climbers — led by pioneers like Tony Howard, who mapped the area's potential — added hundreds of modern routes. Trad lines of world renown now lace the walls: the soaring crack systems of the east face of Jebel Rum, test-pieces like Inshallah Factor, and elegant towers across the valley. Most routes are protected by cams and nuts in soft, sometimes sandy rock that demands careful placements and gentle feet. Multi-pitch outings of ten or more pitches are normal, abseil descents are often complex, and an early start buys shade and margin.

## Barrah Canyon and the crack-climbing heartland

A 4x4 ride east of Rum village, Barrah Canyon gathers some of the desert's most celebrated pitches in one sculpted corridor. Merlin's Wand, a laser-cut finger-and-hand crack of around five pitches, is the most famous line in Wadi Rum, and its neighbours offer everything from friendly jamming to fierce off-widths. The canyon floor makes a natural camp, and parties often spend days here, climbing morning and evening and sheltering from the midday sun. For climbers raised on indoor walls, Barrah is the desert's best classroom in pure crack technique.

## Living in the desert: camps, guides and culture

Logistics in Wadi Rum run through the Bedouin community. Most climbers stay in Rum village guesthouses or desert camps, arrange 4x4 transfers to distant massifs and hire local guides for the Bedouin routes — both a safety choice and a way of keeping climbing income in the valley. Hospitality is generous; accept the tea. Respect conservative dress norms in the village, ask before photographing people, and remember that the desert is a working landscape of herders and camps, not a playground. Entry permits are handled at the visitor centre, and overnight wall bivouacs should be discussed with your hosts.

## Seasons, water and desert hazards

The climbing seasons are spring, roughly March to mid-May, and autumn, October to November, when days are warm, nights cool and the rock at its best. Summer is brutally hot; winter brings short days, cold nights and occasional rain that can render sandstone fragile — let the rock dry fully before climbing. Carry far more water than feels reasonable, protect yourself from sun, and treat route-finding seriously: many epics here end with benightment on a descent, not a fall. A headtorch, warm layer and humility belong on every harness in Wadi Rum.

## Beyond the ropes

Rest days in Wadi Rum hardly feel like rest. Scrambling outings cross the massifs to Burdah Arch and the summit of Jebel Um ad Dami, Jordan's highest point at 1,854 metres on the Saudi border; camel treks and canyon walks fill quieter days. Most visitors pair the desert with Petra, two hours north, and the Red Sea reefs of Aqaba an hour south — a trio that makes Jordan one of the most rewarding adventure destinations anywhere. Many climbers plan a week and stay a month; the desert has that effect.
