The rope is the literal lifeline of roped climbing, the dynamic cord that catches a falling climber and links partners together. Yet for such a critical piece of gear, ropes come in a confusing array of types, diameters, lengths, and treatments. Choosing the right rope depends on what and where you climb: a single rope for sport cragging is a very different tool from the half ropes of an alpine route. Understanding the categories and the key specifications lets you choose a rope that is safe, appropriate, and durable. This guide explains the essentials. ## Dynamic vs Static Ropes

The first distinction is dynamic versus static. Dynamic ropes stretch under load, absorbing the energy of a fall to reduce the force on the climber and the protection — this elasticity is essential for any rope a climber might fall onto. Static ropes barely stretch and are used for rappelling, hauling, ascending fixed lines, and rescue, never for catching lead falls. Using a static rope where a fall is possible would transmit dangerous forces to the body and the anchor.

## Single Ropes

A single rope is designed to be used on its own, the standard choice for sport climbing, gym climbing, and much trad. It is simple to manage, with one strand to clip and belay, and modern single ropes range from about 8.9mm to 10.5mm in diameter. Thinner singles are lighter for redpointing and long routes; thicker ones are more durable and easier to handle, suiting beginners and high-wear top-roping. The single rope is the workhorse of everyday climbing.

## Half and Twin Ropes

For alpine, ice, and wandering trad routes, climbers use two thinner ropes. Half ropes (marked ½) are clipped into alternating pieces of protection, reducing rope drag on wandering lines and allowing full-length rappels. Twin ropes (marked ∞) are clipped together through every piece, like a single rope split in two. Both systems provide redundancy and full-length abseils, at the cost of more complex handling. They are specialist tools for the mountains rather than the sport crag.

## Diameter and Weight

Rope diameter is a trade-off between weight, durability, and handling. Thinner ropes (around 9.0–9.5mm) are lighter, ideal for long routes and redpoint attempts where every gram counts, but they wear faster and can be harder to grip in a belay device. Thicker ropes (9.8mm and up) are more durable and confidence-inspiring for top-roping and learning, but heavier. Matching diameter to use — and to a compatible belay device — is an important part of the choice.

## Length and Dry Treatment

Rope length matters because you must reach the anchor and be lowered safely. Sixty metres was long the standard, but many modern crags have long routes requiring 70m or even 80m ropes; always check the guidebook and tie a stopper knot in the end. Dry treatment is a coating that resists water absorption, essential for ice, alpine, and wet conditions, where a soaked rope becomes heavy, weak, and prone to freezing. For dry sport cragging it is an optional extra.

## Care and Retirement

A rope is a safety-critical item with a finite life. Keep it clean and out of dirt and grit, which abrade the fibres from within, and away from sharp edges, chemicals, and prolonged UV. Inspect it regularly for flat spots, core damage, or sheath wear. Ropes should be retired after a major fall factor, visible damage, or simply age, even if unused. When in doubt, retire it — no rope is worth a life. Always follow the manufacturer's guidance on lifespan.
