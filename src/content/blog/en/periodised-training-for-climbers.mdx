Periodisation is the practice of structuring training into distinct phases, each with a different focus, to develop different qualities in sequence and peak when it matters. Borrowed from other sports and adapted for climbing, it is how serious climbers break through plateaus, build a broad base of fitness, and arrive in top form for a trip or a project. While not every climber needs a formal plan, understanding periodisation helps anyone train more purposefully. This guide explains how periodisation works and how climbers apply it. ## Why Periodise

Training the same way all the time leads to plateaus: the body adapts to a stimulus and stops improving, while the relentless stress raises injury risk. Periodisation addresses this by varying the focus over time, developing different qualities in phases and allowing the body to recover and adapt. It also lets a climber peak — to be in top form at a chosen time, such as a trip or a project window — rather than being permanently fatigued or never quite sharp. Structure, in short, beats endless undirected effort.

## The Classic Phases

A classic periodised plan moves through phases that build on one another. A base phase develops general fitness, technique, and a high volume of climbing to prepare the body. A strength phase, often including hangboarding, builds maximum finger and pulling strength. A power phase, sometimes with campus or limit bouldering, develops explosive strength. A power-endurance phase trains the ability to sustain hard effort. Finally, a performance or peaking phase, with rest and focused climbing, brings the climber into top form for their goal.

## Building the Base

The base phase is the foundation everything else rests on. It emphasises a high volume of climbing at moderate intensity, refining technique, building general fitness and endurance, and conditioning the body — including tendons and antagonist muscles — to handle the harder training to come. Skipping or rushing the base phase, in eagerness to train hard, is a common mistake that leads to injury and undermines later phases. A solid base makes the rest of the plan effective and safe.

## Strength, Power, and Power-Endurance

The middle phases build the specific physical qualities of hard climbing in sequence. Maximum strength comes first, developed when fresh, often through hangboarding and limit climbing. Power builds on that strength, training the explosive recruitment of force. Power-endurance then trains the ability to apply that strength repeatedly before pumping out, the quality that long, hard routes demand. Sequencing these phases, each building on the last, develops a complete and peaked physical capacity.

## Peaking and Performance

The final phase brings everything together for the goal. Training volume drops, intensity stays high but fresh, and the focus shifts to performance climbing — projecting, redpointing, or a trip. Adequate rest lets accumulated fatigue clear so the climber arrives sharp and strong rather than tired. Timing this peak for the right moment is the art of periodisation, ensuring the months of structured work pay off exactly when it counts.

## Periodisation in Practice

Not every climber needs a rigid, formal periodised plan; many improve simply by climbing a lot with attention to recovery. But the principles of periodisation — varying the focus, building in phases, allowing recovery, and timing a peak — help any climber train more effectively and avoid the plateau of always doing the same thing. Applied flexibly and sensibly, periodisation turns training from undirected effort into a structured path toward your climbing goals.
