The Dolomites rise from the Italian Alps in walls and towers of pale limestone, among the most beautiful mountains on Earth. They hold legendary multi-pitch rock climbing dating back over a century, and they are the spiritual home of the via ferrata — the network of protected climbing paths, equipped with cables and ladders, that allow non-technical climbers to travel through vertical mountain terrain. The combination of dramatic rock and accessible adventure makes the Dolomites unique. Find them on the [map](/map?country=it).

## The Setting

The Dolomites are a range of pale limestone massifs — the Tre Cime di Lavaredo, the Marmolada, the Sella, the Civetta, and many more — rising in dramatic walls, towers, and pinnacles above green valleys and meadows. The light on the pale rock at sunrise and sunset, the famous enrosadira alpenglow, is part of what makes these mountains so beloved. The setting is both grand and accessible, served by mountain huts and a deep alpine culture.

## The Rock and the Climbing

Dolomite limestone is steep, pale, and dramatic, holding legendary multi-pitch routes. The great walls — the north faces of the Tre Cime, the Marmolada, the Civetta — hold some of the most historic climbs in alpinism, including early hard free climbs and bold modern routes. The rock can be both superb and loose, and the climbing demands route-finding skill and respect for the serious mountain environment. The towers and walls have shaped the history of European climbing.

## The Via Ferrata

The Dolomites are the home of the via ferrata, the "iron path." These protected routes, equipped with steel cables, rungs, and ladders, allow climbers to travel through steep and exposed mountain terrain with the security of clipping into a cable. Many date from the First World War, when they were built for military movement, and today they offer a way for a huge range of people to experience vertical mountain adventure. The Dolomite ferrate are the finest and most extensive network in the world.

## History and the Great Walls

The Dolomites hold a central place in climbing history. The great north faces saw some of the boldest early ascents in the Alps, and later the development of hard free climbing on the Marmolada and elsewhere. Legendary climbers made their names here, and the walls remain testing grounds for modern alpinists. This deep history, combined with the beauty of the rock, gives the Dolomites their special status.

## Season and Conditions

The Dolomites are a summer destination. The high rock routes and the via ferrata come into condition once the snow has cleared, typically from June to September, when the weather is most settled. Afternoon thunderstorms are a serious mountain hazard, and the high terrain demands respect for changeable conditions. The mountain hut network allows multi-day adventures, and the summer alpine season offers long days in spectacular surroundings.
