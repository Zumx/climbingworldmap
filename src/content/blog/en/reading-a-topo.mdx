A topo — short for topographical diagram — is the map of a climb, a drawing or annotated photo that shows the line a route takes up the rock, its features, its protection, and the belay stations. Reading a topo is an essential skill, especially on multi-pitch routes where finding the way is part of the challenge. A clear topo, well read, lets you follow a complex route through a maze of rock; a misread one can lead you off-route into danger. This guide explains how to read a topo and use it to climb. ## What a Topo Shows

A topo represents a route or a section of cliff in diagram form, showing the line the climb follows from bottom to top. It marks the key features — cracks, corners, roofs, ledges — that define the route, the position of belay stations on a multi-pitch climb, and often the protection, the grade of each pitch, and notable holds or moves. The topo distils the route into a readable map, letting a climber see the whole line before committing to it.

## Lines and Pitches

The central element of a topo is the line showing where the route goes. On a multi-pitch route, the line is broken into pitches, each marked with its grade and length, and the belay stations between them. Reading the line means following it from the start, pitch by pitch, understanding where each one goes and where it ends at a belay. Matching this line to the actual rock in front of you is the heart of route-finding on a big climb.

## Common Symbols and Notations

Topos use a set of conventions to convey detail. Belay stations are usually marked with a circle, dot, or anchor symbol; the grade of each pitch is noted alongside it; arrows or dashes show the direction of travel and traverses. Features like roofs, chimneys, and cracks have their own representations, and notations may flag bolts, gear, or hazards. As with guidebooks, every topo has its conventions, so checking the key and understanding the symbols comes first.

## Reading Topos for Multi-Pitch Routes

Topos are most vital on multi-pitch routes, where the way is rarely obvious and a wrong turn can be serious. Before each pitch, the climber studies the topo to understand where the line goes, where the next belay is, and what features and protection to expect. On the wall, they match the topo to the rock — using the position of cracks, roofs, and ledges — to stay on route. Good topo-reading keeps a team moving confidently up a complex face.

## Matching the Topo to the Rock

The crucial skill is translating the diagram into the three-dimensional reality of the cliff. A topo is a simplification, and the rock is always more complex and confusing in person. Climbers learn to identify the distinctive features shown on the topo — a prominent roof, a particular crack, a notable ledge — and use them as landmarks to orient themselves and confirm they are on the right line. This back-and-forth between diagram and rock is a skill honed over many routes.

## When Topos Mislead

Topos are guides, not guarantees. They can be inaccurate, out of date, or simply hard to match to confusing rock, and routes can have variations or be obscured by features the topo simplifies. Good climbers use the topo as one source of information alongside their own judgement, the look of the rock, and signs of passage like chalk or fixed gear. Treating the topo as a helpful map rather than infallible truth keeps you safe when the rock and the diagram do not quite agree.
