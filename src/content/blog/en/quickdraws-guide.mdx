The quickdraw is the small but essential piece of gear that connects the rope to the protection on a climb. Made of two carabiners joined by a fabric sling, it lets the leader clip the rope into a bolt or piece of gear with a single quick motion — hence the name. Quickdraws are so ubiquitous that climbers rarely think about them, yet the differences between types matter for safety and performance, and using them correctly is fundamental. This guide explains how quickdraws work, the variations, and how to choose and clip them well. Explore sport climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Anatomy of a Quickdraw

A quickdraw has three parts: a top carabiner that clips to the bolt or gear, a bottom carabiner that takes the rope, and a sewn fabric dogbone connecting them. The bottom carabiner is usually held snugly in the dogbone by a rubber keeper so it stays oriented for easy clipping, while the top carabiner is left loose to move freely. The two carabiners are not interchangeable in use: the bolt-end may pick up burrs that could damage the rope, so each end keeps its job.

## Carabiner Gates

The carabiners on a quickdraw come with different gate types. Solid-gate carabiners are robust and durable; wiregate carabiners are lighter, less prone to freezing, and resist "gate flutter," where vibration momentarily opens the gate in a fall. Bent-gate carabiners on the rope end make clipping easier by guiding the rope into the basket. Understanding which gate goes where — and never clipping the rope into the bolt-end carabiner — is part of using draws correctly.

## Sport Quickdraws

Sport quickdraws are built for clipping bolts on demanding routes. They typically have a stiffer, wider dogbone that is easy to grab and stabilises the lower carabiner for quick, confident clipping. The carabiners are often robust, with deep baskets and smooth actions. Many sport climbers carry a set of identical draws for consistency, plus a couple of longer ones to reduce rope drag where the route wanders. Durability matters because sport draws take heavy use.

## Alpine and Trad Quickdraws

For trad and alpine climbing, lighter and more versatile draws are favoured. The alpine, or extendable, quickdraw uses a long sling (a 60cm sling tripled up) that can be extended to its full length to reduce rope drag on wandering routes and over edges. Lightweight wiregate carabiners keep the weight down for long days. The ability to extend the draw is invaluable when placing gear in features that do not line up, keeping the rope running straight.

## Choosing Quickdraws

The right draws depend on your climbing. For sport, choose robust, easy-to-handle draws with a comfortable dogbone and smooth carabiners, in a length around 10–12cm, plus a few longer ones. For trad and alpine, favour lightweight, extendable alpine draws. Beginners need only a modest set to start, building up as they climb more. As with all gear, buy from reputable manufacturers and ensure everything is rated and certified for climbing.

## Clipping and Care

Clipping correctly is essential: the rope must run from the wall side up and out through the bottom carabiner, never back-clipped, and never with the gate against the rock where it could be pushed open. Check that draws are not cross-loaded or twisted over an edge. Inspect carabiners for sharp burrs and worn gates, and replace dogbones that are faded or frayed. Like all soft goods, slings degrade over time and should be retired according to the manufacturer's guidance.
