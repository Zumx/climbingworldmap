In the southern flank of Morocco's High Atlas, near the oasis town of Tinerhir, the Todra River has sliced a narrow gorge through the mountains, leaving limestone walls that soar nearly 300 metres above a streambed barely wide enough for a road. The light is extraordinary here, the rock glows orange and grey, and the climbing begins within steps of the parked car. Add palm groves, Berber villages, and the silence of the desert, and Todra becomes one of North Africa's most atmospheric climbing destinations. Find it on the [map](/map).

## The Gorge and Its Setting

Todra lies at the head of a valley that climbs from the date palms of Tinerhir into the bare flanks of the High Atlas. The gorge proper is a slot canyon where the walls close to a few dozen metres apart, the road and river threading between them. Beyond the narrows the valley opens to villages, terraced fields, and gravel slopes. The contrast of green oasis below and stark limestone above defines the place. Climbers stay in small hotels at the mouth of the gorge, several built right against the rock, with the crags a short walk from the door.

## The Limestone

The rock is solid, compact limestone, grey where it is sheltered and orange-streaked where water and sun have worked it. Angles range from slabby beginner walls to steep, pumpy faces hung with tufas. Sectors such as Central Rivers, Lower Rivers, and Upper Rivers, named for their position along the gorge, host the bulk of the routes, while areas like Petite Gorge offer gentler climbing. The friction is good and the holds are positive, though some popular lines have polished over the years. With hundreds of routes, the gorge suits a wide span of grades and styles.

## Classic Routes and Sectors

Todra is mostly a sport-climbing venue, with bolted single pitches and a handful of longer multi-pitch lines on the higher walls. Named routes like Le Passage, Into the Wild, and Neverland have become trade-route favourites, and sectors carry an international flavour, with French, Spanish, and even Polish names left by visiting teams over the decades. The grading is honest and the protection generally good, but climbers should still carry a thorough rack of quickdraws and check each line, as fixed gear quality varies. Easier walls offer plenty for those building experience, including stretches locally tagged escalade facile.

## The Multi-Pitch Adventure

Above the roadside crags rise the full gorge walls, where committing multi-pitch routes climb 200 metres or more to the rim. These are serious outings, with route-finding, loose sections in places, and long abseil descents, and they reward parties with alpine experience. From the top the view stretches across the High Atlas, with the great peaks of the range, including the Toubkal massif far to the west, hinting at Morocco's bigger mountains. For climbers wanting a taste of adventure rather than pure sport cragging, the long walls of Todra deliver height, exposure, and a genuine summit feeling.

## Season, Climate, and Conditions

Todra sits high and dry, and the climate swings hard. Summer days are fierce, with the gorge baking under the desert sun, so the best seasons are autumn and spring, roughly October to April, when days are warm and nights cool. Winter can bring genuine cold and even snow on the surrounding peaks, but sun-facing walls stay climbable. The gorge offers both sun and shade depending on the time of day, letting climbers chase conditions. Water in the river runs low for much of the year, and the air is bone-dry, which keeps the rock in fine shape.

## Berber Hospitality and Local Life

Part of Todra's charm lies beyond the climbing. The valley is Amazigh, or Berber, country, and life revolves around the palm groves, terraced fields, and herding. Small family-run hotels and gîtes welcome climbers, serving tagine and mint tea, and the pace is slow and warm. The nearby town of Tinerhir is the supply hub, and the wider region around Ouarzazate and the Dadès Gorge offers more to explore on rest days. Respecting local customs, dressing modestly away from the crag, and supporting village businesses are part of climbing here responsibly.

## Getting There and Logistics

Most climbers reach Todra by road from Marrakech or Fès, a long but scenic drive over the High Atlas passes, often via Ouarzazate. A hire car gives the freedom to combine Todra with the Dadès valley and the desert beyond, though buses and grands taxis also serve Tinerhir. There is no dedicated climbing shop in the gorge, so bring all your own gear, including a generous draw count and a rope of at least 70 metres for some routes. Guidebooks and topos cover the main sectors, and local guesthouses can point you to current conditions.
