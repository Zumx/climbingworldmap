South Korea is one of Asia's most surprising climbing destinations, a mountainous country where superb granite rises directly above a city of ten million. The crown jewel is Bukhansan National Park on the northern edge of Seoul, where the soaring dome of Insubong offers some of the finest multi-pitch granite in East Asia, all reachable by subway and a hike. Beyond the famous peaks, Korea has a deeply rooted alpine tradition and an exploding indoor scene, with bouldering and lead gyms packed into every district of Seoul. The areas below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=kr).

## Bukhansan and Insubong

Bukhansan National Park, rising on the northern fringe of Seoul, is the heart of Korean rock climbing. Its centrepiece is Insubong, a clean granite dome reaching 810 m, whose sweeping faces hold classic multi-pitch routes on excellent rock. The friction is superb, the lines are long and committing, and the summit views sweep across the sprawling capital below. Reached by subway and a forested approach hike, Insubong is one of the most accessible great granite peaks anywhere, and a rite of passage for Korean climbers.

## Bukhansan's Other Peaks

Beyond Insubong, the granite of Bukhansan holds far more. The neighbouring peaks of Baegundae, the park's highest at 836 m, and Seoninbong offer slabs, cracks and multi-pitch routes across clean grey granite, with bolted sport lines and traditional gear placements alike. The dense network of routes, set in forested mountains above the city, makes the park a lifetime's worth of climbing within an hour of central Seoul. Weekend crowds are real, but the quality and convenience are unmatched.

## Dobongsan and the Seoul Granite

Adjoining Bukhansan, Dobongsan offers more of the same superb granite, with its own array of spires and walls. Together the two ranges form a vast granite playground on Seoul's doorstep, threaded with hiking trails and dotted with climbing routes. The Korean alpine tradition runs deep here, with generations of climbers honing their skills on these crags before heading to the greater ranges of the Himalaya, a heritage visible in the busy weekend scene and the well-worn approach paths.

## Beyond Seoul

Korea's climbing reaches well beyond the capital. The country's granite and volcanic rock appear in ranges across the peninsula, and Jeju Island in the far south adds coastal volcanic climbing in a milder maritime setting. Regional cities such as Daejeon and Gwangju have their own crags and a strong gym culture, and the mountainous spine of the country offers endless exploration for those willing to seek out the lesser-known venues away from the Seoul crowds.

## The Indoor Scene

South Korea has one of the most vibrant indoor climbing scenes in the world. Seoul alone holds dozens of gyms, from bouldering specialists to towering lead walls, with venues like The Climb in Yeonnam, the gyms of the Gangnam and Samsung districts, and outdoor competition walls such as the one at Yongma Falls Park. The indoor boom has fed a generation of strong young climbers, and for visitors the gyms are a superb wet-weather option and an easy way to meet local partners.

## Season, Access and Practicalities

Korean climbing is shaped by a sharply seasonal climate. Spring and autumn are the prime seasons, when the granite is dry and the temperatures cool and stable, with the brilliant autumn foliage of October making Bukhansan especially memorable. Summers are hot and humid with a monsoon, and winters are cold but can offer crisp climbing on sunny faces. Seoul's Incheon airport and superb public transport make access easy, with the major crags reachable by subway and a hike, no car required.
