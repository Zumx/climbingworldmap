New Zealand is a small country with mountains and rock far out of proportion to its size. The Southern Alps run like a glaciated spine down the South Island, holding the country's serious mountaineering, while a short drive away the limestone basins of Canterbury offer some of the finest bouldering in the southern hemisphere. Add sea cliffs, volcanic crags and a strong indoor scene, and a climber can move from a glaciated summit to a sun-warmed boulder in a single day. The scenery, from braided rivers to beech forest, is part of the reward.

## The Southern Alps and Aoraki / Mount Cook

The Southern Alps are the heart of New Zealand mountaineering. Aoraki / Mount Cook, the highest peak in the country at 3,724 metres, presides over a range of glaciated peaks that have trained generations of alpinists, including Sir Edmund Hillary before Everest. The climbing here is serious, with objective hazards of crevasses, rockfall and notoriously fast-changing weather. The Aoraki/Mount Cook National Park gives access to classic alpine routes, and the Plunket Dome and surrounding peaks offer rock and mixed climbing in a genuinely alpine setting.

## Castle Hill and the Limestone Boulders

In the high country of Canterbury, the limestone basin of Castle Hill, known to Māori as Kura Tāwhiti, is one of the world's great bouldering destinations. Hundreds of grey limestone boulders dot the tussock-covered slopes, holding problems of extraordinary quality across every grade. The Dalai Lama is said to have called it a spiritual centre, and climbers know it as sacred ground of a different kind. Nearby, Flock Hill and the Castle Rock climbing area extend the bouldering and add sport routes, making the whole region a destination for weeks of climbing.

## Wānaka, Diamond Lake and the Southern Lakes

The Southern Lakes region around Wānaka and Queenstown combines stunning scenery with excellent rock. Above Wānaka, Diamond Lake and the schist crags of the area give sport and trad climbing with views over the lake and mountains. The schist of the region is distinctive, often steep and technical, and the climbing community here is strong. This is also a gateway to the Mount Aspiring National Park, where the spectacular peak of Tititea / Mount Aspiring draws alpinists to one of the finest mountains in the country.

## Wharepapa South and North Island Sport

On the North Island, the volcanic and ignimbrite crags around Wharepapa South in the Waikato are the country's premier sport climbing destination. Crags such as Froggatt Edge and the surrounding sectors hold hundreds of bolted routes across a wide range of grades on excellent rock, and the area has long been the centre of North Island sport climbing. The milder North Island climate means the crags climb well for much of the year, and the region serves climbers from Auckland, Hamilton and beyond.

## Indoor Walls and the Urban Scene

New Zealand's cities support a lively indoor climbing culture. Auckland has Extreme Edge and the Faultline Climbing Gym, along with the playful Clip 'N Climb concept that originated in the country and spread worldwide. Christchurch has Boulder Co., and Hamilton has Extreme Edge Hamilton, while smaller centres like the HangDog Indoor Climbing Centre serve regional communities. These walls keep climbers strong through wet weather and feed a steady stream of new climbers toward the country's outdoor crags.

## Sea Cliffs and Volcanic Rock

Beyond the alpine and the boulders, New Zealand offers varied crag climbing. Around Christchurch, the volcanic rock of the Port Hills gives accessible local climbing, while sea cliffs and coastal crags around both islands add adventure. The volcanic plateau of the central North Island and the basalt around the cities provide further variety. This breadth means a climbing trip can combine very different styles, from committing alpine routes to roadside sport and lakeside bouldering.

## Seasons and Travel

The South Island alpine season runs through the summer months of December to February, when the weather is most settled, though Southern Alps conditions can change rapidly in any season. Castle Hill and the Canterbury boulders climb well in the cooler, drier months of autumn and spring, and even in winter on fine days. The North Island crags are mildest and can be climbed much of the year. Distances are manageable on a single island, but ferry or flight connects the two; Christchurch and Queenstown are natural bases for the southern climbing.
