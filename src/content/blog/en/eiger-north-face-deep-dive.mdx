Few mountain faces in the world are as instantly recognisable, or as feared, as the north face of the Eiger. Rising roughly 1800 metres of limestone and shattered rock above the meadows of Grindelwald in the Bernese Oberland, the Eiger Nordwand is a concave amphitheatre of loose rock, ice fields, and falling stones. From the railway terrace at Kleine Scheidegg, tourists once watched climbers struggle and die through telescopes, coffee in hand. This is the story of that wall and the men who learned to climb it. ## The Wall Itself

The Eiger stands 3967 metres high, the easternmost of the great trio that also includes the Mönch and the Jungfrau. While its other flanks are merely serious, the north face is something apart. Concave in shape, it gathers and channels rockfall and avalanches toward any climber on it, and its limestone is notoriously loose and friable. The wall faces north, so the sun rarely touches it, and it brews its own weather, with storms forming on the face even when the valley is clear. The Eiger means ogre, and the name has come to fit the wall perfectly.

## Why It Took So Long

By the 1930s the Alps had been climbed almost everywhere, but the great north faces remained the last great problems. The Eiger Nordwand was the most notorious of them all. Early attempts in 1935 and 1936 ended in tragedy. In 1935 two Bavarian climbers froze to death high on the wall at the spot now called the Death Bivouac. In 1936 a party of four perished in one of mountaineering's most harrowing episodes, when Toni Kurz died alone, hanging on a rope just out of reach of his rescuers after his three companions had already died. The face earned a grim reputation and the press began calling it the Mordwand, the murder wall.

## The Hinterstoisser Traverse

The 1936 attempt gave the wall one of its most famous features. Andreas Hinterstoisser pioneered a delicate tension traverse across a smooth, holdless slab to reach the first ice field. Crucially, on the retreat his party could not reverse the traverse because they had removed their fixed rope, leaving them stranded above it as the weather closed in. The Hinterstoisser Traverse remains a key passage on the route to this day, and the lesson of leaving a rope fixed across it was learned at terrible cost. It is one of several named obstacles that string together the only feasible line up the face.

## The 1938 First Ascent

The wall was finally climbed in July 1938 by a party of four: the Austrians Heinrich Harrer and Fritz Kasparek, joined on the face by the Germans Anderl Heckmair and Ludwig Vörg. Heckmair, the most experienced ice climber of the group, led the hardest sections, and the four roped together to summit after three days on the wall, surviving avalanches and a storm near the top. The route they forged, known as the Heckmair route, is still the classic line on the face. Harrer later wrote the definitive account, The White Spider, named for the distinctive ice field high on the wall.

## The Named Features

The Heckmair route reads like a litany of feared landmarks. Above the Hinterstoisser Traverse lie three great ice fields, climbed in succession. Between and above them sit the Death Bivouac, the Ramp, the Brittle Ledges, and the Traverse of the Gods, an airy ledge system leading to the most iconic feature of all: the White Spider. This is a star-shaped ice field whose radiating gullies funnel everything that falls from above, making it one of the most dangerous places on the route. Above the Spider, the Exit Cracks lead to the summit ice field and the top.

## The Mittellegi Ridge and Other Lines

The north face is not the only celebrated route on the Eiger. The Mittellegi ridge, the long and serrated east ridge, was first climbed in 1921 by the Japanese alpinist Yuko Maki with local guides, and it remains a coveted classic, with a small hut perched on its crest. The west flank offers the ordinary descent route. But it is the Nordwand that defines the mountain's reputation, and over the decades dozens of harder direct lines have been added to it, including the Japanese Direttissima and John Harlin's eponymous direct route, climbed in 1966 at the cost of Harlin's life.

## Speed and the Modern Era

In the twenty-first century the Eiger Nordwand has become a stage for speed climbing. What once took the 1938 party three days has been compressed dramatically by soloists climbing without the weight of bivouac gear. The Swiss climber Ueli Steck repeatedly broke the record on the Heckmair route, eventually descending below two and a half hours for the entire wall, climbing largely unroped at a pace that redefined what the face could be. Dani Arnold later traded the record back and forth with him. These ascents, watched from Kleine Scheidegg much as the tragedies once were, show how far technique and conditioning have come, even as the wall itself remains as loose and dangerous as ever.

## The Legacy in Books and Film

Few walls have produced such a literature. Harrer's The White Spider became a classic that shaped how generations imagined the face, and the 1936 tragedy has been retold again and again, including in the film North Face, which recreates the fate of Toni Kurz and the struggle of the German and Austrian climbers. The British author and mountaineer Joe Simpson wrote The Beckoning Silence about his own reckoning with the wall, and the stories of the Eiger have repeatedly reached far beyond the climbing world. That the railway and hotels at Kleine Scheidegg gave spectators a ringside seat made the dramas public in a way no other great north face experienced, sharpening the wall's grip on the popular imagination. To this day the name Eiger carries as much association with courage and loss as with pure climbing achievement.

## Visiting the Eiger

You do not need to climb to feel the wall's presence. The Jungfrau Railway tunnels directly through the heart of the Eiger, with a window in the rock at the Eigerwand station once used to evacuate stranded climbers. From Grindelwald, Wengen, and the resorts above Interlaken, the face fills the skyline. Kleine Scheidegg, the high pass and railway junction below the trio of Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau, remains the classic viewpoint, while the Lauberhorn ridge nearby hosts the famous downhill ski race each winter. To stand at Kleine Scheidegg and trace the Heckmair route with your eye is to understand at once why this wall haunted a generation.
