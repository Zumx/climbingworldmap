Towering above the Chamonix valley on the French side and Courmayeur on the Italian, Mont Blanc rises to 4,808 metres, the highest point in Western Europe and the cradle of modern mountaineering. Its first ascent in 1786 by Jacques Balmat and Michel Paccard is often taken as the birth of the sport itself. The mountain is a sprawling massif of granite spires, hanging glaciers, and snow domes, and its summit draws thousands every summer, even as its glaciers retreat and its routes grow more serious with the warming climate. ## The Massif and Its Setting

Mont Blanc is not a single peak but the high point of a vast massif straddling France, Italy, and Switzerland. From Chamonix, the Aiguilles — granite needles such as the Aiguille du Midi and the Aiguille du Chardonnet — frame the valley, while the summit dome itself is a broad expanse of snow and ice. The Aiguille d'Argentière and the spires above the Argentière glacier hold some of the finest mixed and ice climbing in the range. The town of Chamonix, with its cable cars, guides' office, and deep alpine history, is the natural base for the whole region.

## The Goûter Route

The Goûter route, sometimes called the voie royale, is the most popular line to the summit. From Les Houches it climbs by the Tramway du Mont-Blanc to the Nid d'Aigle, then up to the Tête Rousse hut before crossing the infamous Grand Couloir, a stretch swept by rockfall that has claimed many lives. Above lies the Goûter hut and a long snow climb over the Dôme du Goûter and the Bosses ridge to the summit. Though technically straightforward, the route demands fitness, acclimatisation, and an early start to clear the couloir before the day warms.

## The Trois Monts Route

The Trois Monts, or Three Mountains route, starts from the Aiguille du Midi cable car station and crosses the summits of Mont Blanc du Tacul and Mont Maudit before reaching the main peak. It is more committing than the Goûter, with steep ice slopes, crevasse hazard, and serac danger, but it avoids the rockfall of the Grand Couloir. Many climbers consider it the more aesthetic and serious of the two voies normales, and conditions on the steep traverse below Mont Maudit often decide whether parties succeed or turn back.

## The Great Classic Lines

Beyond the trade routes, Mont Blanc and its satellites offer some of the most storied climbing in the Alps. The Frêney pillars and the Brouillard ridge on the Italian side are committing high-mountain objectives, while the granite of the Aiguilles provides classic rock climbs of every grade. Routes like the Innominata ridge and the Peuterey integrale rank among the longest and most demanding lines in the western Alps. For alpinists, the massif is a lifetime's worth of objectives, from sunny granite cracks to serious mixed faces.

## Glaciers, Hazards, and the Changing Mountain

Mont Blanc is heavily glaciated, and its hazards are real: crevasses, serac collapse, rockfall, and rapid weather changes. The warming climate has made these dangers more acute, with the Grand Couloir more active and permafrost loss destabilising once-solid terrain. Authorities have at times limited numbers on the Goûter route to manage crowding and risk. Climbers must be equipped for glacier travel, skilled in crevasse rescue, and prepared to abandon a summit attempt when conditions turn, a discipline as important here as raw ambition.

## Season, Huts, and Logistics

The summer climbing season runs broadly from June to September, when the high huts — Tête Rousse, Goûter, and the Cosmiques near the Aiguille du Midi — are staffed and the routes are in condition. Reservations for the popular huts are essential and book out far in advance. Many climbers hire a Chamonix guide, both for safety and because the guides' deep knowledge of conditions is invaluable on a mountain that changes daily. Good acclimatisation, often on nearby 4,000-metre peaks, greatly improves the odds of a safe and successful summit.
