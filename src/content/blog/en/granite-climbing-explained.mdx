Granite is the great rock of climbing history, the stone of Yosemite's walls, Chamonix's spires, and countless ranges where the sport came of age. Formed deep underground from slowly cooled magma, it is hard, solid, and coarse-grained, offering superb friction and the clean cracks and features that define classic climbing. Granite rewards crack technique, footwork, and a head for big, committing terrain, and it ranges from delicate friction slabs to the world's greatest big walls. This guide explores the character of granite and how to climb it. Explore granite climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## How Granite Forms

Granite is an intrusive igneous rock, formed when magma cools slowly deep beneath the earth's surface, allowing large mineral crystals — quartz, feldspar, and mica — to grow. Over millions of years, erosion strips away the overlying rock and exposes the granite, which weathers into domes, walls, cracks, and spires. Its coarse crystalline structure gives it strength, roughness, and the friction climbers prize, while its tendency to fracture along straight planes creates the splitter cracks granite is famous for.

## The Character of Granite Climbing

Granite climbing is defined by its features: clean cracks of every size, corners and dihedrals, friction slabs, and crimpy or knobbly faces. Because granite fractures cleanly, it often offers continuous crack systems that take excellent natural protection, making it a paradise for trad climbing. Its rough texture gives outstanding friction for both hands and feet, though it can be hard on the skin. The rock is generally solid and trustworthy, which is part of why it suits long routes and big walls.

## Crack Climbing on Granite

Cracks are the soul of granite climbing. Granite splits along straight lines to form fissures of every width — finger cracks, hand cracks, fist cracks, offwidths, and chimneys — that the climber ascends by jamming and protects with cams and nuts. Mastering crack technique is essential to granite, and the great granite areas are crack-climbing meccas. The clean, parallel-sided cracks of granite both demand and reward the jamming skills that face climbing never teaches.

## Friction and Slabs

Where granite is not cracked, it offers friction climbing on slabs and faces. The rough crystalline surface gives remarkable grip, allowing climbers to stand on tiny ripples and smears that look impossible. Granite slabs, like the famous apron below Yosemite's walls, demand delicate footwork, balance, and trust in friction. This blend of crack and friction climbing means a granite climber must be a complete all-rounder, comfortable jamming a splitter one pitch and smearing a slab the next.

## Big Walls and Alpine Granite

Granite's solidity and clean lines make it the rock of big-wall and alpine climbing. The sheer walls of Yosemite's El Capitan, the granite towers of Patagonia, the spires of Chamonix and the Bugaboos all draw climbers to multi-day and committing ascents. Granite's reliable cracks take protection and anchors well, and its strength supports the soaring features that big walls demand. For the climber drawn to scale and adventure, granite offers the world's most iconic objectives.

## The Great Granite Destinations

Granite shapes climbing destinations across the globe. Yosemite is the spiritual home, with its walls, domes, and crack climbing. Chamonix and the Mont Blanc massif offer alpine granite; Patagonia's Fitz Roy and Cerro Torre are granite dreams; the Bugaboos, Squamish, the Sierra Nevada, and Cornwall's sea cliffs each give their own granite character. Each rewards the crack technique, footwork, and boldness that granite demands, and together they form a roll-call of climbing's greatest places.
