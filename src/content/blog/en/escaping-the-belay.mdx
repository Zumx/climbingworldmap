Picture the situation every multi-pitch climber should rehearse and hopes never to meet: your partner falls, is struck by rock or simply stops responding, and now hangs on a loaded rope while your brake hand is the only thing holding them. You cannot reach them, you cannot help them, and you cannot let go. The belay escape is the technique that gets you out of that trap — a sequence of friction hitches and releasable knots that transfers your partner's weight from your harness to the anchor, leaving you free to descend to them, ascend for help, or rig a rescue. It looks intricate; in truth it is a drill, learnable in an afternoon and retained through practice.

## When You Would Actually Need It

The belay escape is the gateway move of self-rescue. An injured leader hanging in space, a second knocked out by stonefall, a partner having a medical emergency mid-pitch — in each case the rescue cannot begin until the belayer's hands are free and the load is off their harness. It matters most in exactly the places help is slow: long mountain routes, sea cliffs, remote crags far from a road. Phone coverage and helicopter rescue have made the full scenario rarer, but rescue services themselves teach the skill, because the first half-hour belongs to the party on the wall, and a belayer welded to a loaded rope can do nothing with it.

## What You Need to Know First

Three building blocks make the escape possible, and they should be solid before you chain them together. First, friction hitches: the prusik or klemheist, tied with a cord loop you always carry, which can grip a loaded rope. Second, the munter hitch, which lets you belay or lower from a carabiner. Third, the mule-overhand combination, the releasable tie-off that locks a munter or a belay device dead while keeping the option of releasing it under load. None of these is exotic; all are core alpine knot craft. If any of them is shaky, drill it separately before attempting the full sequence.

## Step One: Free Your Hands

Everything starts with making the brake hand redundant. Keeping firm control, tie off the belay device with a mule knot around the rope's brake strand, finished with an overhand backup — the standard tie-off that holds the load yet can be released while weighted. If you are belaying directly off your harness, this single step transforms the situation: your partner now hangs from the locked device, not your grip. Take a breath. From here onward nothing is urgent enough to justify sloppiness, and each following step is backed up before the previous one is undone.

## Step Two: Transfer the Load to the Anchor

Now move the weight off your body. Tie a friction hitch on the loaded rope ahead of the device and connect it to the anchor's master point with a sling or cordelette, ideally through a munter-mule-overhand so this new attachment is itself releasable later. Slide the hitch snug, then gradually release your tied-off device until the friction hitch and anchor take the load. Your partner is now hanging from the anchor through the hitch. Watch the hitch as it loads — a neat, well-dressed prusik grips; a sloppy one slips — and keep the original brake strand in hand until you trust it.

## Step Three: Back It Up, Then Get Out

A friction hitch alone should never be the only thing holding a human. Before you remove yourself, back up the transfer: tie the brake-side rope directly to the anchor with a clove hitch or figure-eight on a bight, leaving a little slack so the hitch carries the load but the knot catches if it fails. Now you can untie your belay device, unclip from the system and stand free at the anchor. The load path runs from your partner through the friction hitch to the anchor, with a hard knot as insurance — and you, for the first time since the accident, have both hands and full freedom of movement.

## After the Escape: Choosing the Next Move

Escaping the belay is not the rescue; it is the doorway to it. With hands free, assess: can your partner respond? Are they breathing, bleeding, in further danger? The realistic options are to call for rescue and keep them stable, to descend to them by rappelling on a separate strand or counterbalance system, to lower them to a ledge, or — rarely and laboriously — to haul. Each branch is its own technique with its own course-worth of detail. The honest takeaway for most climbers: master the escape itself, carry a phone or communicator, and know that getting free and calling early beats an improvised solo rescue in almost every case.

## Mistakes That Teach

Certain errors appear every time this is taught. Rushing the tie-off and losing brake control for a moment — the one unforgivable slip. Building the friction hitch backwards so it slides when loaded. Forgetting the backup knot and trusting a single hitch with a life. Making the transfer with so much slack that the system shifts with a lurch, shock-loading the hitch. And the strategic error: never having practised, so that the first attempt happens with cold hands, real fear and a partner who needs help now. Every one of these is cured by rehearsal on the ground, where mistakes cost nothing and teach everything.

## Practise It Until It Is Boring

Set up a belay at ground level — a tree, a gym anchor, a basement beam — hang a pack or a patient friend on the rope, and run the full sequence: tie off, transfer, back up, escape. Time yourself. A practised climber completes it in under ten minutes; a first-timer can flounder for an hour. Repeat with the device in guide mode and off the harness, with one cord and with two, until the order of operations is automatic. Club courses and guide-run self-rescue days provide structure and correction, and pairing the drill with rope ascent and tandem rappel turns you into a genuinely self-sufficient partner.
