There is a visible difference between a climber fighting an overhang and a climber flowing up one, and most of it lives in the hips. The fighter faces the wall squarely, arms bent, front-on like a ladder climber, burning strength with every locked-off reach. The flowing climber turns sideways — hip pressed to the rock, one knee rotated downward, arm reaching from a twisted, stable frame. The two techniques behind that transformation are the twist lock and the drop knee, and together they are the energy-saving heart of steep climbing. They cost nothing but coordination, and they routinely make moves feel an entire grade easier.

## Why Square-On Fails on Steep Rock

Climbing face-on works fine on slabs and vertical walls, where the feet carry weight directly. Tilt the wall past vertical and the geometry turns hostile: a square-on body hangs from bent arms like a sack, the hips sag away from the rock, and every upward reach becomes a pull-up followed by a lock-off. Biceps and shoulders, small muscles by climbing standards, do work that should belong to the skeleton. Turning the hip into the wall changes everything at once — the arm straightens onto bone, the shoulder rotates closer to the hold, the reaching distance grows by many centimetres, and the feet load properly instead of skating.

## The Twist Lock

The twist lock is the basic rotation. As you reach with the right hand, you turn the left hip into the wall, outside edge of the left foot on its hold, body twisted so the chest faces sideways rather than out. The magic is in what the twist does to the reaching arm: rotation brings the shoulder several centimetres closer to the target, and the pulling arm locks close to the torso, where it is strongest — or better, hangs straight, letting the skeleton take the load. Done well, a twist lock turns a desperate dynamic slap into a calm, static reach, and a string of them turns a steep pitch into a series of restful positions connected by rotations.

## The Drop Knee

The drop knee — the Egyptian, or lolotte in French — is the twist lock's more dramatic sibling. Place one foot on a hold roughly level with or above the other, then rotate that knee downward until it points at the ground, hips swivelling into the wall. The position looks contorted and feels miraculous: the rotated leg becomes a strut, pressing outward between two footholds, jamming your body against the rock so firmly that the hands can relax. On steep terrain a good drop knee can take most of your weight off your arms entirely, which is why climbers hunt for them on overhanging routes like rest stops on a motorway.

## When Each Tool Fits

Twist locks are the default on any steep ground where you reach far: cheap, quick and repeatable on every other move. Drop knees demand more specific architecture — two opposing footholds at compatible heights, ideally with the route steep enough that the position pays for its setup time. They shine in corners and grooves, on tufa pillars, between widely spaced jugs in caves, and anywhere a clip or a long reach must be made from steep ground in control. The diagnostic question is direction of pull: when the next hold demands a stable, locked body rather than a quick slap, some rotation — modest twist or full drop knee — is almost always the answer.

## Protecting the Knee

The drop knee has one genuine cost: it puts the knee into deep rotation under load, the position in which menisci and ligaments are most vulnerable, and aggressive drop knees have injured climbers in the act of using them. The protection is technique and moderation. Rotate smoothly rather than snapping into position, avoid maximal drop knees when the foothold is high and the load extreme, and never bounce in the position. Build the supporting muscles — hamstrings, hip rotators, the muscles around the knee — with simple strength work. If a drop knee produces a sharp twinge, back off; the move has alternatives, and a meniscus tear does not.

## Learning the Rotation

The pattern is best built on easy steep terrain, exaggerated until it becomes natural. On a juggy overhanging gym wall, climb a route turning the hip deliberately into every reach: right hand reaches, left hip turns in, outside edge of the left shoe on the hold. Feel the difference between standing on the inside edge, square, and the outside edge, twisted — the same foothold becomes a different tool. For drop knees, find two opposing holds on a steep panel and practise rotating in and out slowly, noticing how the pressure between the feet lets the hands open. Ten sessions of deliberate exaggeration and the hips start turning on their own.

## The Footwork Underneath

Both techniques live or die on the feet. The twist lock requires trusting the outside edge of the shoe, a contact beginners rarely use, on footholds that face the wrong way for it. The drop knee requires precise placement — the rotating foot must be set before the knee turns, because adjusting mid-rotation is how feet pop. Strong toes and ankles that hold tension through rotation come from climbing on small footholds with intent. The reward for the investment is paid on every steep route you ever climb: positions where others hang and burn, you stand and rest.
