Climbing shoes are a significant and recurring expense, but with care and timely resoling, a good pair can last for years rather than months. The rubber wears down with use, especially at the toe, but the shoe itself often has plenty of life left, and a resole — replacing the worn rubber — can restore it for a fraction of the cost of new shoes. Understanding how to care for your shoes and when to resole rather than replace saves money and waste. This guide covers climbing shoe care and resoling. ## How Climbing Shoes Wear

Climbing shoes wear primarily at the toe, where the rubber takes the most punishment from edging, smearing, and toeing in. The sole rubber thins over time, and eventually the rand — the rubber wrapping the front of the shoe — begins to wear through. Catching this wear early is key: a shoe resoled before the rand is breached is a straightforward, durable repair, while one left until the rand is worn through is harder and more expensive to restore. Watching the toe is the secret to long shoe life.

## When to Resole

The right time to resole is when the sole rubber has worn thin but before it wears through to the rand. A common sign is the sole becoming smooth and thin at the toe, losing its edge and friction, while the rand is still intact. Resoling at this point is simple and restores the shoe well. Waiting too long, until the rand itself wears through, means a more involved and costly repair, and risks ruining the shoe entirely. Inspect your shoes regularly and resole proactively.

## How Resoling Works

A resole is done by a specialist cobbler who removes the worn sole rubber and bonds on a fresh piece, restoring the climbing surface. If the rand is worn, it can often be repaired too, though this is more involved. A good resoler can return a worn shoe to near-original performance at a fraction of the cost of replacement, and a quality shoe can be resoled several times over its life. Choosing a reputable resoler and the right rubber for your climbing matters to the result.

## Caring for Your Shoes

Good care extends shoe life and performance. Keep shoes clean and dry, letting them air out between sessions rather than leaving them stewing in a bag, which degrades the rubber and breeds odour. Avoid walking on abrasive surfaces like gritty ground, which wears the soles needlessly. Brush dirt off the soles, as grime reduces friction. Store shoes away from heat and sun, which degrade the rubber. These simple habits keep the rubber sticky and the shoes lasting longer.

## Getting the Fit and Feel Right

Resoling can subtly change a shoe's fit and feel, as new rubber may be slightly thicker or stiffer than the worn original. A good resoler matches the rubber thickness and type to keep the shoe performing as intended. Some climbers resole with a stiffer or grippier rubber to suit their climbing. Communicating what you want, and choosing a resoler who knows climbing shoes, ensures the resoled shoe feels right rather than subtly off.

## The Case for Resoling

Resoling is good for the wallet and the planet. New climbing shoes are expensive, and resoling costs a fraction of that, making a good pair far more economical over its life. It also reduces waste, keeping shoes out of landfill and extending the value of a quality product. For the cost-conscious and the environmentally minded alike, resoling a good pair of shoes, rather than throwing them away at the first sign of wear, is simply the sensible choice.
