The freedom to climb at a crag is something many climbers take for granted, yet it rests on a fragile web of landowner goodwill, legal arrangements, local agreements, and the everyday behaviour of climbers. Crags around the world are closed every year because of conflict, damage, or simple loss of permission, and many great climbing areas exist only thanks to the tireless work of access organisations and volunteers. Understanding how access works — and the part each climber plays in protecting it — is as important to the sport's future as any climbing skill. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Access Is a Privilege, Not a Right

Most crags sit on land owned by someone — a private landowner, a community, a government agency — and climbing there depends on their permission or tolerance. Even where laws grant a right to roam, that right comes with responsibilities and can be conditional. Climbers who treat access as an entitlement, ignoring rules or causing problems, put it at risk for everyone. The starting point of good stewardship is recognising that the ability to climb somewhere is a privilege that must be earned and maintained.

## How Crags Get Closed

Crags close for many reasons, most of them avoidable. Conflict with landowners over parking, trespass, noise, dogs, or litter is common. Environmental damage, erosion, and disturbance to wildlife can trigger closures by authorities. Liability concerns, new ownership, or a single serious incident can end access overnight. Sometimes the cause is simply too many climbers overwhelming a fragile place. Almost all of these causes trace back to climber behaviour, which means almost all are within our power to prevent.

## Access Organisations

Around the world, dedicated organisations work to secure and protect climbing access — bodies like the Access Fund in the United States, the BMC in Britain, and national alpine and climbing clubs across Europe. They negotiate with landowners, purchase or lease crags, fund trails and toilets, manage seasonal closures, and represent climbers to authorities. This work is largely invisible to the climber at the crag, but it underpins access everywhere. Joining and supporting these organisations is one of the most valuable things a climber can do.

## Respecting Local Rules

Every crag has its own rules and norms: where to park, which approach to use, when seasonal closures apply, whether dogs are allowed, and local ethics on bolting and chalk. These rules often exist because of hard-won agreements with landowners, and breaking them — even unknowingly — can jeopardise access. Before visiting a new area, climbers should research the local rules through guidebooks, access organisations, and online resources, and follow them scrupulously. Ignorance is not an excuse the landowner will accept.

## Being a Good Ambassador

Every climber is an ambassador for the sport in the eyes of landowners, neighbours, and the public. Parking considerately, greeting locals, keeping noise down, controlling dogs, picking up litter, and behaving respectfully all build the goodwill on which access depends. Conversely, a single climber behaving badly can undo years of careful relationship-building. The way climbers conduct themselves at and around the crag directly shapes whether they remain welcome.

## What You Can Do

Protecting access is a shared responsibility with concrete actions. Join your national access organisation and support its work. Follow all local rules and respect closures. Volunteer for trail days and crag clean-ups. Park and behave considerately. Educate newer climbers about stewardship. Report problems and damage. Give back through donations or labour to the maintenance that keeps crags open. Small contributions from many climbers are what keep the world's climbing accessible for the future.
