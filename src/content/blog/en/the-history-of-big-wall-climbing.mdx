Big wall climbing — ascending faces so large they take days, with the team living on the rock — was forged in the granite cathedral of Yosemite Valley. From the audacious first aid ascents of El Capitan in the 1950s and 60s, through the lightweight and free-climbing revolutions, to the landmark free ascent of the Dawn Wall, the history of big walls is a story of vision, technology, and the steady expansion of what climbers believed possible. This guide traces that history, from pitons and bolts to free climbing the steepest walls on earth. Explore big wall destinations on the [map](/map).

## The First Ascents of El Capitan

Big wall climbing came of age on El Capitan, the 900-metre granite monolith of Yosemite. In 1958, Warren Harding and his team made the first ascent of The Nose after a siege lasting many days over more than a year, using extensive aid, fixed ropes, and bolts. In 1961, Royal Robbins and his team climbed the Salathé Wall in better style, with less reliance on fixed ropes. These ascents proved the great walls could be climbed and established big-wall technique and ethics.

## Aid Climbing and the Wall Pioneers

The early big walls were climbed largely by aid, the climbers standing in slings on pitons, bolts, and other gear to make upward progress on rock too hard or blank to free climb. The Yosemite pioneers developed the systems — hauling, portaledges, multi-day logistics — that made living and climbing on a wall for days possible. This era forged the tools and tactics of big-wall climbing, and the rivalry and vision of climbers like Harding and Robbins shaped its ethics.

## The Free Climbing Revolution

As standards rose, climbers began to ask whether the great walls could be climbed free — using only hands and feet on the rock, with gear for protection alone. In 1988, Todd Skinner and Paul Piana made the first free ascent of the Salathé Wall, a landmark that opened the era of free big-wall climbing. Climbers increasingly sought to free routes first climbed by aid, transforming the great walls from aid objectives into free-climbing challenges of staggering difficulty.

## The Dawn Wall

The pinnacle of free big-wall climbing came in 2015, when Tommy Caldwell and Kevin Jorgeson completed the first free ascent of the Dawn Wall on El Capitan after years of effort. With pitches up to 5.14d on tiny, technical holds, it was hailed as the hardest big-wall free climb in the world and captured global attention far beyond climbing. The Dawn Wall represented the culmination of the free-climbing vision that had been pushing big walls for decades.

## Speed and Modern Big Walls

Alongside the push for difficulty came a push for speed. What once took weeks was compressed into days, then hours, as climbers refined their systems and fitness. The Nose speed record fell repeatedly, eventually under two hours, a staggering transformation of a route that once took a siege. Modern big-wall climbing spans this whole spectrum, from leisurely multi-day aid ascents to lightning free and speed ascents, all built on the foundations laid in Yosemite.

## Big Walls Beyond Yosemite

While Yosemite remains the spiritual home, big-wall climbing spread to the great walls of the world: the granite towers of Patagonia, the remote faces of Baffin Island and Greenland, the desert walls of the American Southwest, and the soaring rock of the Karakoram. Each demands the techniques, endurance, and commitment forged in Yosemite, and together they represent climbing at its most adventurous, the ongoing expansion of what is possible on the world's biggest rock.
