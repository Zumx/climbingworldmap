Rising from the dry Andes of western Argentina, Cerro Aconcagua reaches 6,961 metres, making it the highest mountain in the Americas and the highest summit anywhere outside Asia. It stands in the province of Mendoza, near the Chilean border, a vast pyramid of rock and ice surrounded by the parched valleys of the Provincial Park that bears its name. Unlike the technical giants of the Himalaya, Aconcagua's standard route is a long, high-altitude walk, but the thin air, fierce wind, and sheer scale make it a serious undertaking. ## The Mountain and Its Setting

Aconcagua dominates the Cordillera Principal, the main range of the Andes, roughly 110 kilometres west of the city of Mendoza. It is a massif rather than a single spire, with a long summit ridge linking the slightly lower south summit to the main north peak. The mountain sits within the Aconcagua Provincial Park, a protected area of high desert, glacial valleys, and braided meltwater rivers. The approach passes through stark, almost lunar terrain, with little vegetation above the valley floors, where the wind and altitude define every hour of the day.

## The Normal Route

The Northwest or Normal Route is the most travelled line to the summit and the reason Aconcagua draws thousands of aspirants each season. It begins in the Horcones valley, passing the base at Confluencia before reaching Plaza de Mulas, the main base camp at around 4,300 metres and one of the largest base camps in the world. From there the route climbs through a series of high camps — Canadá, Nido de Cóndores, and Cólera — before the long summit day. The final push crosses the Gran Acarreo scree and the dreaded Canaleta, a steep gully of loose rock that drains the strength of even fit climbers near the top.

## The Polish Glacier and Harder Lines

For those seeking a genuine mountaineering challenge, the eastern side offers the Polish Glacier Route, approached through the Vacas valley and the camp at Plaza Argentina. The original glacier line involves steep ice and real exposure, while the more common Polish Traverse skirts the glacier to join the upper Normal Route. The formidable South Face, one of the great alpine walls of the world, rises some 3,000 metres and is the preserve of elite alpinists. These harder routes transform Aconcagua from a high trek into a committing climb.

## Altitude and Acclimatisation

Aconcagua's danger lies not in difficulty but in altitude. The summit pushes well into the zone where acute mountain sickness, high-altitude pulmonary oedema, and cerebral oedema become real threats. Successful expeditions build in carry-and-return rotations, sleeping low and climbing high to let the body adapt over a typical two-to-three-week itinerary. Hydration, slow pacing, and patience with the weather matter more than raw fitness. Many strong climbers fail here not from the terrain but from underestimating how the body responds above 6,000 metres.

## Weather, Wind, and the Viento Blanco

The mountain's most feared adversary is the wind. The notorious viento blanco, the white wind, can arrive without warning, driving snow horizontally and dropping wind chill to lethal levels. Summit windows are dictated less by snow conditions than by the forecast for the high winds that scour the upper slopes. The climbing season runs from mid-November to late February, the southern summer, when the days are longest and the worst of the winter storms have passed, though even then a stable summit day is never guaranteed.

## Permits, Mendoza, and Logistics

Climbing Aconcagua requires a permit issued by the province of Mendoza, with fees varying by season and route, and the busy holiday weeks commanding the highest prices. Most expeditions stage from Mendoza city, a relaxed provincial capital famous for its Malbec wine, before driving to the trailheads at Penitentes or the Punta de Vacas. Mule trains carry gear to base camp, and a network of guiding operators, medical checks at base camp, and helicopter evacuation options has grown around the mountain. This infrastructure makes the peak accessible, but the responsibility for safe progress remains with the climber.
