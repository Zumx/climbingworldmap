Cyprus sits in the far eastern Mediterranean, closer to the Levant than to Greece, and its climbing follows the island's logic: limestone warmed by three hundred days of sun, sea cliffs above turquoise water and a season that peaks when most of Europe is buried in winter. The scene is small but growing fast, with bolted sport crags scattered across the Paphos hills, deep-water soloing at Cape Greco and the forested Troodos massif rising behind it all. For climbers chasing winter sun without the crowds of Kalymnos or El Chorro, Cyprus is one of the Mediterranean's best-kept secrets. The places below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=cy).

## Episkopi and the Paphos Crags

The crag above the village of Episkopi, in the Ezousa valley east of Paphos, is the spiritual home of Cypriot sport climbing. A long band of grey and orange limestone rises above the village, with bolted routes across the grades on pockets, tufas and technical walls. Route names here range from playful to heroic — lines like Batman, Angel Eyes and Aragon sit alongside harder pitches that have drawn visiting climbers from across Europe. The crag faces the afternoon sun, the approach is a few minutes from the village, and the view rolls down the valley to the sea.

## Cape Greco and the Sea Cliffs

At the island's southeastern tip, Cape Greco (Cavo Greko) National Forest Park serves up the island's most photogenic climbing. White limestone cliffs drop straight into clear water, and the area has become Cyprus's deep-water soloing playground, with low traverses and bolder lines above the sea caves. Above the waterline, bolted sport routes climb the compact cliff bands, and the nearby resort towns of Ayia Napa and Protaras make logistics simple. Climbing here in the morning and swimming from the same rock in the afternoon is the cape's standard itinerary.

## Droushia and the Akamas Hills

In the island's wild west, the hills around Droushia and Ineia on the edge of the Akamas peninsula hold quiet crags with long views over Chrysochou Bay. The limestone here is rougher and the atmosphere more remote: vineyards, goat bells and dirt roads rather than resorts. The Akamas itself, one of the last undeveloped corners of coastal Cyprus, rewards rest days with gorge walks through the Avakas Gorge and swimming at the Blue Lagoon. The crags suit climbers who want their winter sun with solitude attached.

## The Troodos Massif

Rising to 1,952 metres at Mount Olympus, the Troodos massif is the island's green heart — pine forests, Byzantine monasteries and, in most winters, enough snow for a small ski area. For climbers it offers cool-weather bouldering and scrambling, summer escapes from the coastal heat and a dramatic change of scenery: within an hour you can swap sea cliffs for mountain air. The diabase and gabbro of the high Troodos differ completely from the coastal limestone, and exploration here is still genuinely open.

## Style, Grades and the Local Scene

Cypriot sport climbing is bolted to modern standards, largely by a small and dedicated local community along with visiting equippers. Grades follow the French system and the spread favours the middle of the scale, roughly 5c to 7b, with harder projects appearing each season. The rock is mostly compact limestone with pockets and the occasional tufa, sharp in places where traffic is light. Local climbers are welcoming and easy to find through the island's gyms and social channels, and new sectors continue to appear as the scene matures.

## Seasons: Winter Sun at Its Best

The Cyprus season is the inverse of northern Europe's. From October to April conditions are excellent: daytime temperatures between 15 and 25 °C on the coast, dry rock and long, mild days. Midsummer is brutally hot at the coastal crags, though the Troodos stays climbable at altitude. Rain is scarce and short-lived even in winter, which is precisely the island's appeal — few European destinations offer such reliable January friction. Sea-cliff conditions at Cape Greco are best on calm days, so check the swell before planning a deep-water solo session.

## Practicalities

Cyprus is easy: two international airports at Larnaca and Paphos, cheap hire cars, English widely spoken and distances small — no crag is much more than two hours from either airport. Paphos makes the best base for Episkopi and the Akamas, while Ayia Napa serves Cape Greco. Note that the island remains divided; the crags described here lie in the Republic of Cyprus in the south. A 70-metre rope and a standard sport rack cover everything, and a swimsuit belongs in every crag pack.
