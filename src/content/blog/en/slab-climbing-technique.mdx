Slab climbing is the discipline of ascending rock that leans back from vertical, where the angle works with you but the holds are often tiny or absent. It is climbing's great humbler: powerful athletes who crush overhangs are frequently undone by a delicate slab, because slab is not about strength but about balance, friction, and trust in your feet. Mastering it teaches footwork and body awareness that improve every other style of climbing. This guide explains why slab is so distinct and how to move on it with confidence. ## What Makes Slab Different

A slab is rock at less than vertical, where gravity pulls you onto the rock rather than off it. This sounds easier than steep climbing, and on big holds it is, but slabs are often nearly featureless, demanding that the climber stand on minuscule edges, ripples, or pure friction. Where steep climbing rewards pulling power, slab rewards precise footwork, balance, and the nerve to commit weight to holds that look like nothing. It is a mental as much as a physical game.

## Trusting Your Feet

The cardinal rule of slab climbing is to trust your feet. Beginners instinctively hug the rock and grip with their hands, which pulls the body in and lifts weight off the feet, making them skate. The correct posture is to stand tall, keep the hips away from the rock, and drive weight directly down through the feet onto the holds. Climbing shoes have remarkable friction, and learning to believe in it — to stand on a smear that looks impossible — is the heart of slab climbing.

## Smearing and Edging

Slab footwork relies on two techniques. Edging places the edge of the shoe on a small hold or ripple, using the precise inner or outer edge. Smearing presses the sole flat against the rock to generate friction where there is no defined hold at all, relying entirely on the rubber's grip and the angle of the foot. On a featureless slab, smearing is everything: the climber chooses the best texture, sets the foot deliberately, and weights it smoothly without sudden movement.

## Body Position and Balance

Balance is the slab climber's currency. The body must stay centred over the feet, with weight directed straight down into the holds. Sudden, jerky movements break the delicate friction, so good slab climbing is slow, smooth, and deliberate. Climbers often keep their hands low and use them for balance rather than pulling, shifting weight from foot to foot in a controlled dance. Reading the sequence and finding rest positions where the balance is stable are key skills.

## The Mental Game

Slab climbing demands composure. Because the holds are often poor and the protection can be spaced, a slab can feel committing and exposed, and fear makes climbers grip harder and stand worse — the opposite of what works. The skill is to stay calm, breathe, trust the feet, and move with confidence. Slab falls can be ugly, scraping down the rock, so many climbers find slab more frightening than steep terrain despite the gentler angle. Confidence, built through practice, is the answer.

## Why Slab Makes You a Better Climber

Slab climbing forces good habits: precise foot placement, quiet feet, centred balance, and trust in friction. These transfer directly to every other style, making vertical and even steep climbing more efficient. Many great climbers credit time on slabs with building the footwork and body awareness that underpin their hardest ascents. Even if slab is not your favourite, deliberately practising it pays dividends across your whole climbing.
