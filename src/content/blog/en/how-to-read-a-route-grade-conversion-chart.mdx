Stand at the base of a route in Céüse and the topo says 7a. Fly to the Red River Gorge and the same difficulty is written 5.11d. Open an old Alpine guidebook and you meet a Roman numeral, VII+. None of these numbers is wrong; they are simply different languages describing the same physical effort. A grade conversion chart is the dictionary that lets you move between them, and reading one well is a skill every travelling climber eventually needs. This guide explains what each system measures and how to translate between them without nasty surprises.

## What a Grade Actually Measures

A free-climbing grade describes the difficulty of the hardest required moves on a route, climbed without resting on gear. It is a consensus opinion, refined by many ascents, not a precise physical measurement. Grades bundle together steepness, hold size, spacing and the subtle question of how much technique is needed. Because they are subjective, two routes of the same number can feel quite different, and a chart can only ever give an approximate equivalence. Treat conversions as a guide, not gospel, especially near the boundaries between numbers.

## The French System

The French sport grade is the most widely used worldwide for bolted routes. It runs from easy starts around 3 and 4 up through 5, 6, 7, 8 and into 9, with each number after 6 split by letters a, b and c, and sometimes a plus. So the sequence climbs 6a, 6a+, 6b, 6b+, 6c, 6c+, 7a and onward. The current frontier sits at 9c, held by routes such as Silence in Flatanger, Norway. The French grade describes the overall difficulty of a pitch, factoring in length and sustained effort, not just the single hardest move.

## The American YDS

The Yosemite Decimal System, used across North America, looks unusual to outsiders. Technical rock climbing all sits in the fifth class, written 5.x. The numbers run 5.0 up to 5.9, then continue 5.10, 5.11, 5.12 and beyond, with each of these higher grades split by letters a to d. So 5.10a is harder than 5.9, and 5.12d is harder than 5.12a. The decimal is not a fraction: 5.10 is read five-ten, not five-point-one. The system tops out for now around 5.15d, the YDS equivalent of French 9c.

## The UIAA Scale

The UIAA scale, common in Germany, Austria and across the Eastern Alps, uses Roman numerals with plus and minus modifiers: V, V+, VI-, VI, VI+ and so on, now reaching into the XII range. It was historically the standard for Alpine and trad routes, and you still meet it in older guidebooks and on classic mountain faces. Roughly, UIAA VI corresponds to French 5c and YDS 5.9, while UIAA VIII matches French 6c. Because each numeral covers a fairly wide band, conversions to and from UIAA are among the least precise.

## British, Saxon and Other Systems

The British trad system is famously double-barrelled: an adjectival grade for overall seriousness (Severe, Very Severe, E1, E2 and upward) paired with a technical grade (4a, 5b, 6a) for the hardest move. The two together convey both how hard and how dangerous a route is, something no single number captures. Saxon grading in the Elbsandstein around Dresden, Scandinavian numbers, and the Brazilian and Australian Ewbank systems add further variety. A good chart lines these columns side by side so you can read across from any one to the others.

## How to Use a Conversion Chart

Read a chart as a set of horizontal bands, not exact one-to-one matches. Find your known grade in its column, then read straight across to the system you need. Pay attention to the seams: French 6a sits near YDS 5.10a and UIAA VI+, but a stiff 6a can feel like 5.10b. When in doubt, round toward the harder option and warm up below your limit. Remember that boulder problems use entirely separate scales, the Fontainebleau and V-scales, which a route chart will not cover.

## Reading Local Consensus

The most useful skill is calibrating to a venue. Some crags grade softly, others sandbag mercilessly, and a chart cannot tell you which. Granite slabs in the Verdon, limestone tufas in Kalymnos and gritstone edges in the Peak District each have their own feel at a given number. Ask local climbers, read guidebook introductions, and try a couple of routes well below your maximum before committing to a hard line. Over time you learn to read not just the chart but the place.
