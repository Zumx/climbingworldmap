Power and power-endurance are the engines of hard, steep, modern climbing — the qualities that let a climber move explosively between holds and keep doing so move after move. They sit between pure strength and pure endurance, and they are what most often limits performance on the steep, powerful routes and boulders that define hard climbing today. Understanding what power and power-endurance are, how they differ, and how to train them helps a climber target exactly the quality their goals demand. ## What Power Is

Power is the ability to apply force explosively — to generate maximum force quickly. In climbing, it is what drives dynamic moves, latches distant holds, and powers through hard individual sequences. Power is distinct from pure strength: a climber can be very strong but slow, lacking the explosive ability to move fast. Power combines strength with speed, and it is central to bouldering and the hardest moves on any route, where a single explosive effort makes or breaks the move.

## What Power-Endurance Is

Power-endurance is the ability to apply near-maximal effort repeatedly, move after move, before the muscles fail. Where power is a single explosive effort, power-endurance is the capacity to keep producing hard efforts in succession — to climb a string of difficult moves without the forearms shutting down. It is the quality that most often limits a redpoint on a hard, sustained route, where the moves are not just hard but relentless. Power-endurance bridges power and aerobic endurance.

## How They Differ from Strength and Endurance

These qualities sit on a spectrum. Maximum strength is the most force you can apply, regardless of speed; power adds the dimension of speed, applying force explosively. Aerobic endurance is the ability to sustain moderate effort for a long time; power-endurance is the ability to sustain hard, near-maximal efforts for a moderate time. A complete climber develops all of these, but knowing which one limits you on your goal routes lets you train the right quality rather than guessing.

## Training Power

Power is trained through explosive, high-intensity, low-volume work, always on a well-warmed body. Limit bouldering — attempting the hardest single moves and problems — develops power through climbing. For the advanced and well-conditioned, campus board training builds explosive contact strength, though it carries real injury risk. The key principles are high intensity, full recovery between efforts, and low total volume, training the body to recruit force fast without accumulating fatigue.

## Training Power-Endurance

Power-endurance is trained by performing hard efforts repeatedly with incomplete recovery, so the muscles learn to keep working under fatigue. Linking hard boulder problems, climbing laps on routes near your limit, and interval-style circuits on steep terrain all build it. The training is taxing and should be balanced against recovery, as it accumulates significant fatigue. Power-endurance work is most relevant in the phase approaching a hard, sustained redpoint goal.

## Putting It in Context

Power and power-endurance are best developed in the context of a balanced, periodised approach. Build maximum strength first, develop power on top of it, and add power-endurance as a route demands. Both qualities carry injury risk if rushed, so progress gradually on a well-conditioned body. For most climbers, hard, steep climbing itself develops these qualities effectively, with targeted training reserved for breaking through specific limits. Trained well, power and power-endurance unlock the steepest, hardest climbing.
