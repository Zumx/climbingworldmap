Watch an experienced climber walk up to a hard route, and you will see them stand back, tilt their head, trace lines in the air with a hand, and mutter to themselves. They are reading the route — decoding which holds to use, in what order, and with which hand or foot — long before they tie in. This mental work, often invisible to beginners, is what separates efficient climbers from those who flail and grab whatever is nearest. Reading sequences turns a chaotic wall into a solvable puzzle, and it is a skill you can train as deliberately as finger strength. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## What Beta Means

In climbing, beta is the information about how to climb a route — which holds to use, how to grip them, where to place feet, and the order of moves. The term reportedly originated decades ago at the Shawangunks in New York, and it has become universal climbing slang. Beta can be worked out yourself by reading the rock, gleaned by watching others, or shared verbally and in guidebooks. Knowing the beta transforms a baffling sequence into a series of executable moves, which is why climbers share and guard it so intently.

## Reading From the Ground

Before you leave the ground, study the route. Trace the obvious holds with your eyes, identify the likely line, and look for the features that will be hands and feet. Spot the rests where you can pause and the cruxes where the climbing concentrates. Imagine your body moving through the positions — which hand reaches the next hold, where the feet go to make that reach possible. This ground-up reading is rarely perfect, but it gives you a plan, and a plan keeps you moving when the pump sets in and panic threatens.

## Hand and Foot Sequencing

Much of reading is working out the order of hands and feet so you do not arrive at a hold with the wrong hand. If you need to reach high with your right hand, your left must be on the hold below and your feet positioned to support the move. Climbers learn to think a move or two ahead, setting up each position so the next is possible. Getting the sequence wrong — crossing into a dead end or matching when you should not — wastes energy and often forces a downclimb or a fall.

## Identifying Rests and Cruxes

Reading a route is also about pacing: finding the rests where you can recover and recognising the cruxes where you cannot afford to hesitate. A good rest — a jug, a stance, a stem — lets you shake out and prepare for the hard section above. The crux is where the route is hardest, and identifying it from the ground lets you arrive fresh and committed rather than surprised and pumped. Mapping the route into rests and cruxes turns an undifferentiated wall into a strategy.

## Onsight, Flash, and Redpoint

How you use beta defines the style of an ascent. An onsight means climbing a route first try with no prior information — the purest test of reading ability, since you must decode everything in real time. A flash is first try but with beta gathered beforehand. A redpoint is a clean ascent after practice, where you have rehearsed and memorised the sequence. Each style places different demands on your reading: the onsight rewards intuition and adaptability, the redpoint rewards precise memorisation of a worked-out plan.

## When Beta Goes Wrong

Beta is not gospel. A sequence that works for a tall climber may be impossible for a short one, and vice versa; reach, flexibility, and finger size all change what works. Blindly following someone else's beta can lead you into moves that suit their body, not yours. Good climbers treat beta as a starting point and adapt it, finding intermediate holds, different foot positions, or entirely alternative sequences. Learning when to trust beta and when to find your own is a mark of climbing maturity.

## Training the Skill

Reading improves with deliberate practice. Before climbing anything, force yourself to predict the full sequence, then climb it and compare. Watch others on a route and guess their next move. Study routes you cannot yet climb and rehearse them mentally. Bouldering is especially good training, because the short, intense problems demand precise sequencing and instant feedback. Over time, reading becomes intuitive, and you will find yourself unlocking sequences that once looked blank — climbing not just with your fingers but with your head.
