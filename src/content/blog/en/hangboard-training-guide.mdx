The hangboard, or fingerboard, is the most effective tool for building finger strength, the physical quality that most limits climbing performance. It is a board mounted on a wall with edges, pockets, and slopers of various sizes, from which the climber hangs to load the fingers in a controlled, measurable way. Used well, it builds strength faster and more specifically than climbing alone; used badly, it is a fast route to a finger injury. This guide explains the main protocols and the principles of safe, structured hangboard training. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## What the Hangboard Trains

The hangboard isolates and loads the finger flexors and the connective tissue of the hands. Because finger strength adapts slowly and limits hard climbing so directly, training it specifically can produce gains that are hard to achieve through climbing alone. The hangboard also lets the climber measure progress precisely — by edge depth, added or removed weight, and hang time — which makes it a powerful tool for structured improvement. It does not, however, train technique, so it complements rather than replaces climbing.

## Who Should and Shouldn't Hangboard

Hangboarding is for climbers whose tendons have had time to adapt — generally those with at least a year or two of consistent climbing. Beginners should avoid it entirely, because their finger strength is still developing naturally and their connective tissue is not ready for concentrated load; hangboarding too early is a common cause of injury. Even experienced climbers should approach it conservatively, with thorough warm-ups and a gradual progression.

## Max Hangs

The max-hang protocol builds maximum finger strength. The climber hangs from an edge (commonly around 18–20mm) for a short duration — typically seven to ten seconds — at a high intensity, resting two to three minutes between hangs, for several sets. Intensity is adjusted by adding weight with a harness or removing weight with a pulley. Max hangs train the fingers near their strength limit and are a cornerstone of strength-focused training blocks.

## Repeaters

Repeaters build strength-endurance, the ability to sustain grip over the duration of a route or boulder problem. A typical repeater set involves hanging for around seven seconds and resting three, repeated six or seven times to make one set, with longer rests between sets. The shorter rests keep the forearms working and build the capacity to recover under load. Repeaters complement max hangs by training a different quality of finger fitness.

## Grip Positions and Edges

A hangboard session should train the grips used in climbing, primarily the half crimp and the open-hand, on edges rather than aggressive full crimps, which are higher-risk. Most structured programmes favour the half-crimp and open-hand on a medium edge, avoiding the smallest holds and the most stressful positions. Training a balance of grip types builds robust, transferable strength while minimising the load on the vulnerable pulleys.

## Training Safely

Safety is paramount because the hangboard concentrates load on the most injury-prone part of the body. Always warm up thoroughly with easy climbing and progressively harder hangs before loading maximally. Progress slowly, adding small amounts of weight or reducing edge size over weeks, not sessions. Stop at any sharp pain, train no more than two or three times a week with full rest between, and never hangboard when tired or cold. Patience protects the tendons that take years to build.
