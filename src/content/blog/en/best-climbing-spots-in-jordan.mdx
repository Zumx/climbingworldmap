Jordan's climbing is concentrated in one extraordinary place: Wadi Rum, the great sandstone desert of the south. Here weathered domes and towers rise hundreds of metres from red sand, split by chimneys and crack systems that generations of Bedouin have known and Western climbers have mapped. The rock is sandstone, sometimes solid and sometimes friable, and the routes range from sporty bolted lines to long, committing adventure climbs with Bedouin scrambling traditions woven through them. The desert is silent, enormous and unforgettable. The areas below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=jo).

## Wadi Rum: The Valley of the Moon

Wadi Rum, the Valley of the Moon, is one of the world's great desert climbing arenas. Towering massifs such as Jebel Rum and Jebel um Ishrin rise from the sand, their walls streaked black and red by desert varnish. Made famous by Lawrence of Arabia and protected today as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, the desert offers a scale and a stillness that few climbing areas can match. Climbing here means engaging with both the rock and the rhythms of the Bedouin who live among these mountains.

## Bedouin Routes and Adventure Climbing

Long before bolts arrived, the Bedouin moved through Wadi Rum on hidden routes to grazing and water, and many of these natural lines have become classic adventure climbs. Routes weave through chimneys, slabs and domes, demanding route-finding, simul-climbing and a head for exposure rather than pure technical difficulty. The famous traverses and Bedouin ascents are part of the magic here: long days on rock that finish on a summit dome with the whole desert spread out below.

## Crack Climbing and the Sandstone Towers

Wadi Rum's sandstone holds superb crack and slab climbing. The Weida Slabs and similar sectors offer friction climbing on featured rock, while crack systems on the great walls give sustained, traditional pitches. Because the sandstone can be soft, climbers protect with care and treat fixed gear with suspicion, placing their own protection and respecting the fragile rock. The reward is climbing in one of the most beautiful settings on earth, where every belay looks out over an ocean of red sand.

## Bolted Sport Climbing

Alongside the adventure routes, bolted sport climbing has developed at venues such as Tor Al Ahmar and other crags, offering shorter, well-protected lines for climbers who want concentrated movement without the commitment of the big walls. These sectors are a good introduction to the rock and a way to build confidence on the sandstone before venturing onto the longer Bedouin routes. They also serve as warm-ups and rest-day climbing during a longer desert trip.

## Logistics, Guides and the Bedouin

Wadi Rum is entered through the village of Rum, where the protected area charges an entrance fee and local Bedouin run camps, transport and guiding. Hiring a Bedouin guide is both practical and customary, opening access to knowledge of the routes, water and the desert's hidden ways. Most climbers stay in desert camps, travel by 4x4 to the base of the walls, and carry plenty of water. There is no rescue infrastructure to rely on, so self-sufficiency and conservative judgement are essential in this remote terrain.

## Season and Conditions

The desert climate dictates the seasons. Spring, roughly March to May, and autumn, September to November, offer the best conditions, with warm days and cool nights. Summer is fiercely hot, with temperatures that make daytime climbing dangerous, while winter brings cold nights and occasional rain that can leave the sandstone fragile. Climbers avoid the rock for a day or two after rain, as wet sandstone is weak and holds can break. The clear desert skies and dry air make the shoulder seasons a joy.
