Sandstone is one of climbing's most varied and beloved rocks, ranging from the soft, rounded boulders of Fontainebleau to the soaring desert towers of the American Southwest and the bold gritstone edges of England. Formed from compressed sand, it offers rounded slopers, perfect cracks, and delicate friction climbing that rewards subtle technique over brute strength. But sandstone has a crucial vulnerability: it is fragile, especially when wet, and demands particular care to climb responsibly. This guide explores the character of sandstone and how to climb it. ## How Sandstone Forms

Sandstone is a sedimentary rock formed from grains of sand — mostly quartz — compressed and cemented together over millions of years. The strength of a sandstone depends on how well its grains are cemented, which is why sandstones vary so enormously, from soft and crumbly to hard and durable. Wind and water erode it into the rounded forms, cracks, pockets, and towers that characterise sandstone climbing. The grainy surface gives friction, but the rock's relative softness is also its weakness.

## The Character of Sandstone Climbing

Sandstone climbing is wonderfully varied. It offers rounded slopers that demand open-handed strength and body tension, splitter cracks for jamming, pockets and edges, and delicate friction climbing where the grainy texture provides grip on subtle features. The style is often technical and balance-dependent rather than purely powerful, rewarding precise footwork and clever movement. From the powerful bouldering of Fontainebleau to the crack climbing of the desert, sandstone spans a huge range of climbing.

## Slopers and Friction

Many sandstones, especially the softer rounded ones, are defined by sloping holds — rounded bulges with no positive edge that the climber must grip with an open hand, relying on friction and body position. Slopers demand a specific skill: keeping the body positioned so weight presses into the hold, using tension rather than crimp strength. The friction-dependent style of sandstone, especially places like Fontainebleau, is famously humbling and rewards patient, technical climbers over the merely strong.

## Cracks and Desert Towers

Harder sandstones offer superb crack climbing, none more famous than the desert towers and canyons of the American Southwest. Indian Creek in Utah is a crack-climbing paradise of perfect, parallel-sided splitters, while the desert towers offer adventurous, multi-pitch routes on striking formations. Desert sandstone can be soft and demands careful gear placement and judgement, and the towers are serious, committing objectives that reward the crack technique and boldness sandstone climbing develops.

## Fragility and the Wet-Rock Rule

The most important thing about sandstone is its fragility. Sandstone is far weaker when wet, because water weakens the bonds between grains, so holds break easily and the rock can be damaged. The cardinal rule is to never climb sandstone when it is wet, and to wait — often a full day or more — for it to dry after rain. Climbing wet sandstone breaks holds, scars the rock permanently, and is a serious breach of ethics. Respecting this rule is essential to preserving sandstone areas.

## The Great Sandstone Destinations

Sandstone shapes climbing destinations worldwide. Fontainebleau near Paris is the spiritual home of bouldering, its rounded sandstone the birthplace of the sport. The American Southwest — Indian Creek, the desert towers, Red Rock — offers world-class crack and adventure climbing. Saxon Switzerland in Germany, the Czech sandstone towers, and South Africa's Rocklands each give their own sandstone character and ethic. Each rewards the technique, friction, and care that sandstone demands.
