Big wall climbing is the most committing form of rock climbing: ascending faces so tall that the climb takes multiple days, with the team living on the wall between pitches. It demands not just climbing skill but a whole logistics of hauling supplies, sleeping suspended on portaledges, and managing systems for aid and free climbing over hundreds of metres. Yosemite's El Capitan is the spiritual home of the discipline, but big walls rise on every continent. This is climbing as expedition, where endurance and self-sufficiency matter as much as moves. ## What Makes a Wall "Big"

A big wall is generally a face long enough that it cannot be climbed in a day by most teams, often requiring one or more nights spent on the rock. El Capitan rises about 900 metres of sheer granite; routes like The Nose take experienced parties two to four days. The defining feature is not just height but commitment: once committed, retreat is difficult, and the team must carry everything needed to live and climb until they top out.

## Aid and Free Climbing

Big walls combine free climbing, where the climber moves on the rock itself, with aid climbing, where gear placed in the rock is used to make upward progress. Many classic big-wall routes were first climbed largely by aid, and some have since been freed — climbed without using gear for progress — as a separate, far harder achievement. Tommy Caldwell and Kevin Jorgeson's 2015 free ascent of the Dawn Wall on El Capitan was a landmark, with pitches up to 5.14d.

## Hauling and the Haul Bag

A team cannot carry everything on their backs while climbing, so supplies travel in a haul bag — a tough, heavy duffel dragged up the wall behind the climbers using a pulley system. The "pig," as climbers call it, holds water, food, sleeping gear, and the portaledge, and hauling it is hard physical labour that can rival the climbing itself. Efficient hauling systems and rope management are core big-wall skills.

## Sleeping on a Portaledge

When there is no ledge to sleep on, big-wall climbers use a portaledge: a fabric-and-aluminium cot that hangs from a single anchor point, suspending the team in space against the wall. Sleeping hundreds of metres up, clipped in at all times, is a defining big-wall experience — exposed, spectacular, and not for the faint-hearted. Storms can turn a portaledge bivouac into a serious ordeal, so weather judgement is critical.

## The Skills and Systems

Big-wall climbing layers many skills: leading both free and aid pitches, building bombproof anchors, hauling, jumaring (ascending fixed ropes), managing two or more ropes, and living efficiently in a vertical world. Partners must work as a seamless team, because inefficiency costs time and time costs safety. Most climbers build toward big walls gradually, learning aid technique and systems on shorter routes before attempting a multi-day objective.

## The Great Walls

Yosemite Valley, with El Capitan and Half Dome, is the birthplace and global centre of big-wall climbing. Beyond it lie the granite of Patagonia's towers, the walls of Baffin Island, the sandstone of the desert Southwest, and remote faces from Greenland to Pakistan's Trango Towers. Each demands its own blend of skill, endurance, and commitment, and each represents climbing at its most adventurous and self-reliant.
