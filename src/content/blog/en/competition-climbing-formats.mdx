Competition climbing has grown into a global sport with a clear structure built around three disciplines: lead, bouldering, and speed. Each tests different abilities, follows its own format and rules, and crowns its own champions on the World Cup circuit and at the Olympics. For spectators and aspiring competitors alike, understanding how each format works — how routes and problems are scored, how rounds progress, and what each discipline demands — makes following and competing in the sport far richer. ## The Three Disciplines

Modern competition climbing is built on three distinct disciplines. Lead tests endurance and the ability to climb a long, hard route as high as possible. Bouldering tests power and problem-solving on short, intense problems. Speed tests explosive power in a race up a standardised wall. Each demands different physical and mental qualities, and few climbers excel at all three, which is why they are usually contested as separate events with their own specialists and champions.

## Lead Climbing Competitions

In lead competitions, climbers attempt a single long route on a high wall, on-sight — without having seen anyone else attempt it. Each climber gets one try, and the goal is to reach as high as possible before falling; the score is the highest hold reached. Climbers are isolated beforehand so none sees the route climbed, making it a true on-sight test of route-reading, endurance, and nerve. Lead rewards the ability to keep climbing on hard, sustained terrain under pressure.

## Bouldering Competitions

Bouldering competitions present a series of short, hard problems that climbers attempt within a time limit, again typically without prior viewing. Modern scoring rewards reaching defined zone and top holds in as few attempts as possible. The problems test power, coordination, and the ability to decode and execute complex, often dynamic sequences quickly. Bouldering competitions are dramatic and spectator-friendly, with climbers throwing themselves at bold, modern problems in full view of the crowd.

## Speed Climbing Competitions

Speed competitions are races up the standardised 15-metre wall, usually head-to-head on two lanes in a knockout bracket. The fastest climber advances, and the format builds to a final showdown between the two quickest, often decided by a fraction of a second. With its standardised route and direct head-to-head racing, speed is the most immediately understandable format for new spectators and the most purely athletic.

## World Cups and the Competition Circuit

The discipline competitions come together in the IFSC World Cup circuit and World Championships, where the world's best compete across a season for rankings and titles. Events are held in each discipline at venues around the world, with climbers specialising or, less often, competing across formats. The circuit provides the structure of elite competition climbing and the pathway that leads, for the very best, to the Olympic Games.

## Scoring and Rounds

Competition climbing typically progresses through rounds — qualification, semi-final, and final — with the field narrowing at each stage. Scoring varies by discipline: height reached in lead, zones and tops in bouldering, and time in speed, with tie-break rules to separate close results. Understanding the scoring transforms watching a competition, turning a series of attempts into a clear, unfolding contest where every hold and every second counts.
