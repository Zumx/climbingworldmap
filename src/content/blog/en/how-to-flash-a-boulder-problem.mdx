A flash is one of bouldering's most satisfying achievements: you walk up to a problem you have never touched, pull on, and reach the top first try. Unlike an onsight, a flash allows prior information — you can watch others, study video, and ask for beta. That single attempt rewards the climber who prepares hardest, not just the strongest. Whether the line is a Fontainebleau classic like the lowball traverses of Bas Cuvier or a roof problem at Rocklands, the difference between flashing and falling is usually how well you read the rock and rehearse the sequence before your hands ever touch it.

## What a Flash Actually Is

In bouldering, a flash is a successful first attempt on a problem about which you already have information. This separates it from an onsight, where no prior knowledge is permitted. The flash sits between the two ideals of pure cold sight and worked redpoint: you arrive informed but untested. Because you get only one go that counts, the flash punishes wasted attempts and rewards thorough preparation. Many climbers can redpoint a grade or two above their flash level, which is exactly why the flash is such an honest measure of reading ability and execution under pressure.

## Reading the Line From the Ground

Before touching the rock, spend real time looking. Trace the holds from start to finish, identify the crux, and decide which hand goes where. Look for chalk on key holds, foot smears polished by previous ascents, and the obvious rest positions. On a Fontainebleau slab the read is about subtle feet and balance; on a Rocklands roof it is about body tension and the order of a long sequence. Visualise yourself moving through the whole problem, then mentally rehearse the crux several times. A climber who has the sequence memorised before pulling on has already done half the work.

## Gathering Beta

Because the flash permits information, gather it intelligently. Watch other climbers attempt the line and note which methods work and which fail. Ask locals or partners how the crux goes, whether a hold is better matched or used as an undercling, and where the hidden foot is. Video, whether filmed on the day or found online, lets you replay the exact hand sequence. Be selective: not every climber's beta suits your height or strength, and a tall climber's reach move may force a shorter climber into a different, harder solution that you must adapt before you ever try.

## Adapting Beta to Your Body

Beta is not gospel; it is a starting point. Reach, finger size, flexibility, and strengths all change the optimal sequence. A move that a tall climber spans static may demand a dynamic throw or an intermediate hold for someone shorter. A powerful climber might campus a section that a more technical climber resolves with a heel hook and precise feet. Watch the beta, then honestly translate it to your own body before committing. The best flashers are not those who copy the strongest local but those who quickly recognise which parts of the beta apply to them.

## Tactics and the Warm-Up

Tactics decide many flashes. Warm up thoroughly so your first serious attempt on the project is also your best, not a groggy first pull. Brush the holds, check your shoes are clean, and chalk appropriately for the conditions — friction matters enormously on subtle holds. Time your attempt for cool, dry conditions if the problem is hard for you, since a few degrees of temperature can transform a marginal crimp. Commit fully on the go that counts: hesitation on a flash often costs the send more than any single hard move.

## The Mental Side

The flash is as much mental as physical. You get one chance, and the pressure of that can either sharpen focus or cause hesitation. The antidote is preparation: when you have read the line carefully and rehearsed the crux in your mind, you can climb with conviction rather than doubt. Breathe, trust the sequence you built, and climb decisively. If you fall, the attempt becomes a worked send rather than a flash, which is no failure at all — but the climbers who flash consistently are those who turn careful reading into calm, committed movement.
