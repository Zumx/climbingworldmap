Limestone is the rock of modern sport climbing, the stone that holds the steepest, most spectacular routes and the dream destinations climbers travel the world to reach. Formed from the compressed remains of ancient marine life, it is sculpted by water into pockets, tufas, stalactites, and overhanging caves unlike any other rock. Limestone climbing is gymnastic, powerful, and three-dimensional, rewarding endurance and the ability to read complex features. From Spain to Greece to Thailand, the world's best sport crags are limestone. This guide explores its character and how to climb it. Explore limestone climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## How Limestone Forms

Limestone is a sedimentary rock formed mainly from calcium carbonate, the compressed remains of shells, coral, and marine organisms laid down over millions of years on ancient seabeds. Uplifted and exposed, it is then sculpted by water: rain and groundwater dissolve the rock, carving the pockets, runnels, tufas, and caves that give limestone its distinctive, featured character. This dissolving action is why limestone offers such varied, three-dimensional holds and the dramatic overhangs that define hard sport climbing.

## The Character of Limestone Climbing

Limestone climbing is defined by its features and its steepness. Water-sculpted pockets, edges, tufas, and stalactites give an endless variety of holds, and the rock frequently overhangs, creating the gymnastic, powerful climbing that limestone is famous for. Routes are typically bolted sport climbs, and the rock rewards endurance, powerful movement, and the ability to read complex three-dimensional sequences. Limestone can be sharp on fresh rock, polished on popular routes, and greasy in humidity.

## Pockets and Tufas

Two features define limestone climbing. Pockets — holes in the rock, from deep buckets to shallow two- or one-finger pockets — demand finger strength and the ability to use whatever depth and shape the rock offers. Tufas are flowing, draped formations of deposited calcite, like frozen streams down the wall, which climbers pinch, squeeze, and stem against in uniquely three-dimensional ways. Reading and using these features is a skill specific to limestone, and the tufa caves of Spain and Greece are among climbing's great prizes.

## Steep and Endurance Climbing

Because limestone so often overhangs, it is the home of steep, endurance sport climbing. Long, pumpy routes through caves and on overhanging walls demand the ability to keep moving, find rests, and manage the pump that fills the forearms on sustained terrain. Limestone rewards aerobic endurance and power-endurance as much as raw strength, and the great limestone routes are tests of staying power as much as difficulty. Pacing, resting, and reading the line are essential limestone skills.

## Conditions and Care

Limestone is sensitive to conditions. In humidity and heat it can feel greasy, with holds and friction suffering, while cool, dry weather brings out its best. Popular routes can become polished and slick over years of traffic, demanding precise footwork. Unlike sandstone, limestone is generally robust when wet, though seepage can make some areas unclimbable. Understanding how a limestone area responds to weather and season is key to catching it in good condition.

## The Great Limestone Destinations

Limestone defines world sport climbing. Spain — from the tufa caves of Margalef and Siurana to the multi-pitch walls of the Costa Blanca — is the heartland. Greece's Kalymnos, Thailand's Railay, the Frankenjura in Germany, the Verdon in France, and countless others offer limestone of every style. Each rewards the endurance, finger strength, and feature-reading that limestone demands, and together they form the global circuit of the world's best sport climbing.
