The story of sport climbing's hardest grades is one of relentless progress, as each "impossible" barrier fell to a new generation. The breakthrough into the 9th grade on the French scale, marked by Wolfgang Güllich's Action Directe in 1991, opened an era of ever-harder routes that continues today, culminating so far in Adam Ondra's Silence, the world's first 9c. These milestones chart not just rising numbers but the evolving training, tactics, and belief that pushed human climbing performance to extraordinary heights. ## Action Directe and the First 9a

In 1991, in the Frankenjura region of Germany, Wolfgang Güllich climbed Action Directe, widely regarded as the world's first route of the 9th grade — 9a on the French scale. The route demanded explosive power on tiny mono-finger pockets, and to train for it Güllich invented the campus board. Action Directe became a benchmark of hard sport climbing, repeated only by the world's strongest climbers, and it marked the moment the 9th grade entered the sport's vocabulary.

## Pushing Through the 9th Grade

After Action Directe, the hardest grades rose steadily. Climbers established routes at 9a+, then 9b, as training, technique, and tactics advanced. Chris Sharma's Jumbo Love in 2008 was hailed as a contender for the first 9b in America, and a generation of climbers worldwide pushed the upper end of the scale. Each new grade, once thought to be near the limit of possibility, was surpassed as climbers refined how they trained and projected the hardest routes.

## Adam Ondra and Silence

The current pinnacle of sport climbing came in 2017, when Adam Ondra climbed Silence in the Flatanger cave in Norway, proposing it as the world's first 9c. The route, on a steep, overhanging wall, demanded a combination of power, endurance, and bizarre, specific body positions, and Ondra trained for it with extraordinary dedication, even rehearsing the moves mentally and physically off the rock. Silence stands as the hardest sport route in the world and the current high point of the grade.

## How Standards Rose So Fast

The rapid rise of the hardest grades was driven by advances beyond raw strength. Structured, scientific training — hangboarding, campus training, periodisation — let climbers build precisely the strength the routes demanded. Refined projecting tactics, better understanding of nutrition and recovery, and the spread of indoor training all contributed. Above all, each breakthrough expanded what climbers believed possible, and belief, as much as physiology, propelled the relentless rise of the grades.

## The Role of Key Crags

Certain crags became the proving grounds of the hardest grades. The Frankenjura, where Action Directe lies, the Spanish limestone of areas like Oliana and Margalef, the cave of Flatanger, and others hosted the routes that defined each new level. These venues, with their steep, featured, world-class rock, drew the strongest climbers and became laboratories where the limits of the possible were repeatedly redrawn.

## What Comes Next

The push shows no sign of stopping. Each generation of climbers, training harder and smarter and believing in higher numbers, continues to chip away at what seems impossible. Whether the next barrier is a confirmed 9c+ or beyond, the history of the hardest grades suggests it will eventually fall. The story of 9a and beyond is ultimately a story about human potential and the refusal to accept any limit as final.
