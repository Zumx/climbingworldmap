Rappelling, or abseiling, is the technique of descending a rope under control, and it is how climbers get down from many routes, multi-pitch descents, and big walls. It looks simple and feels routine, which is precisely why it is one of the most accident-prone skills in climbing — statistically, descending and rappelling cause a disproportionate share of serious accidents, often to experienced climbers who let their guard down. Rappelling demands respect, a reliable system, and rigorous checks every single time. This guide covers the technique and the safety practices, though it cannot replace proper instruction. ## How Rappelling Works

In a rappel, the climber descends a rope that is anchored at the top, controlling their speed with a belay or rappel device that creates friction on the rope. The rope is usually doubled through the anchor so it can be pulled down afterward. The climber's brake hand controls the descent by holding the rope below the device; releasing or weakening that grip is what makes rappelling dangerous, so the brake hand must never leave the rope. Smooth, controlled movement is the goal, not speed.

## The Importance of Backups

Because rappelling depends on the brake hand and a single device, climbers add a backup so that a loss of control does not become a fall. The most common is a friction-hitch backup, such as a prusik or autoblock, tied below or above the device, which grips the rope and stops the descent if the brake hand lets go. A backup turns a moment of distraction or a rockfall strike into a stop rather than a catastrophe, and using one is strongly recommended on every rappel.

## Setting Up Correctly

A safe rappel starts with a correct setup, checked every time. The anchor must be solid; the rope must be threaded correctly through the device and the carabiner locked; both ends of the rope must reach the next anchor or the ground, with stopper knots tied in the ends to prevent rappelling off the end of the rope — a classic and fatal mistake. The climber checks the whole system before committing their weight, every single time, regardless of experience.

## Common Rappelling Accidents

Rappelling accidents follow recognisable patterns, almost all preventable. Rappelling off the end of a rope that is too short happens when no stopper knots are tied. Anchor failure occurs when the anchor is not properly checked. Threading the device wrong, unclipping at the wrong moment, or losing control of the brake hand are others. Inattention and complacency, especially at the end of a long day, are common threads. The defence is a disciplined system and unwavering checks, never rushed.

## Stopper Knots and Rope Ends

Tying a stopper knot in each end of the rope is one of the simplest and most important rappelling habits. It prevents the device from running off the end of the rope, the cause of many deaths. Knowing the rope reaches the next station before committing is equally vital; if it does not, the rappel must be re-planned. These simple, non-negotiable habits, applied without exception, eliminate some of rappelling's deadliest errors.

## Learning and Practising Safely

Rappelling must be learned properly, from a qualified instructor or experienced mentor, and the systems practised until they are automatic. It should always be treated with the seriousness its accident record demands, never as a casual end-of-day routine. Use a backup, tie stopper knots, check everything, and stay focused all the way down. The climbers who rappel safely for a lifetime are those who never let familiarity erode their discipline.
