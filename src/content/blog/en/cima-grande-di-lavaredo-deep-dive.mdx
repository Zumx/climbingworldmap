Seen from the Rifugio Locatelli at dusk, the three towers of the Tre Cime di Lavaredo look less like mountains than like a wall built by something that did not care about human scale. The middle tower, the Cima Grande, rises to 2999 metres, and its north face drops roughly 550 metres in a single sweep of grey and yellow dolomite, the lower half of it actually overhanging. This is one of the six great north faces of the Alps, the only one that is pure rock climbing from bottom to top, and since Emilio Comici unlocked it in 1933 it has been a pilgrimage site for every generation of climbers.

## Three Towers, One Skyline

The Tre Cime stand on the border between the Sexten Dolomites and the Cadore, above the meadows of the Drei Zinnen plateau. The Cima Grande (2999 m) is flanked by the Cima Ovest (2973 m) to the west and the slimmer Cima Piccola (2857 m) to the east, with the smaller Punta di Frida and Cima Piccolissima completing the chain. The south sides are broken and comparatively gentle — the Via Normale on the Grande goes at around grade II — while the north faces fall away in those famous vertical and overhanging sheets. The whole group sits inside a protected natural park and, since 2009, the Dolomites UNESCO World Heritage area.

## Anatomy of the North Face

The north face of the Cima Grande has a distinct two-part character that dictates every route on it. The bottom 200 to 250 metres are a compact, leaning shield of smooth yellow-grey rock, overhanging enough that water from afternoon storms falls clear of the wall. Above a prominent ledge system the angle eases to merely vertical, the rock becomes more broken and ledgy, and the climbing turns from gymnastic to classical alpine: looser holds, route-finding, and long runouts on easier ground. Most parties find the lower third the technical crux and the upper two thirds the place where alpine judgement, speed and rope management decide the day.

## The Comici-Dimai, 1933

In August 1933 Emilio Comici and the Cortina guides Giuseppe and Angelo Dimai spent three days forcing the first route up the face, a masterpiece of its era now graded around VII (or VI+ and A0 if you pull on the odd peg). The line finds the weakness in the overhanging lower shield through a series of leaning corners and shallow grooves, with pitch after pitch of sustained, exposed climbing on mostly good rock. Comici, the great theorist of the direttissima — the ideal line a falling drop of water would take — later soloed his own route in a matter of hours. Today it is heavily travelled, equipped with pegs of every vintage, and still serious: 18 or so pitches, plus the long scrambling exit to the summit.

## The Hasse-Brandler Direttissima

In 1958 the Saxon and German team of Dietrich Hasse, Lothar Brandler, Jörn Lehne and Sigi Löw pushed an even more audacious line straight through the steepest part of the shield: the Direttissima, one of the defining big-wall aid climbs of its decade. Climbed free, it is a different beast again — sustained at the upper end of the scale, with the hardest climbing around 8a on small holds through wildly overhanging territory. The route has become a benchmark free objective for strong alpinists, and even with modern gear it demands fast, confident climbing to fit the 500-plus metres into a Dolomite weather window.

## The Neighbouring Walls

The Cima Grande does not stand alone. On the Cima Ovest, the 1935 Cassin-Ratti route weaves through gigantic roofs, and Alexander Huber added the futuristic free lines Bellavista (8c) and Pan Aroma (8c) through the great overhangs in the 2000s. The Cima Piccola carries the most elegant moderate in the group, the Spigolo Giallo or Yellow Edge, climbed by Comici in 1933 at around grade VI, an arete so sharp and exposed it feels like climbing the corner of a skyscraper. A party based below the towers for a week can move between these objectives as conditions and ambition allow.

## Huts, Approach and Descent

Logistics are unusually civilised for a great north face. A toll road (payment collected near Lago di Misurina) climbs to the Rifugio Auronzo at 2320 metres, from which a broad track leads past the Rifugio Lavaredo and over the Forcella Lavaredo to the foot of the north faces in under an hour. The Rifugio Locatelli (Dreizinnenhütte, 2405 m) on the far side gives the classic head-on view and a good base for early starts. Descent from the summit follows the Via Normale on the south side, a long scramble with some abseils that deposits you back near the Forcella. Cortina d'Ampezzo, Auronzo di Cadore and Dobbiaco are the valley bases.

## Season, Conditions and Strategy

The window is short. July to mid-September is the reliable season; before that the upper ledges hold snow and meltwater streaks the face, after it the days shorten brutally and storms turn to snow. Because the face looks north it never sees sun, so even in August parties climb in jackets, and an afternoon thunderstorm — a near-daily Dolomite ritual — turns the upper chimneys into waterfalls. Start at first light, climb the lower crux pitches while you are fresh, and keep moving on the easier upper wall. A 60-metre double-rope system is standard for the abseils and the wandering pitches, and a helmet is non-negotiable under walls this travelled.

## A Wall That Made History

Few faces compress so much climbing history into one piece of rock. Comici's 1933 ascent redefined what was possible on overhanging limestone; the 1958 direttissima brought big-wall aid tactics to the Alps; the free ascents of the Hasse-Brandler and Huber's routes on the neighbouring Ovest mapped each new generation's ambition onto the same skyline. Stand below the face on the Locatelli side in evening light, when the towers go amber above the WWI tunnels of the Paternkofel, and the whole arc of alpinism is written above you in cracks and roofs.
