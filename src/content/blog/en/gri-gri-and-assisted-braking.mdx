Assisted-braking belay devices, of which the Petzl GriGri is the most famous, have transformed sport climbing belaying. By adding a mechanism that helps lock the rope under a sudden load, they provide a margin of safety that a simple tube device cannot, and they make holding a hanging or working climber far easier. But they are widely misunderstood: they are not automatic, not hands-free, and not foolproof, and misuse has caused serious accidents. Understanding how they work — and their limitations — is essential for anyone who uses one. This guide explains assisted-braking devices in depth. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## How Assisted Braking Works

An assisted-braking device contains a mechanism — typically a cam — that pivots and pinches the rope when it is loaded sharply, as in a fall. When the climber's weight comes onto the rope suddenly, the cam engages and clamps the rope, assisting the belayer's brake hand in holding the load. This provides a backup that can stop the rope even if the brake hand is momentarily off, which is the key advantage over a tube device. The mechanism resets when the load is released and the rope is fed normally.

## The Benefits

Assisted-braking devices offer real advantages. The locking mechanism adds a margin of safety against the most catastrophic belaying error — a brake hand coming off the rope — though it does not replace good technique. They make holding a hanging climber easy, which is invaluable when working a route, projecting, or belaying repeated falls. For high-volume sport climbing, where the belayer holds many falls and hangs, this ease and added security make assisted-braking devices the popular choice.

## The Pitfalls and Misuse

The danger of assisted-braking devices is the false sense of security they can create. They are not hands-free: the brake hand must remain on the rope at all times, because the cam can fail to engage if the rope is held wrongly or if the device is overridden. A common fatal error is holding the cam open while feeding slack, which disables the assist; another is gripping the device in a way that prevents it locking. These devices demand specific correct technique, and complacency around them has caused accidents.

## The Brake Hand Still Matters

The single most important thing to understand about assisted-braking devices is that the brake hand is still essential. The assist is a backup, not a replacement for correct belaying. The belayer must keep the brake hand on the rope below the device, feed and take in rope correctly, and never override the locking mechanism. Treating the device as automatic, taking the brake hand off, or belaying inattentively because "the device will catch it" are exactly the behaviours that lead to accidents.

## Feeding Slack to a Leader

Belaying a lead climber with an assisted-braking device requires specific technique, because feeding slack quickly can conflict with the locking mechanism. There is a correct method — taught by the manufacturer — for paying out rope smoothly while keeping the brake hand in control and ready to lock. Learning this technique properly is essential; the improvised, incorrect ways of feeding slack fast are precisely where many GriGri accidents originate. Practise the correct feeding method until it is second nature.

## Choosing and Using One Well

An assisted-braking device is an excellent tool for sport climbing and any situation with lots of hanging and falling, used alongside a tube device for multi-pitch and rappelling on two ropes. Choose a device rated for your rope diameter, read and follow the manufacturer's instructions, and learn the correct technique from a competent instructor. Used correctly, it adds genuine safety; used carelessly, it can lull a belayer into the very inattention it was meant to guard against.
