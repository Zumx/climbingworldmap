The Balkans hold a near-continuous belt of limestone running from the Julian Alps down to the Aegean, and for the road-tripping climber that geology is a gift. A single summer can string together Slovenia's overhanging tufas, Croatia's sea-washed gorges, the conglomerate towers of North Macedonia and the wild canyons of Albania, all linked by short, scenic drives across borders that have grown easier to cross. Grades are honest, prices are low, the food is generous and the crowds, outside a few famous names, are still thin. This is one of Europe's great climbing road trips, and it rewards curiosity as much as fitness.

## Paklenica, Croatia

Paklenica National Park, set where the Velebit mountains plunge towards the Adriatic, is the spiritual home of Croatian climbing. Its great gorge holds hundreds of routes on solid grey limestone, from single-pitch sport lines to the long multi-pitch walls of Anića Kuk, one of the most impressive faces in the country. The combination of accessible cragging and serious big-wall objectives, with the sea glittering just below, makes Paklenica the natural anchor for any Balkan trip and a place climbers return to for life.

## Osp and Mišja Peč, Slovenia

A short hop north, Slovenia packs world-class sport climbing into a tiny corner near the Italian border. Osp and the neighbouring crag of Mišja Peč are dripping with steep, pumpy tufas and have produced some of Europe's hardest routes. The village of Osp itself sits beneath a vast overhanging amphitheatre, and the relaxed local scene, mild winters and quality rock have made this one of the continent's premier cold-season sport destinations. Nearby Črni Kal adds more vertical, technical climbing for variety.

## The Croatian coast and islands

Beyond Paklenica, the Croatian coast is studded with crags. Omiš, where the Cetina river meets the sea, offers steep limestone above a dramatic town, while the island of Brač and the crags near Baška on Krk give sun-warmed climbing within sight of the Adriatic. This is climbing you can pair with swimming and sailing, and the long, mild shoulder seasons of spring and autumn make the coast a genuine year-round playground when the inland mountains are out of condition.

## North Macedonia and Serbia

Inland, the scene grows wilder and quieter. Near Prilep in North Macedonia, surreal fields of rounded granite and gneiss boulders rise from the plain, offering bouldering and short routes in a landscape unlike anywhere else in the region. In Serbia, crags around Niška Banja and the gorges of the interior reward those willing to explore, and the climbing communities of Belgrade and Niš are welcoming to visitors who make the effort to reach their home rock.

## Albania and the southern frontier

Albania is the Balkans' rising frontier. The Osumi and Gjipe canyons, and the developing crags near Berat and along the Albanian Riviera, offer dramatic limestone in a country only now opening to climbers. Access is rougher and information thinner than on the Croatian coast, but the rewards are solitude, raw scenery and the sense of climbing somewhere genuinely new. A guidebook, a hire car and a flexible plan are the keys to unlocking this part of the tour.

## Bulgaria and the eastern crags

The eastern Balkans close the loop. Around Vratsa in Bulgaria, towering limestone walls rise above the town, offering long sport and multi-pitch routes, while Karlukovo and the crags of the Iskar gorge add more variety. Bulgaria's climbing is cheap, uncrowded and increasingly well bolted, and it makes a fitting eastern bookend to a tour that began in the Julian Alps, proving how much vertical reward this overlooked corner of Europe holds.
