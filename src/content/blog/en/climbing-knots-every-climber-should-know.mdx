Roped climbing rests on a small set of knots, each with a specific job, and knowing them well is a core safety skill. The knot that ties you to the rope, the hitches that secure you to anchors, the friction knots that grip a rope for ascending or backing up a rappel — these are the threads on which a climber's safety hangs. Learning a handful of essential knots, and being able to tie and check them reliably, is fundamental. This guide introduces the key knots and what each is for, though they must be learned hands-on, not from text alone. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Why Knots Matter

In climbing, a knot is often the single connection between the climber and safety: the tie-in knot links you to the rope, an anchor hitch secures you to the wall, a backup knot can save a rappel. A knot tied wrong, left incomplete, or dressed badly can fail or slip, with serious consequences. This is why climbers learn a small set of knots thoroughly, tie them the same way every time, and always check them. Reliability matters more than knowing many knots.

## The Figure-Eight Follow-Through

The figure-eight follow-through is the standard knot for tying the rope to the harness, the single most-tied knot in climbing. It is strong, secure, easy to check visually, and unlikely to come undone. The climber ties a figure-eight in the rope, threads the tail back through the harness tie-in points, and follows the original knot back through to double it. A correctly dressed and tightened figure-eight, with a long enough tail, is the trusted lifeline of roped climbing.

## The Clove Hitch

The clove hitch is the climber's quick, adjustable hitch for securing the rope to a carabiner, most often at an anchor. It can be tied with one hand, adjusted without untying, and holds securely under load while remaining easy to release. On multi-pitch routes, the clove hitch lets a climber clip into the anchor and fine-tune the length quickly. It is one of the most useful and frequently used knots in the roped climber's repertoire.

## The Prusik and Friction Hitches

Friction hitches like the prusik grip the rope when loaded and slide when not, making them essential for ascending a rope, backing up a rappel, and self-rescue. A prusik loop wrapped around the climbing rope locks under weight but can be slid up when unweighted. These hitches are the foundation of crevasse rescue, escaping a belay, and the rappel backup that has saved countless lives. Knowing how to tie and use a friction hitch is a key self-rescue skill.

## Other Useful Knots

Beyond these, a few more knots round out the climber's repertoire. The double fisherman's joins two ropes for a long rappel and ties prusik loops. The girth hitch attaches a sling to a harness or feature. The Munter hitch can belay or rappel directly off a carabiner if you drop your device. The stopper knot in the end of a rope prevents rappelling or lowering off the end. Each has its place, and a well-rounded climber knows them all.

## Learning Knots Properly

Knots must be learned by tying them, not by reading about them. Practise each essential knot until you can tie it correctly every time, including in awkward positions and under pressure, and learn to recognise when one is dressed and tightened properly. A course or experienced mentor can teach the knots and, crucially, how to check them. Tie your knots the same way every time, check them before every climb, and never trust a knot you are unsure of.
