In the southern highlands of Madagascar, beside the Andringitra mountains, the Tsaranoro massif lifts a row of granite shields up to 800 metres above a valley of rice paddies, zebu pastures and sacred forest. The walls are vast, smooth and improbably steep for slab-dominated rock, streaked black and gold like the hide of some enormous animal. Climbing arrived in the 1990s, and the valley has since become Africa's most celebrated big-wall destination: long, bold, friction-heavy routes under equatorial light, with ring-tailed lemurs in the trees below and one of the poorest, warmest rural communities on earth hosting the whole show.

## The massif and the valley

The Tsaranoro group stands at the edge of the Andringitra National Park, whose granite domes culminate in Pic Boby — at 2,658 metres Madagascar's highest accessible summit. The climbing concentrates on a family of walls: Tsaranoro Be and Tsaranoro Kely, the great twin shields; Karambony, the compact wall of hard test-pieces; and satellites like the Chameleon (Caméléon) tower across the valley. Beneath them, villages live by rice and zebu, and the forest at the foot of Tsaranoro Be is locally sacred — treat boundaries and customs with care. The scale only reveals itself when climbers on the lower slabs shrink to dust specks.

## How the climbing feels

Tsaranoro is friction country. The granite is generally compact, with crystals, scoops and faint runnels instead of crack systems, so almost all routes are bolted — but in a bold, alpine style, with spacious runouts that put the grades in honest perspective. Pitches of slab and vertical face stack up fifteen or twenty deep, and a typical big route means a full day of climbing on the feet, calves screaming, with route-reading as important as finger strength. Belays are bolted, descents are by abseil down the lines, and a 60-metre double-rope system plus a generous quickdraw rack is the standard kit.

## Tough Enough and the famous lines

The wall of Karambony carries the valley's most famous route: Tough Enough, roughly 380 metres of relentlessly thin face climbing that stood for years among the hardest big-wall free climbs on earth and drew elite climbers from across the world to attempt its redpoint. Around it, the great classics of Tsaranoro Be and Kely — long mid-to-hard grade outings established by visiting teams since the mid-1990s — offer more attainable but still serious adventures, and new lines continue to appear. For most visitors the goal is not the hardest route but the biggest day: picking a wall, starting at dawn and topping out into highland wind.

## Camp Catta and valley logistics

The valley's hub is Camp Catta, a long-established camp and lodge at the foot of the walls, named after the ring-tailed lemur (Lemur catta) that lounges around its grounds. Climbers stay in bungalows or tents, eat communally and walk to most approaches in under an hour; local guides and porters can be hired, and hiring locally is both useful and the right thing to do. Getting there is an expedition in itself: from Antananarivo, a long drive south via Fianarantsoa to the town of Ambalavao, then rough roads into the valley. Plan two travel days each way and carry spares for everything.

## Season and equatorial weather

The climbing season is the dry austral winter, roughly April to November. June to August bring the most stable weather, with warm days, cold nights and walls that go from chilly at dawn to baking by mid-morning on sunny aspects — many parties climb east faces early and rest through the midday glare. The rainy season, December to March, turns roads to mud and slabs to waterfalls and is best avoided entirely. At this latitude the sun is fierce year-round: sunscreen, litres of water and an early alarm are as essential as the rope.

## Culture, conservation and fair travel

Tsaranoro's villages welcome climbers, and the sport has become a meaningful source of local income, but visitors carry responsibility. Respect the sacred forest and burial sites, ask before photographing people, and spend money locally — guides, porters, meals, handicrafts from Ambalavao's famous paper workshops. The nearby Anja Community Reserve, where habituated ring-tailed lemurs bask on boulders, shows how conservation and village income can reinforce each other. Madagascar's nature is fragile and found nowhere else; the climber's footprint here should be as light as anywhere on earth.

## Beyond the walls

Rest days fill quickly. Hikers cross the Andringitra plateau to Pic Boby through moonscape granite and endemic flora; the Tsaranoro summit plateau itself rewards a non-climbing ascent by the walking route; and longer trips bolt on the canyons of Isalo National Park or the rainforest lemurs of Ranomafana. Many teams finish with days on the coast at Ifaty or Anakao. Combining the country's south makes the long journey worthwhile — Tsaranoro is the centrepiece, but Madagascar refuses to be a single-stop destination.
