Moored off the coast of Africa but flying a Spanish flag, the Canary Islands are Europe's warmest year-round climbing destination. The archipelago is pure volcano: basalt columns, golden ignimbrite gorges and phonolite domes stacked from sea level to the 3,718-metre summit of Teide. Gran Canaria and Tenerife carry the bulk of the routes — thousands of bolted lines between them — while the smaller islands hide quiet crags and rough bouldering above the Atlantic. When the mainland shivers from November to March, the Canaries serve T-shirt sport climbing, mountain landscapes from another planet and rest days that end on black-sand beaches.

## Gran Canaria: ravines and pine forests

Gran Canaria is often called a miniature continent, and its climbing matches. In the south, the palm-lined ravines of Ayagaures and the riverbed crags of Sorrueda offer steep, pocketed volcanic rock with hundreds of well-bolted routes, climbable on winter mornings and shaded summer evenings. Up high, the pine forests of Tamadaba hold slabby and vertical walls of rough rock at altitude, cooler in the warm months. At the island's heart, the great monolith of Roque Nublo rises to 1,813 metres — its tower carries historic adventure routes, though climbers must respect the protected-area rules that govern it.

## Tenerife: Arico and the high crags

Tenerife's signature crag is Arico, a winding gorge of golden ignimbrite in the south of the island, where pockets and edges line both walls of a barranco minutes from the motorway. Grades cluster in the friendly middle of the spectrum, making it one of Europe's best winter venues for mileage. Higher up, the crags around the Teide National Park rim — such as the walls near El Río at around 2,000 metres — give cool conditions and surreal views of the great volcano, while areas like Guía de Isora and Las Vegas add basalt and phonolite variety. Few places let you climb above the cloud sea before lunch and swim in the ocean after.

## Bouldering above the Atlantic

The Canaries' rough volcanic blocks make for skin-hungry, satisfying bouldering. Coastal fields of basalt boulders on Tenerife and Gran Canaria offer problems with the surf as soundtrack, and the islands' climbing communities keep developing new sectors inland, from forest blocks to crater-edge stones. The rock rewards open-hand strength and careful footwork — friction is enormous, but edges can be sharp, so tape and spare skin are wise. Winter is prime, when temperatures hover around 20 °C at the coast; in summer, boulderers follow the locals to altitude or to shaded northern slopes.

## The quieter islands

Beyond the two giants, the archipelago thins out deliciously. La Palma, the steep green island, has bolted crags in its barrancos and an interior of staggering relief; La Gomera offers phonolite plugs and roques rising from laurel forest; and El Hierro, the wild westernmost island, hides sea-cliff sectors above crystal Atlantic water. Lanzarote and Fuerteventura are flatter, but their volcanic outcrops and coastal blocs entertain climbers attached to surf trips. Route information on the small islands is thinner and some fixed gear is old — treat them as adventure destinations and check local topos.

## Climate, microclimates and the trade winds

The islands sit in eternal spring, but the trade winds carve them into microclimates. North coasts catch cloud and humidity; south coasts bake; altitude changes everything, with the high crags of Tenerife and Gran Canaria sometimes cold and windy while the beaches roast. This means there is always somewhere in condition: chase the south in winter, the heights and the north in summer, and learn to read the cloud sea. Rain is rare in the south but possible from November to February, and the calima — a Saharan dust wind — occasionally turns the sky orange and the air thick for a few days.

## Access, ethics and volcanic rock sense

Much Canarian climbing sits inside protected natural spaces, and several emblematic formations, including parts of the Teide National Park and Roque Nublo's surroundings, carry restrictions — check current rules before climbing and never bolt or clean without local consent. Volcanic rock varies from bullet-hard to crumbly within a single wall, so test holds, wear a helmet at adventurous crags and respect closures for nesting birds. The local scenes on both big islands are active and welcoming, with gyms in Las Palmas and Santa Cruz where visiting climbers can find partners and current information.

## Logistics: flights, hire cars and rest days

The Canaries are among the easiest exotic-feeling trips in Europe: cheap direct flights serve both major islands, ferries link the archipelago, and a hire car puts every crag within an hour or two. Stay in the south of either big island for winter sport climbing, or rent rural houses inland for altitude and quiet. Rest days write themselves — Teide's summit cable car, the dunes of Maspalomas, laurel-forest hikes on the north sides and seafood in fishing villages. Pack for four seasons if you plan to climb high; the summit zone can freeze while the coast holds 22 °C.
