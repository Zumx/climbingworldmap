The boulderer who falls from the top of a problem lands on whatever was put beneath them, and that decision was made minutes earlier on the ground. A crash pad setup is not just a foam mat thrown down at random; it is a deliberate arrangement of pads, spotters, and attention that turns a hard, uneven landing into a survivable one. Get it right and a long fall is absorbed and shrugged off; get it wrong and a routine slip breaks an ankle. This guide covers choosing pads, layering them, and managing the landing on everything from a low traverse to a frightening highball.

## What a Crash Pad Actually Does

A crash pad works by spreading the force of a fall over time and area. Its foam is layered deliberately: a firm, dense closed-cell top layer stops you punching straight through, while a softer open-cell base layer compresses to absorb energy. A single thick pad of good construction can soften a fall of two or three metres onto flat ground, but its job is only to handle the impact your body cannot — it does not make you invincible, and landing well still matters. Understanding this layering explains why a cheap single-foam pad feels either too hard or bottoms out instantly.

## Choosing Your Pads

Pads come in three broad styles. A taco pad folds in a single hinge-free piece, leaving no gap but bulking up in the pack; a hinged pad folds flat and carries easily but leaves a seam that a heel can find. A baffled or hybrid design tries to combine the two. For a first pad, most boulderers buy a large hinged or hybrid model around the standard full size, then add smaller pads later. Consider thickness, the durability of the cover fabric, the carry system, and weight, because a pad you have to hike with for an hour to reach a boulder field needs to be carriable as well as protective.

## Building a Multi-Pad Landing

One pad rarely covers a real landing. The aim is to build a continuous, gap-free surface beneath the fall zone, with no seams directly under where you will hit. Place your biggest pad in the most likely landing spot, then fill the surrounding ground, overlapping edges so a falling heel cannot punch into a gap. Crucially, never leave a seam between two abutting pads exposed under the crux fall — lay a third pad or a smaller satellite pad across the join. On uneven ground, fill hollows and level the surface beneath the pads first, because a pad bridging a hole simply collapses into it under load.

## Spotting Done Properly

A spotter does not catch the climber; that would injure both. The spotter's job is to protect the head and steer the falling body onto the pads, keeping the climber upright so they land on their feet. Stand close with hands raised, thumbs tucked, watching the hips rather than the hands, because the hips show where the mass is going. As the climber falls, guide their shoulders and trunk to keep them over the pads and prevent a backward topple onto rock or a hard edge. Good spotting is active and attentive, and on a committing problem it is as important as the foam itself.

## Highballs and the Limits of Foam

A highball — a tall boulder problem where a fall from the top is genuinely dangerous — pushes a pad setup to its limit. Famous highballs on the granite of Bishop's Buttermilks in California, or the tall sandstone of Fontainebleau, are climbed above stacks of pads and multiple spotters, but the honest truth is that foam can only do so much. From a real height, even a perfect landing onto deep pads carries serious risk, and many highballs are climbed in a frame of mind closer to soloing than to ordinary bouldering. Knowing where padding stops protecting and starts merely reassuring is a vital judgement.

## Reading the Landing Zone

Before anyone climbs, study the ground. Identify the fall line for each section of the problem, note where the climber will be moving sideways and might land off to one side, and find the hazards: a boulder behind the landing, a tree root, a drop-off, a slope that will roll an ankle. Arrange pads to cover the real fall zones, not just the spot directly below the start, and reposition them as the climber moves up and the likely landing shifts. On traverses and dynamic problems, pads and spotters must move with the climber rather than staying put.

## Care, Transport and Etiquette

Pads live a hard life and reward basic care. Brush off grit before folding, dry them out after wet sessions so the foam does not degrade, and avoid leaving them baking in a hot car, which breaks down foam over time. When sharing a boulder field, pool pads generously — a problem protected by one group's pads is safer for everyone — and pick up litter and brush tick marks off holds before you leave. A well-built setup, combined with a watchful spotter and honest judgement about height, is what lets bouldering stay both bold and survivable.
