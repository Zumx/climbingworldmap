Rock climbing is not one sport but several, and the three main disciplines — bouldering, sport climbing, and traditional (trad) climbing — differ enormously in their gear, their risks, and the kind of experience they offer. Understanding the distinctions helps any climber choose where to start and how to progress. Each has its own culture, its own great destinations, and its own rewards, and many climbers practise all three. Explore the venues for each on the [map](/map).

## Bouldering

Bouldering is climbing short, hard problems on boulders or low walls, without ropes, protected by thick foam crash pads and spotters below. Falls are frequent and part of the game, cushioned by the pads. Bouldering distils climbing to its most intense and gymnastic — powerful, technical movement over a few metres — and requires minimal gear: shoes, chalk, and a pad. It is accessible, social, and focused on pure difficulty of movement, making it a popular entry point and a discipline in its own right.

## Sport Climbing

Sport climbing involves ascending longer routes protected by bolts pre-placed in the rock. The climber clips the rope into quickdraws attached to the bolts, and a fall is held by the rope and the belayer. Because the protection is fixed and reliable, sport climbing allows climbers to push physical difficulty with relative safety, focusing on the movement and endurance of the climb. It requires a rope, quickdraws, a harness, and a belay device, and it is the most popular form of roped climbing worldwide.

## Traditional (Trad) Climbing

Trad climbing involves placing your own removable protection — cams, nuts, and other gear — into cracks and features as you climb, rather than relying on fixed bolts. The leader places gear, clips the rope, and the second removes it on the way up. Trad demands judgement, experience, and a cool head, as the climber is responsible for their own protection. It carries more risk and commitment than sport climbing, and it opens up the adventurous, traditional lines that bolts cannot or should not protect.

## Risk and Commitment

The disciplines differ in risk. Bouldering keeps falls short and cushioned, though landings and highballs carry risk. Sport climbing, with fixed protection, is the most controlled form of roped climbing, though falls and belaying still demand care. Trad carries the most commitment, as the climber's safety depends on the gear they place and their judgement. Understanding these differences is essential to climbing safely and choosing the discipline that suits your appetite for risk and adventure.

## Gear and Accessibility

The disciplines also differ in gear and accessibility. Bouldering needs the least — shoes, chalk, and a pad — making it the easiest to start. Sport climbing requires a rope, draws, harness, and belay skills. Trad requires a full rack of protection and the experience to place it well, making it the most involved to learn. Many climbers begin with bouldering or sport and progress to trad, and the gear and skills build naturally across the disciplines.
