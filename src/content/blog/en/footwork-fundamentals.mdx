Ask any climbing coach what separates good climbers from strong ones and the answer is almost always footwork. The legs are far stronger than the arms and tire far more slowly, so a climber who stands well on their feet conserves the precious grip strength that fails first. Yet footwork is invisible, unglamorous, and easy to neglect in favour of pulling harder. Learning to place the feet precisely, quietly, and with trust is the single biggest improvement most climbers can make. This guide covers the fundamentals that turn flailing into flow. ## Why Feet Matter More Than Hands

The hands grip and the arms pull, but they fatigue quickly and can only generate so much force. The legs, by contrast, are powerful and enduring. A climber who drives weight through the feet takes load off the fingers, climbs longer before pumping out, and moves with control. The fundamental shift from beginner to competent climber is learning to push up with the legs rather than haul up with the arms — to treat the feet, not the hands, as the engine.

## Edging

Edging is using the edge of the climbing shoe — usually the precise inside edge near the big toe — to stand on small holds. The foot is placed deliberately so that the stiff edge of the shoe rests on the hold, and the climber stands up over it. Edging demands precision: the toe must be placed exactly on the hold and kept still, because the slightest shift can roll the foot off. Good edging lets a climber stand confidently on holds the size of a coin.

## Smearing

Where there is no defined edge, the climber smears: pressing the sole of the shoe flat against the rock to create friction. Smearing relies on the rubber's grip and the angle of the foot and ankle, and it feels insecure until trusted. It is essential on slabs and on featureless rock, and even on steeper terrain a smear can provide a surprising amount of support. The key is to weight the smear smoothly and keep the heel low to maximise rubber contact.

## Precision and Placement

The hallmark of good footwork is precision: looking at a foothold, placing the foot exactly where intended, and keeping it there. Beginners tend to paw at footholds, adjust repeatedly, and look away too soon. The skilled climber watches the foot onto the hold, places it once, and trusts it. This precision saves energy, prevents slips, and allows the smooth weight transfer that good climbing depends on. Watching the foot all the way to the hold is a simple, powerful habit.

## Quiet Feet

A telling sign of skill is quiet feet: placing the foot softly and accurately rather than scraping, slapping, or repositioning. Noisy, clumsy feet waste energy and break the smear, while quiet feet reflect control and precision. Coaches often have climbers practise climbing in silence, placing each foot so gently it makes no sound, to build awareness and accuracy. The discipline of quiet feet quickly translates into more secure, efficient movement.

## Using the Feet to Move the Body

Beyond standing, the feet position and move the whole body. Techniques like the back step (turning the hip into the wall by standing on the outside edge), the flag (using a trailing foot for balance against barn-dooring), and the drop knee use clever footwork to keep weight over the feet and reduce the load on the arms. These techniques all flow from the basic principle: the feet, well used, do the work that tired arms cannot.
