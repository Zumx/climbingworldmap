Deep water soloing, often shortened to DWS or psicobloc, is one of climbing's most exhilarating forms: ascending sea cliffs and overhanging walls without rope, harness, or protection, with deep water below as the only safety net. Fall, and you splash rather than slam. It strips climbing back to pure movement and freedom while carrying its own distinctive risks. Born on the sea cliffs of Britain and made famous in Mallorca, it has become a sun-soaked summer discipline practised wherever climbable rock rises straight from deep water. This guide explains how it works and how to do it safely. Explore coastal climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## How Deep Water Soloing Works

In deep water soloing the climber ascends a cliff or wall that overhangs deep water, with no rope or gear. The water provides the fall protection, so the climber can push hard knowing that a fall ends in a splash. Climbs are often committing and overhanging, traversing out over the sea before rising. Because there is no protection, the climber must judge for themselves the height, the water depth, and the consequences of a fall at every point on the route.

## The Hazards Are Real

The water is forgiving but not harmless. Falls from height can injure — hitting the water flat or awkwardly from ten or more metres hurts, and a high fall can knock the wind out of you or worse. Shallow water, submerged rocks, strong currents, tides, and cold all add danger. Getting back out of the water and away from the cliff base can be harder than it looks. Climbers must check water depth and exit before climbing, and never assume conditions are safe.

## Falling Technique

Falling well is a core DWS skill. The aim is to enter the water feet-first and vertical, body straight, arms in, to minimise impact. A flat back- or belly-flop from height is painful and potentially injurious. Climbers learn to spot their fall, push off cleanly from the rock, and control their body in the air. On high routes, a bad fall is a real risk, so many climbers practise entries from lower heights before committing to tall lines.

## The Great Venues

Mallorca is the spiritual home of modern deep water soloing, its limestone coves and tufa-draped caves offering world-class psicobloc; Chris Sharma's first ascent of Es Pontàs, a huge sea arch, became an iconic moment for the discipline. Britain's sea cliffs, where the style was pioneered, remain classic, and venues in Thailand, Vietnam's Ha Long Bay, and along the Mediterranean offer warm-water soloing. Each demands local knowledge of tides, depth, and access.

## Tides, Depth, and Timing

Coastal climbing is governed by the sea. Tides change water depth dramatically through the day, turning a safe landing into a shallow hazard or cutting off access to the base of a climb. Climbers must consult tide tables, know the depth at the time they plan to climb, and account for swell and currents. Timing a session for the right tide and calm conditions is as much a part of DWS as the climbing itself.

## Doing It Safely

Deep water soloing rewards caution. Never solo alone — a partner in the water or on a kayak can help if something goes wrong. Check depth and submerged hazards before committing, ideally by swimming the landing zone first. Climb within your limit on tall routes, build fall experience gradually, and respect the sea's power. With sound judgement and local knowledge, DWS is one of climbing's purest joys; without it, the consequences can be severe.
