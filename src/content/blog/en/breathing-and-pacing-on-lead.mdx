Film almost any climber moving above their last bolt and you will observe a quiet phenomenon: the breathing stops. The chest locks, the shoulders rise, the grip clamps to twice the necessary force, and movement that was fluid on toprope turns wooden. Breath-holding under stress is a deep human reflex, and on lead it conspires with hurried, panicky pacing to drain climbers far below their physical limit. The fix is unglamorous and astonishingly effective: deliberate breathing and deliberate rhythm, trained until they survive contact with fear. Of all the things that decide a hard onsight, the lungs are the most neglected.

## What Held Breath Does to Climbing

Breath-holding is not just an oxygen problem — it is a tension problem. A locked diaphragm braces the whole trunk, which stiffens movement and turns smooth weight shifts into lurches. Carbon dioxide accumulates, and rising CO2 is precisely the signal the brain reads as suffocation and danger, amplifying the very anxiety that stopped the breath. Grip force climbs in sympathy: frightened climbers squeeze holds far harder than needed, accelerating the pump. The cascade feeds itself — fear stops breath, stopped breath feeds fear — and the route feels harder with every metre. Restarting the breath interrupts the whole loop at its cheapest point.

## The Exhale Is the Lever

Breathing advice fails when it is vague, so make it mechanical: the exhale is the tool. Long, audible exhales trigger the body's parasympathetic brake, dropping heart rate and releasing trunk tension in a way that deliberate inhales do not. Many strong climbers exhale sharply on hard moves — the climber's grunt has real function, like a tennis player's — and use slow extended exhales at stances and before clips. A workable pattern on route: breathe steadily through easy ground, one deliberate exhale as you set up each hard move, and a long sigh-like reset whenever you reach a stance. If you can hear your own breathing, it is working.

## Pacing: Bursts and Pauses, Not a Grind

Efficient lead climbing is not one steady speed; it is a rhythm of bursts and pauses. Through cruxy, steep or strenuous ground, the right pace is brisk — committed, continuous movement that gets you through expensive territory before the pump compounds. At rests and good stances, the pace drops to zero on purpose: breathe, shake, read, then go again. The common failure is the inverse: dawdling through the hard moves, hesitating with bent arms while strength drains, then rushing past the rests out of anxiety. Watch an experienced climber and the pattern is unmistakable — slow where it is cheap, fast where it is expensive.

## Clips, Stances and Micro-Decisions

The clipping moment concentrates everything. Climbers rush to clip from too low, fumbling overhead with metres of slack out, when one more move would reach a better stance — or they hang about at a strenuous clipping position, bleeding strength while their breathing stalls. Decide clipping stances during the ground read, clip from straight arms where possible, and breathe through the clip itself: exhale, lift, clip, reset. The same discipline applies to every micro-decision on route — chalk now or later, rest here or push on. Each decision made calmly on schedule costs little; each one made gasping and late costs double.

## Nerves, Ritual and the First Bolt

Pacing begins before the route. A pre-climb ritual — the same shoe-tightening, the same knot check, the same three slow breaths before pulling on — gives the nervous system a familiar runway and starts the route composed rather than scattered. The first bolts deserve particular calm: many leads unravel in the opening metres, where anxiety is highest and the body not yet warm. Climb the easy start deliberately slowly, breathing audibly, and let the rhythm establish itself before the climbing demands it. By the time the route steepens, breath and pace should already be running on rails.

## When It All Falls Apart

Even trained rhythm breaks sometimes — a surprise runout, a wrong sequence, a foot skating off. The recovery skill is the reset: stop at the best available hold, plant the feet, and take three long exhales before deciding anything. Ten seconds of deliberate breathing reliably downgrades panic into mere difficulty, and the decisions made after the reset — downclimb, push on, take — are consistently better than the ones made mid-gasp. Practise the reset on safe ground so it exists as a habit. The climbers who look calm in trouble are not fearless; they simply have a rehearsed first move when fear arrives, and it is an exhale.

## Training Breath and Rhythm

Like any technique, this one is built below your limit. Dedicate easy mileage sessions to audible breathing on every route, noticing the exact moves where the breath wants to stop — usually clips, cruxes and the moment above a bolt. Climb routes at deliberately varied pace: one lap slow and continuous, one in burst-and-pause rhythm, feeling the difference in the forearms. Box breathing or slow exhale practice on rest days builds the basic skill the wall will stress-test. Within weeks the pattern starts asserting itself under pressure, which is the entire point: a breath that holds its rhythm when the bolt is at your feet.
