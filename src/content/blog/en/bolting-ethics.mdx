Few subjects in climbing stir as much debate as bolting. The bolts that make sport climbing possible are permanent alterations to the rock, placed by someone for everyone who follows, and the questions of who should place them, where, and whether at all touch on ethics, safety, tradition, and access. A well-equipped route is a gift to the community; a badly bolted one, or a bolt placed where none should be, can spark lasting conflict. This guide explores the ethics around bolting and why they matter so much to the sport's culture and future. Explore sport climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## What Bolting Is

Bolting means installing permanent anchors — expansion or glue-in bolts — into the rock to protect a climbing route. A sport route typically has a line of bolts up the wall, each clipped with a quickdraw, plus a lowering anchor at the top. Equipping a route is skilled, responsible work: the bolter chooses the line, places the bolts at sensible intervals to protect the climbing, and installs them correctly so they will hold a fall for decades. It is a service to every climber who repeats the route.

## Who Should Bolt

There is broad consensus that bolting should be done only by those with the experience, skill, and local knowledge to do it well and responsibly. A bolt placed wrong — in poor rock, too shallow, or with the wrong hardware — can fail catastrophically, and badly positioned bolts make a route dangerous or unpleasant. Equipping routes also carries responsibility to the community and the landowner. Most areas have established equippers or local clubs, and newcomers are expected to learn from them rather than bolt unilaterally.

## The Trad vs Sport Divide

The deepest bolting debates arise where sport and trad traditions meet. In areas with a strong trad ethic, placing bolts on a route that could be protected with removable gear is seen as an affront — a permanent scar where none was needed, robbing future climbers of the adventure. Retrobolting existing trad routes is especially contentious. The principle that has emerged in many places is that the first ascensionist's style should be respected, and that bolts should not replace what can be protected naturally.

## Where Bolting Is and Isn't Acceptable

Whether bolting is acceptable depends heavily on local ethics and rules. Some areas are fully bolted sport venues; others ban bolts entirely to preserve a trad or wilderness character; many have nuanced local agreements about which crags or styles may be bolted. National parks and protected areas often regulate or prohibit bolting. The unbreakable rule is to know and respect the local ethic before placing anything, because a bolt is permanent and a mistake affects everyone.

## Maintenance and Rebolting

Bolts do not last forever. Older bolts corrode, especially in coastal and humid environments, and hardware standards have improved over the decades. Rebolting — replacing old, unsafe bolts with modern ones — is essential maintenance, often carried out by dedicated volunteers and local organisations. Climbers benefit from this invisible work and should support it, whether through clubs, donations, or by reporting bolts that look worn. Trusting a bolt with your life means someone has maintained it.

## A Shared Responsibility

Bolting ethics ultimately come down to respect: for the rock, for the established traditions of an area, for the landowners and authorities who allow access, and for the climbers who come after. The bolts on a route are a shared, permanent resource, and the decisions around them affect the whole community. Engaging with local norms, supporting maintenance, and treating equipping as a serious responsibility rather than a personal whim keeps the sport sustainable and its crags open.
