The Dolomites are not just a mountain range; they are the spiritual home of alpine rock climbing. These pale towers in northeastern Italy — a UNESCO World Heritage site — rise in vertical walls of golden dolomite above green meadows, and almost every face carries history: Comici on the Cima Grande, the great south face of the Marmolada, the spires where modern via ferrata was born from the trenches of the First World War. Today the region offers everything from roadside sport crags and the Cinque Torri's friendly towers to thousand-metre faces on the Civetta, all linked by passes, lifts and the best mountain-hut network in the Alps.

## Tre Cime di Lavaredo: the great north faces

The three towers of the Tre Cime di Lavaredo are the Dolomites' defining silhouette. The north face of the Cima Grande, 2,999 metres at its summit, overhangs for its first half, and the route Emilio Comici climbed in 1933 remains one of alpine climbing's most storied lines. Classics of every era surround it, from the Yellow Edge on the Cima Piccola to modern test-pieces, while easier normal routes climb the south sides. A toll road to the Rifugio Auronzo makes approaches short, so the walls are busy in summer — start early and bring a helmet, because dolomite traffic means stonefall.

## Marmolada: the silver wall

The Marmolada, at 3,343 metres the highest summit of the Dolomites, hides its glacier on the north side and drops to the south in a 800-metre sweep of silvery limestone — the famous Parete d'Argento. Routes here, like the legendary Via dell'Ideale and the modern free climbs around it, are long, technical and committing, on compact rock where small wires and a cool head matter. Punta Penia's normal route, with its west-ridge via ferrata, gives mortals a magnificent summit day. The south face is a serious undertaking with afternoon storm risk, and it represents, for many, the finest big-wall climbing in the range.

## Sella, Gardena and the pass-top towers

The Sella massif squats at the hub of the Dolomite passes, and its towers are the range's most accessible multi-pitch playground. The First Sella Tower's Trenker crack, the classic traverses of the Second and Third Towers and countless mid-grade lines start minutes from the Passo Sella road. Nearby, Passo Gardena, Passo Falzarego and the Cinque Torri above Cortina d'Ampezzo offer the same magic in miniature: short approaches, solid rock, ledgy belays and summit views across half the range. These are the perfect venues to learn Dolomite climbing before committing to the bigger faces.

## Civetta and the wall of walls

Above Alleghe, the northwest face of Monte Civetta rears more than a kilometre high and several kilometres wide — climbers call it la parete delle pareti, the wall of walls. The Solleder-Lettenbauer route of 1925, the first grade VI in the Alps, still demands respect, and neighbouring lines like the Philipp-Flamm on the Punta Tissi rank among Europe's great rock adventures. This is the serious end of the Dolomites: long approaches, complicated descents, loose passages and weather that arrives fast. Parties who climb here earn the range's deepest sense of scale and solitude.

## Catinaccio and the Vajolet Towers

In the western Dolomites, the Catinaccio (Rosengarten) group cradles the Vajolet Towers, three elegant spires whose classic routes — the Delago Edge above all — pack huge exposure into moderate grades. The approach through the Vajolet valley, past the Rifugio Vajolet and Rifugio Re Alberto, is itself one of the range's finest walks, and at dusk the rock glows pink in the enrosadira, the alpenglow that gave the Rosengarten its name. The towers are an ideal first taste of Dolomite summits, with abseil descents that reward tidy rope work.

## Via ferrata: born in the Dolomites

The Dolomites invented the via ferrata. Cables and ladders first fixed for troops in the First World War now form a vast network of protected routes, from gentle classics like the Sentiero Bocchette in the nearby Brenta group to vertical challenges such as the Tomaselli above Falzarego or the Marmolada's west ridge. Climbing-grade fitness, a harness, a certified lanyard set and a helmet open hundreds of kilometres of astonishing terrain. Ferratas are also the best bad-weather-eve alternative and rest-day adventure for climbers, and they trace the war history written into these mountains.

## Sport crags, towns and logistics

Beneath the big walls, the valleys hold excellent sport climbing — crags around Cortina d'Ampezzo, Arco-style limestone near Landro and Misurina, and bouldering in Val Gardena and Alta Badia. Towns like Cortina, Canazei and Corvara make comfortable bases with gear shops, lifts and busses over the passes. The season runs roughly June to late September for the high faces, longer for valley crags; rifugi let you stage multi-day linkups without heavy packs. Afternoon thunderstorms are the great hazard of a Dolomite summer — alpine starts are not optional on the long routes.
