Watch a strong climber move through a roof at Rocklands or a steep cave at Kalymnos and you will see something that contradicts the beginner's instinct: the feet are not just standing on holds, they are gripping, pulling, and hanging. On overhanging rock, where gravity tries to peel the body off the wall, the heel hook and the toe hook turn the foot into a third and fourth hand. These techniques redistribute load away from tiring fingers, generate movement, and lock the body in positions that would otherwise be impossible. Mastering them transforms how steep climbing feels.

## Why Feet Become Hands

On vertical rock the feet mostly bear weight downward, but as the wall tilts past vertical that simple relationship breaks down. The body swings away from the holds, and pure finger strength can no longer hold the position. Hooking solves this by letting the foot pull rather than just push — a heel or toe pressed against a hold can resist the outward swing, counterbalance a reach, and take real load off the hands. On steep terrain a good hook is often the difference between a controlled move and a barn-door swing into space.

## The Heel Hook

A heel hook places the back of the heel onto a hold so the leg can pull. It is most powerful when the hold is roughly at or above waist height and you can engage the hamstring to draw the body toward the foot. Heel hooks shine on roofs, where they keep the hips up and stop the legs swinging down, and at the lip of an overhang, where a strong heel pull lets you rock over onto the slab above. Precision matters: place the heel deliberately on the best part of the hold, then weight it progressively rather than slapping it on and hoping.

## The Toe Hook

A toe hook uses the top of the toes and foot, pressing upward against a hold to pull the body inward toward the wall. Where a heel hook generally pulls down or sideways, a toe hook pulls the lower body in, which is invaluable in roofs and on steep aretes for stopping the legs swinging out. Toe hooks are often used in opposition with a hand or a heel, creating tension that locks the body in place. They demand flexible ankles and a willingness to point the toes hard, and a good rubber rand on the shoe makes an enormous difference.

## Hooking in Opposition

The real power of hooks emerges when they work against another point of contact. A toe hook pulling one way while a hand pulls the other creates a tension that pins the body to the rock, freeing the opposite limb to move. On a steep arete a heel hook on one side and a hand on the other can turn a desperate swing into a stable, almost restful position. Learning to set up these opposing forces — to feel where the tension wants to go and arrange your limbs to resist it — is the heart of steep climbing technique.

## Engaging the Right Muscles

Hooking is not passive; it is active muscular work. A heel hook engages the hamstring and calf, and consciously pulling with the leg rather than simply resting the heel on the hold makes the difference between a hook that holds and one that slips. Toe hooks engage the shin and the muscles along the top of the foot. Climbers who train these often-neglected muscles, and who practise consciously activating them, develop hooks that feel solid even when the position is precarious. The strength comes from the whole leg, not just the contact point.

## Practising and Refining

Hooks are best drilled where the consequences are low: a steep bouldering wall or a roof at a gym, or gentle overhanging problems outdoors. Practise placing the heel and toe precisely, weighting them gradually, and feeling how the body responds. Pay attention to shoe choice, since a downturned shoe with a good heel cup and a sticky toe rand hooks far better than a flat beginner shoe. Over time you will start to spot hook opportunities on sight — a flake for a toe, an edge for a heel — and steep rock will stop feeling like a fight against gravity.
