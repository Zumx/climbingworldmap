When a climbing day goes wrong — a partner is injured, a leader is stuck, or a team must retreat from high on a route — there is often no one to call and no quick rescue. Self-rescue skills, the techniques a team uses to get themselves out of trouble, can be the difference between a manageable problem and a serious emergency. These are advanced skills that build on the fundamentals of ropework, and they must be learned and practised hands-on, not from text. This guide introduces the core self-rescue techniques and why every committed climber should learn them. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Why Self-Rescue Matters

In many climbing situations — multi-pitch routes, alpine terrain, remote crags — outside help is far away or hours off, and a team must be able to deal with problems themselves. An injured leader, a second who cannot follow, a stuck rope, or the need to retreat all demand self-rescue skills. Without them, a minor incident can escalate into a serious one. Self-rescue is the safety net beyond the safety net, the ability to manage an emergency when no one else can help in time.

## The Core Skills

Self-rescue is built on a set of core techniques, all using the rope, friction hitches, and basic gear. Escaping the belay — freeing yourself from a loaded belay to go for help or assist — is fundamental. Ascending and descending the rope, using friction hitches or devices, lets a climber move on a fixed line. Building improvised hauling systems can raise an injured or stuck climber. Lowering and tandem rappelling can bring an injured partner down. Together these form the toolkit of self-rescue.

## Escaping the Belay

A foundational self-rescue skill is escaping the belay: transferring the weight of a hanging climber from your belay device onto the anchor, so you can free your hands to act — to go for help, ascend to the climber, or set up a rescue. It uses a friction hitch to hold the load while you transfer it, and it is the starting point for most rescue scenarios. Practised until smooth, it turns a stuck, weight-bearing belayer into someone able to respond.

## Ascending and Hauling

Two more core skills are ascending a rope and hauling. Ascending — climbing a fixed rope using friction hitches or ascenders — lets a climber reach an injured partner or escape a predicament. Hauling, using a system of pulleys and friction hitches to gain mechanical advantage, can raise a climber who cannot move themselves, such as an injured second past a hard section. These systems are built from basic gear and knowledge, and they are central to getting a team out of trouble.

## Why Hands-On Training Is Essential

Self-rescue cannot be learned from reading. The techniques are complex, the systems must be rigged correctly under pressure, and in a real emergency there is no margin for error. They must be learned hands-on, from a qualified instructor or experienced mentor, and practised repeatedly until they can be performed reliably, even when stressed and tired. A self-rescue skill half-remembered is worse than useless. Investing in proper training, and refreshing it regularly, is what makes these skills available when they are needed most.

## Building Toward Competence

Self-rescue is an advanced skill set that builds on solid fundamentals: a thorough grasp of knots, friction hitches, anchors, and ropework. Climbers heading into committing terrain — multi-pitch, alpine, or remote routes — should make learning self-rescue a priority, taking a course and practising the systems until they are second nature. The hope is never to need them, but the climber who has them is far better prepared to handle the emergencies that committing climbing can bring.
