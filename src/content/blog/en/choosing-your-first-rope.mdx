Your first rope is the single most important purchase you will make as a climber, and the wall of glossy cords at the shop can be bewildering. Numbers cover the packaging: 9.5 millimetres, 70 metres, dry-treated, a fall rating, an impact force in kilonewtons. Each is a real choice that shapes how the rope handles, how long it lasts, and how safe you are. The good news is that a sensible first rope is not hard to identify once you understand what the figures mean. This guide walks through diameter, length and treatment so your first cord matches the climbing you actually do.

## Why a Single Rope First

Almost every new lead climber should start with a single rope, a dynamic cord rated to be used on its own to catch a leader fall. Single ropes are simpler to belay, easier to handle, and used for the vast majority of sport climbing, indoor lead, and single-pitch trad. Half and twin ropes exist for specific multi-pitch and alpine situations, but they add complexity that a beginner does not need. Look for the circled 1 marking on the packaging, which certifies the rope as a single, tested to hold repeated leader falls.

## Understanding Diameter

Diameter, measured in millimetres, is the headline number. Modern single ropes run roughly from 8.5 up to 10.2 millimetres. Thinner ropes weigh less and feed faster but wear quicker and demand more attention from the belay device. Thicker ropes are more durable and forgiving but heavier and stiffer to handle. For a first rope, a diameter between 9.4 and 9.8 millimetres hits the sweet spot: tough enough to survive learning mistakes and the abrasion of indoor walls, yet light enough to climb comfortably and compatible with almost every belay device.

## Choosing a Length

Length is dictated by where you climb. Indoor walls and older crags were often built for 60-metre ropes, but many modern sport routes, especially in limestone areas like Spain or Greece, need 70 or even 80 metres so the leader can be lowered all the way to the ground. A 70-metre rope is the most versatile first choice in most regions. Whatever the length, always tie a stopper knot in the free end: lowering off the end of a too-short rope is a recurring and entirely avoidable accident.

## Dry Treatment and When You Need It

A dry treatment is a coating that helps the rope shed water and resist dirt. A fully dry-treated rope, where both the core and sheath are treated, is essential for ice climbing, mountaineering and alpine routes, where a soaked rope freezes, grows heavy and loses strength. For pure indoor and dry-crag cragging it is an unnecessary expense. If your budget allows and you expect occasional wet days or alpine trips, a sheath-only dry treatment is a reasonable middle ground that also slows dirt and abrasion.

## Reading the Safety Numbers

Two figures on the label describe how a rope performs in a fall. The fall rating counts how many severe test falls the rope held in the lab; a higher number means more durability, not that you get that many real falls. Impact force, given in kilonewtons, measures how hard the rope yanks on the system in a fall; a lower number means a softer, more comfortable catch. For a first rope, do not obsess over these. Any certified single rope is safe; prioritise a sensible diameter and length over chasing the lowest impact force.

## Handling, Care and Lifespan

A rope is a consumable that wears with use. Always uncoil a new rope properly to avoid kinks, and flake it before climbing rather than pulling from a tangled pile. Keep it off grit and sharp edges, use a rope bag or tarp, and never step on it, as embedded grit cuts the fibres from inside. Wash it occasionally in cool water with a rope-specific cleaner. Inspect it by running the whole length through your hands, feeling for flat spots, core damage or a fuzzy, sheath-worn surface, and retire it when in doubt.

## A Sensible First Setup

Put together, a strong first rope for most climbers is a single rope around 9.5 millimetres in diameter, 70 metres long, with at least a sheath dry treatment if you can afford it. Pair it with a closing knot habit, a rope bag, and a belay device matched to that diameter. This combination handles indoor lead, sport crags and beginner multi-pitch with margin to spare, and it forgives the inevitable scuffs of learning. Buy quality from a reputable maker rather than the cheapest cord, and it will serve you for several seasons.
