Nepal needs no introduction as a mountaineering country: eight of the world's fourteen 8,000-metre peaks stand wholly or partly on its soil, Everest among them, and the word Himalaya alone carries the weight of two centuries of climbing ambition. What surprises visitors is the rest of the pyramid — a system of permitted trekking peaks that puts 6,000-metre summits within reach of fit amateurs, a small but genuine rock climbing scene around the Kathmandu Valley, and city walls that train the Sherpa and Nepali climbers now rewriting the sport's history. The places below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=np).

## The 8,000ers: Everest and the Expedition World

The giants — Everest at 8,849 metres, Lhotse, Makalu, Cho Oyu, Dhaulagiri, Manaslu, Annapurna and Kangchenjunga on the border — define expedition climbing. Seasons revolve around the pre-monsoon window in April and May and a quieter autumn, base camps are small towns, and the economics of permits, Sherpa support and oxygen shape every climb. Whatever one thinks of the crowds on the normal routes, the achievement of Nepali climbers — from the first ascent era to the winter K2 generation — has made this the proudest mountaineering culture on Earth.

## Trekking Peaks: The Accessible Six-Thousanders

Nepal's brilliant middle tier is its catalogue of permitted "trekking peaks", administered by the Nepal Mountaineering Association. Island Peak (Imja Tse, 6,189 m) and Mera Peak (6,476 m) in the Everest region are the famous names, offering glacier climbs with fixed-line cruxes that reward sound acclimatization more than technical brilliance, while Lobuche East (6,119 m) adds a sharper, more technical summit day. These climbs slot into classic treks, permits are affordable, and for most mountaineers they are the sanest way to experience genuine Himalayan altitude.

## Hattiban: The Kathmandu Valley's Crag

On the forested southern rim of the Kathmandu Valley near Pharping, the crag at Hattiban is the home of Nepali rock climbing. Bolted slabs and walls rise among the pines below the Champa Devi ridge, with routes across the grades, a short approach from the road and views that occasionally open toward the white wall of the high Himalaya. It is a teaching crag, a weekend social scene and the venue where Kathmandu's climbers transition from plastic to rock — modest in scale, large in importance.

## Nagarjun and the Valley's Other Rock

Climbing in the valley extends beyond Hattiban. The walls inside Nagarjun Forest, part of the Shivapuri Nagarjun National Park on the city's northwest edge, hold bolted routes in a protected jungle setting, while boulders and small crags scatter the valley rim. The scene is young and access arrangements evolve, so the etiquette is to connect with local climbers first — easily done through the gyms — and to treat park rules and entry fees as part of the deal. Sport climbing in Nepal is a community project, and visitors who plug into it find partners immediately.

## Kathmandu's Walls and the New Generation

Indoor climbing anchors the city scene. The Astrek Climbing Wall in Thamel, with its outdoor tower, has introduced a generation of Nepalis and travellers to the sport, while the Kathmandu Sport Climbing Centre and newer bouldering venues train a competitive scene that increasingly sends athletes to international events. Treks aside, these walls are the best rainy-day option in the capital, and the noticeboards and instructors double as the valley's climbing information service.

## Pokhara and Beyond

Pokhara, Nepal's lakeside second city beneath the Annapurna massif, adds its own modest crags and a growing outdoor community, and makes the natural base for the Annapurna region's treks and peaks. Further afield, the limestone and granite potential scattered through Nepal's middle hills is enormous and barely touched — development is limited less by rock than by logistics. For most visitors, the pattern remains: rock days around Kathmandu or Pokhara, then upward to the trekking peaks and the big ranges.

## Seasons, Permits and Practicalities

Nepal runs on two seasons: pre-monsoon (March to May) and post-monsoon (October to November), with the autumn window offering the most stable air and clearest views. The monsoon drenches the middle hills from June to September, though crags can dry quickly between storms. Trekking peaks need NMA permits and a registered agency; the 8,000ers need full expedition permits. Kathmandu's gear shops can outfit almost anything, flights to Lukla remain the famous bottleneck, and patience — with weather, bureaucracy and yaks — is the essential piece of kit.
