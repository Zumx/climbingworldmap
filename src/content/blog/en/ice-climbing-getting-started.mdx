Ice climbing is the art of ascending frozen waterfalls, ice gullies, and glaciated faces using ice axes and crampons. It is a winter discipline of striking beauty and serious hazard, where the medium itself is constantly changing and a climber's safety depends on reading the ice as much as on technique. For those drawn to the cold and the vertical, it opens a whole new season and a whole new kind of terrain. This guide introduces the gear, the movement, and the judgement that ice climbing demands, but proper instruction is essential before venturing onto real ice. ## The Gear

Ice climbing requires specialised equipment: a pair of technical ice tools (curved axes with sharp picks), rigid mountaineering boots, and crampons with vertical front points for kicking into ice. Ice screws provide protection, threaded into solid ice as the climber leads. A helmet is essential against falling ice, and warm, layered clothing keeps the climber functional in the cold. The tools and crampons must be sharp and well-maintained — dull picks and points are both inefficient and dangerous.

## Movement and Technique

Good ice climbing is efficient and economical, not brute force. The climber kicks the front points of the crampons into the ice to stand on, and swings the tools to set the picks securely, aiming for a solid placement with the minimum number of swings. The ideal is to climb in a stable triangle, weight on the feet, arms straight, moving up methodically. Over-gripping the tools and relying on the arms is the classic beginner's mistake that leads to exhaustion. Trust the feet.

## Reading Ice Conditions

Ice is a living medium, and reading it is the heart of safe ice climbing. Good ice is plastic and takes a tool well; brittle, dinner-plating ice shatters; rotten or detached ice can collapse. Temperature, sun, and recent weather all change the ice from hour to hour. Climbers learn to assess colour, sound, and structure, to seek well-bonded ice, and to avoid free-hanging or thinly bonded formations. Misjudging conditions is behind many ice-climbing accidents.

## Grades and Types of Ice

Ice climbs are graded on the WI (Water Ice) scale, running from WI1 (low-angle) to WI7 and beyond (steep, thin, and technical). WI3 and WI4 are common objectives for developing climbers, while WI5 and above demand sustained steep climbing on often-fragile ice. Alpine ice (AI) and mixed routes combining rock and ice add further categories. The grade reflects steepness, sustained difficulty, and the quality and reliability of the ice itself.

## Placing Ice Screws

Lead ice climbing is protected by ice screws, hollow threaded tubes driven into solid ice and clipped with the rope. Placing a screw well — finding good ice, getting the screw started, and driving it home efficiently while balancing on tools and crampons — is a demanding skill. Screws must be placed in solid, well-bonded ice and at sensible intervals. The reliability of ice protection varies far more than bolts, which is part of what makes lead ice climbing serious.

## Starting Out Safely

The safest way to begin is on top-rope under instruction, at a venue with reliable ice and easy access, ideally with a guide or experienced mentor. Learn to move efficiently and read ice before ever leading. A course teaches gear use, movement, screw placement, and the all-important judgement of conditions. Ice climbing rewards patience and punishes the impatient; build skills and experience gradually before committing to bigger, more serious objectives.
