Canada is a climbing country defined by scale and wildness. From the granite walls of Squamish on the Pacific coast to the limestone of the Canadian Rockies and the remote big walls of Baffin Island, it offers some of the most varied and adventurous climbing on the continent. The country rewards those willing to travel and to embrace a strong tradition of bold, self-reliant climbing. The areas below are the essentials; find them on the [map](/map?country=ca).

## Squamish, British Columbia

Squamish, between Vancouver and Whistler, is Canada's most famous climbing destination. The Stawamus Chief — a 700-metre granite dome above the town — offers world-class crack and slab multi-pitch climbing, while the surrounding forest is full of superb granite boulders and sport crags. The combination of big-wall trad, bouldering, and sport, all in a temperate coastal setting, makes Squamish a complete destination. Summer is the season, when the granite dries out.

## The Bow Valley and the Canadian Rockies

Around Canmore and [Banff](https://parks.canada.ca/) in Alberta, the Bow Valley offers extensive limestone sport and trad climbing beneath the spectacular peaks of the Canadian Rockies. Crags like those above the valley provide bolted routes across the grades, while the mountains themselves hold serious alpine objectives. The Rockies are also a world centre for [ice climbing](https://www.theuiaa.org/) in winter, with frozen waterfalls drawing ice climbers from around the world.

## Ice Climbing in the Rockies

The Canadian Rockies are one of the planet's premier ice-climbing destinations. In winter, countless frozen waterfalls form across the valleys around Canmore, Field, and the Icefields Parkway, offering everything from accessible classics to long, serious mixed routes. The reliable cold and the sheer density of routes make the Rockies a global hub for waterfall ice, complementing the summer rock climbing and giving the region a true year-round climbing identity.

## Skaha and the Okanagan

In British Columbia's Okanagan valley, Skaha Bluffs near Penticton offer excellent gneiss sport climbing in a warm, dry climate. With a long season and a huge spread of bolted routes, Skaha is a popular destination, especially in spring and autumn when Squamish may be wet. The drier interior of British Columbia adds welcome variety to the coastal granite, extending the Canadian climbing season.

## Eastern Canada

Climbing is not confined to the west. Ontario and Quebec hold significant crags, including the limestone and granite of areas accessible from the major eastern cities, and Newfoundland and the Maritimes offer adventurous sea-cliff and remote climbing. While the western destinations draw the headlines, eastern Canada supports a strong climbing community on its own quality rock.

## The Remote Big Walls

For the committed alpinist, Canada offers some of the most remote and serious big-wall climbing on Earth. Baffin Island in the Arctic holds enormous granite walls — Mount Thor and the Stewart Valley among them — that have drawn expeditions seeking the ultimate in wilderness big-wall adventure. These objectives are the preserve of experienced teams, but they represent the extreme end of Canada's vast climbing potential.

## Season and Scale

Canadian climbing is governed by a continental climate and immense distances. The western rock is largely a summer pursuit; the Rockies offer rock in summer and ice in winter; and the remote objectives demand serious expedition planning. Travelling between regions means covering great distances, so most trips focus on one area. Respecting the wilderness, the weather, and the bold local ethic is central to climbing here.
