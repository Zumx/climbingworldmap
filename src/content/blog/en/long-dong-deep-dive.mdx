On the wild northeast coast of Taiwan, less than an hour by road from Taipei, the cliffs of Long Dong rise straight out of the Pacific. The name means Dragon Cave, and the place lives up to it: a long band of grey-gold sandstone, scalloped by the sea, with hundreds of routes that start barely above the waves. Surf crashes at the base, fishermen work the rock platforms, and climbers move across compact, sharp-edged stone with the ocean filling the view. This is Taiwan's premier climbing area. ## The Coast and the Crag

Long Dong sits within the Northeast Coast National Scenic Area, between the fishing harbours of Bitou and Aodi, a stretch of coastline shaped by tilted sandstone beds and pounding surf. The cliffs run for more than a kilometre in a series of bays and headlands, the rock layered and fractured into walls, aretes, and roofs. Approaches wind along clifftop paths and down to wave-washed platforms at the base. The setting is dramatic and exposed, with the sea always present, and on a clear day the view stretches across the blue water toward the horizon.

## The Sandstone

The rock is a hard, fine-grained quartz sandstone, compact and surprisingly solid for a sea cliff. It climbs through cracks, pockets, and crimpy faces, with edges that can be sharp on the fingertips. Both sport and traditional climbing thrive here: many walls are bolted, while crack lines take natural gear well. The rock near the sea can be greasy with salt and humidity, and the quality varies from immaculate to friable, so testing holds is wise. With grades spanning easy warm-ups to hard test-pieces, Long Dong rewards climbers across the spectrum.

## Sectors and Classic Routes

The cliff is divided into named sectors strung along the coast, including Backdoor, School Gate, Music Hall, Long Dong Village, and the Grand Auditorium, each with its own aspect and character. Backdoor is a popular, accessible starting point, while the Grand Auditorium holds some of the steeper, harder lines. Routes range from single-pitch sport climbs to multi-pitch trad adventures up the bigger walls. The variety of sectors means climbers can chase shade or sun, calm or breeze, and find suitable lines whatever the conditions or grade they seek.

## Sport, Trad, and Local Ethics

Long Dong has a mixed climbing culture. Sport routes are well established, but the area also has a strong traditional ethic, and many classic lines are protected by natural gear, so a rack of cams and nuts is valuable. The local climbing community, supported by Taiwanese clubs and the work of dedicated developers over decades, maintains the fixed gear, but salt air corrodes bolts and anchors, so climbers should inspect protection carefully and treat older hardware with caution. Respecting fishermen, who share the rock platforms, is part of the etiquette here.

## Tides, Weather, and Safety

The sea is a constant factor. Many routes start just above the waterline, and at high tide or in a swell the base platforms can be washed by surf, making access dangerous. Climbers check the tide and sea state before committing to a low sector, and rogue waves are a genuine hazard on exposed platforms. Typhoon season, roughly summer into autumn, can bring sudden storms and big swells, and the humid heat of a Taiwanese summer makes midday climbing unpleasant. Caution, timing, and respect for the ocean are essential at Long Dong.

## Season and Climate

The best climbing season at Long Dong runs through the cooler, drier months, roughly October to April, when temperatures are comfortable and humidity drops. Winter is mild but can be wet and windy on the exposed coast, while spring offers some of the finest conditions. Summer is hot, humid, and typhoon-prone, best avoided for serious climbing. Because the cliffs face the sea and catch sun and wind differently across the sectors, climbers can often find a workable wall, but checking the forecast for rain and swell is always wise before the drive from the city.

## Getting There from Taipei

Long Dong's appeal is sharpened by its accessibility. From Taipei, the coast is reachable in around an hour by car along the scenic northeast coastal highway, and buses also serve the area, though a car gives the most freedom to move between sectors and chase conditions. Taipei itself has a thriving indoor climbing scene, with numerous gyms where visitors can train and meet local climbers who know the cliff well. There is no shop at the crag, so bring all gear from the city, including plenty of slings and a helmet for the loose-prone walls.
