Overhanging rock is the arena of modern hard climbing: the steeper the wall, the more spectacular the movement and the greater the demand on power, body tension, and technique. From gently leaning walls to fully horizontal roofs, climbing past vertical turns gravity into a constant adversary that tries to peel you off the rock. Yet good steep climbing is not about brute strength alone — it is about keeping weight on the feet, using body tension to stay close to the wall, and moving efficiently before the pump sets in. This guide covers the techniques that make overhangs climbable. Explore steep climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Why Steep Rock Is Different

On vertical rock, gravity pulls straight down through the body; on overhanging rock, it also pulls you backwards off the wall, loading the arms and fingers far more. The steeper the angle, the more the upper body must work to keep the climber attached. This is why overhangs feel so strenuous and why the pump — the swelling, failing forearms — is the great enemy. The key insight is that footwork and body tension, not just arm strength, keep weight off the hands even on steep ground.

## Body Tension Is Everything

Body tension is the ability to keep the whole body rigid so that pulling with the hands translates into pressure on the feet rather than the feet cutting loose. A tense core links the pulling arms to the pushing feet, keeping the hips close to the wall and the feet weighted. Without tension, the feet swing off and the entire bodyweight hangs from the arms. Developing core strength and the skill of staying tight is the foundation of steep climbing.

## Keeping Weight on the Feet

Even on a roof, the feet do crucial work. Climbers press, hook, and bicycle their feet on holds to take weight off the hands and to control the body. Heel hooks and toe hooks are especially powerful on steep ground, letting a climber pull with the leg and lock the body in position. The instinct on overhangs is to crank with the arms and forget the feet; the skilled climber does the opposite, constantly seeking footholds to relieve the upper body.

## Heel Hooks and Toe Hooks

On steep terrain, the feet become almost like extra hands. A heel hook places the heel on a hold and pulls, often allowing the climber to take a hand off or rock into a new position. A toe hook catches the top of a hold or feature with the top of the foot, pulling the body inward to counter the barn-door swing of steep climbing. Mastering these hooks transforms what feels possible on overhangs and roofs.

## Pacing and the Pump

On steep routes the clock is always running: the forearms fill with lactic acid and grip fails. Efficient steep climbing is about moving steadily, finding the brief shakes and rests, and not pausing in strenuous positions. Learning to recover on the wall — finding a heel hook or a knee bar that takes weight off the arms — buys time. Reading the route to plan rests and climbing decisively before the pump arrives is as important as raw power.

## Knee Bars and Rests

A knee bar is a powerful rest where the climber jams a knee against one feature while the foot presses against another, locking the leg and freeing both hands. On the right terrain, a good knee bar can let a climber hang almost effortlessly and recover fully. Spotting knee bars and other no-hands rests is a hallmark of an experienced steep climber, turning a desperate pump-fest into a manageable sequence of efforts.
