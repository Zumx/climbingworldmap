Ireland is a small island with a big climbing character, where the Atlantic weather shapes everything and the rock ranges from sea-battered cliffs to quiet inland crags. The granite of Dalkey sits within reach of Dublin, the glacial valley of Glendalough offers classic mountain routes, and the limestone of the Burren and the great sea cliffs of the west provide adventure on an oceanic scale. The areas below are the essentials; find them all on the [map](/map?country=ie).

## Dalkey Quarry

On the southern edge of Dublin, Dalkey Quarry is the spiritual home of Irish climbing. This disused granite quarry above the town of Dalkey holds hundreds of routes across the East Valley, West Valley and Upper Cliffs, with everything from gentle slabs to steep test pieces. Lines such as those on the Telegraph Slab and around the Pulpit Area have introduced generations to climbing. Close to the city, well-protected and quick to dry, Dalkey is the training ground and meeting place of the Dublin climbing community.

## Glendalough

In the Wicklow Mountains south of Dublin, the glacial valley of Glendalough offers classic mountain crag climbing on granite and mica-schist above its famous monastic ruins and dark lakes. Long multi-pitch routes climb the crags flanking the upper lake, with airy positions and a real mountain atmosphere. The valley's combination of history, scenery and adventurous trad climbing has made it one of Ireland's most beloved areas, drawing climbers seeking a step beyond the roadside crags.

## The Burren

In County Clare, the Burren is a unique limestone landscape of bare pavements, terraces and sea cliffs. Crags such as those around Ailladie, the great sea cliff on the Clare coast, offer superb steep limestone climbing directly above the Atlantic, with the swell crashing below. The trad routes here are committing and atmospheric, and the wider Burren holds sport and bouldering on its grey, water-sculpted rock. The combination of geology, flowers and ocean makes the Burren one of the most distinctive climbing regions in Europe.

## The Western Sea Cliffs

Ireland's Atlantic coast holds some of the most dramatic sea-cliff climbing anywhere. The sandstone and quartzite cliffs of Donegal, including the immense Slieve League and the crags around Cruit Island, offer adventurous routes in wild, remote settings. Further south, Connemara and the Mayo coast add yet more. These are serious undertakings, where tides, swell, loose rock and the long approach demand experience, but the reward is climbing of real grandeur far from the crowds.

## The Mournes and the North

In the north, the Mourne Mountains of County Down offer the finest granite climbing in Ireland. The Mourne granite is rough, sound and superb, with crags such as those on Slieve Bearnagh and the slabs and tors scattered across the range giving trad and bouldering in a beautiful mountain setting. The Mournes climb well in good weather and hold a strong tradition, complementing the sea cliffs and quarries to make the north a rewarding climbing destination in its own right.

## Weather and the Indoor Scene

Ireland's defining challenge is its weather: rain is frequent, and climbers learn to seize dry spells and chase the driest crags. The best conditions often come in spring and early autumn, though any settled high-pressure window is precious. To keep climbing through the wet, a strong indoor scene has grown, with walls such as Gravity Climbing Centre and Awesome Walls in Dublin and Cork anchoring a thriving community. The gyms train climbers who head out to Dalkey, Glendalough and the western cliffs whenever the skies clear.
