Climbing takes place in some of the most beautiful and fragile environments on earth, and as the sport grows, so does its impact. Leave No Trace is a set of principles, adapted from wilderness ethics, that help climbers minimise their footprint on the rock, the land, and the wildlife — and, just as importantly, keep crags open. Many climbing areas have been restricted or closed because of damage, litter, or conflict with landowners, all avoidable with care. This guide explains how to climb responsibly and protect the places we love. Explore climbing destinations on the [map](/map).

## Why It Matters

The crags climbers visit are shared with wildlife, landowners, and other visitors, and many sit on sensitive habitats or private land. Erosion, litter, human waste, noise, and damage to vegetation and rock all accumulate as more climbers visit. Beyond the harm to the environment, this impact has real consequences for climbers: access is a privilege, not a right, and crags around the world have been closed because of irresponsible behaviour. Leave No Trace is both an environmental ethic and the price of continued access.

## Pack It In, Pack It Out

The simplest principle is to take everything you bring back out with you, leaving nothing behind. This includes obvious litter like food wrappers and tape, but also less obvious traces: finger tape, cigarette ends, fruit peel (which does not vanish quickly and is unnatural to the area), and any gear or rubbish. Carry a bag for your waste and, where you can, pick up litter others have left. A crag should look as though no one was there.

## Stick to Trails and Protect the Ground

The approach and the base of the crag suffer heavy foot traffic, causing erosion and damage to vegetation. Stick to established trails rather than cutting new ones or shortcutting switchbacks, which accelerates erosion. At the base, keep gear and pads on durable surfaces and avoid trampling plants. In sensitive areas, concentrating impact on existing worn ground is better than spreading it across pristine vegetation. Small choices by many climbers add up.

## Human Waste and Hygiene

Human waste is a serious and growing problem at popular crags, fouling the environment and water sources. Use toilets where they exist; where they do not, follow proper backcountry practice — bury solid waste well away from the crag, trails, and water, or pack it out where required, which is increasingly the norm in fragile or busy areas. Pack out toilet paper. Managing waste responsibly is one of the most important and most neglected aspects of climbing ethics.

## Respect Wildlife and Seasonal Closures

Crags are home to wildlife, and nesting birds in particular are sensitive to disturbance. Many areas have seasonal closures to protect nesting raptors and other species; respecting these closures is essential and non-negotiable. Keep noise down, give wildlife space, control dogs, and never disturb nests or animals. These restrictions can feel inconvenient, but they protect the species that share the cliffs and the access agreements that let climbers be there at all.

## Minimise Impact on the Rock

The rock itself bears the marks of climbing: chalk, tick marks, worn holds, and sometimes damage from cleaning or new routing. Use chalk sparingly and brush off excess and tick marks. Never chip, glue, or alter holds, which is widely condemned as vandalism. Respect local ethics on cleaning and gardening routes, and on sensitive rock types like soft sandstone, avoid climbing when wet, as it breaks easily. Leaving the rock as you found it preserves it for everyone who follows.
